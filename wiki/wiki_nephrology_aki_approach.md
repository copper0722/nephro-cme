# Acute Kidney Injury -- Clinical Approach and Specific Etiologies

## Exam Logic Summary

AKI is the highest-yield topic in Section 07. The exam tests: (1) prerenal vs intrinsic vs postrenal differentiation using urinary indices, (2) KDIGO staging, (3) specific etiology identification by urine sediment + clinical context, (4) management principles including RRT indications, (5) specific AKI syndromes (rhabdomyolysis, hepatorenal). Common distractors: treating FENa as absolute (ignoring diuretic use), confusing ATIN with ATN on urine findings, using dopamine for "renal protection."

**Textbook ref:** Brenner Ch. 30-31 (AKI), Harrison Ch. 310 (AKI), Daugirdas Ch. 25 (ARF in ICU)

---

## AKI Definition and Staging

### KDIGO Definition (current standard)

AKI = any of the following:
- SCr increase >= 0.3 mg/dL (>= 26.5 mcmol/L) within 48 hours
- SCr increase to >= 1.5x baseline within prior 7 days
- Urine output < 0.5 mL/kg/hour for 6 hours

### KDIGO Staging

| Stage | Serum Creatinine | Urine Output |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | 1.5-1.9x baseline OR increase >= 0.3 mg/dL | < 0.5 mL/kg/h for 6-12h |
| 2 | 2.0-2.9x baseline | < 0.5 mL/kg/h for >= 12h |
| 3 | >= 3x baseline OR >= 4 mg/dL (with AKI criteria) OR RRT initiation | < 0.3 mL/kg/h for >= 24h OR anuria >= 12h |

**Exam pearl:** If SCr and UO criteria lead to different stages, assign the HIGHER stage.

### RIFLE vs AKIN vs KDIGO

| System | Year | Key difference |
|---|---|---|
| RIFLE | 2004 | 5 categories (R-I-F-L-E); uses GFR decrease criteria |
| AKIN | 2007 | 3 stages; added absolute SCr >= 0.3 mg/dL criterion; 48h window |
| KDIGO | 2012 | Combines both; 7-day window for 1.5x criterion; current standard |

## Prerenal vs Intrinsic vs Postrenal

### The Master Differentiation Table (MUST MEMORIZE)

| Index | Prerenal | Intrinsic (ATN) | ATIN | HRS |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| FENa | < 1% | > 2% | Variable | < 1% (often < 0.1%) |
| FEurea | < 35% | > 35% | Variable | < 35% |
| Urine Na | < 20 mEq/L | > 40 mEq/L | Variable | < 10 mEq/L |
| Urine osmolality | > 500 mOsm/kg | ~300 mOsm/kg | Variable | > plasma |
| BUN:Cr ratio | > 20 | < 10-15 | Variable | > 20 |
| Urine sediment | Bland/hyaline casts | Muddy brown casts, RTE cells | WBC, WBC casts, eosinophils | Bland |
| Response to fluids | Rapid improvement | No improvement | No improvement | No improvement (fails albumin challenge) |

**Exam pearl:** FENa < 1% does NOT always mean prerenal. FENa can be < 1% in: (1) contrast nephropathy (early), (2) rhabdomyolysis (early), (3) hepatorenal syndrome, (4) acute GN, (5) obstruction (early). Use FEurea when on diuretics (less affected).

**Exam pearl:** BUN:Cr > 20 = disproportionate BUN elevation. Causes beyond prerenal: GI bleeding (blood digested in gut), steroids, high protein intake, tetracycline, catabolic state.

## Acute Tubular Necrosis (ATN)

Most common cause of intrinsic AKI (38-55% of all AKI; ~76% of ICU AKI).

### Types
- **Ischemic ATN:** hypotension, shock, sepsis (most common), post-surgical, post-cardiac surgery
- **Toxic ATN:** medications (aminoglycosides, amphotericin B, cisplatin, vancomycin + pip-tazo, CNIs), contrast media
- **Pigment ATN:** myoglobin (rhabdomyolysis), hemoglobin (hemolysis)

### Diagnostic Criteria (Key Numbers)

| Finding | Value |
|---|---|
| Urine sediment | **Muddy brown granular casts** + RTE cells (pathognomonic) |
| FENa | > 2% |
| Urine Na | > 40 mEq/L |
| Urine osmolality | < 350-400 mOsm/kg (isosthenuric -- lost concentrating ability) |
| BUN:Cr | < 10-15 |
| Urine-to-plasma Cr ratio | < 20 |

**Exam pearl:** Muddy brown casts + FENa >= 1% combined: sensitivity 94%, specificity 100% for ATN (Kidney360 2022). This is the highest-yield diagnostic combination.

### Medications Causing ATN (Exam Favorites)

- Vancomycin + piperacillin-tazobactam: OR 3.4 for AKI (highest risk combo on exam)
- Aminoglycosides: nonoliguric ATN; direct proximal tubule toxicity
- Amphotericin B: distal tubule injury + renal vasoconstriction
- Cisplatin: dose-dependent; 31.5% develop AKI after first cycle
- Contrast: usually reversible; peaks 48-72h; prevent with periprocedural hydration

### Drugs That Raise SCr WITHOUT True GFR Decrease

Cimetidine, trimethoprim, fenofibrate, cefoxitin, pyrimethamine -- these block tubular creatinine secretion. **Exam distractor:** Patient on TMP-SMX with rising Cr but stable cystatin C = pseudo-AKI.

### Management
- NO specific pharmacotherapy for ATN
- Hemodynamic support; avoid nephrotoxins
- Diuretics: may convert oliguric to nonoliguric but NO effect on mortality/RRT/recovery
- Low-dose dopamine: NOT recommended [KDIGO 1A] -- no benefit in 61 RCTs
- Fenoldopam: NOT recommended [KDIGO 2C] -- hypotension risk
- Dialysis: for refractory volume overload, hyperkalemia, acidosis, uremia

## Acute Tubulointerstitial Nephritis (ATIN)

### Classic vs Real Presentation

| Feature | Textbook (5-10% of cases) | Actual presentation |
|---|---|---|
| Triad | Rash + fever + eosinophilia | Usually INCOMPLETE |
| Rash | Morbilliform, trunk | Only 22-27% |
| Fever | Present | Only 15-36% |
| Eosinophilia | Present | Only 23-36% |
| Eosinophiluria | Diagnostic | Sensitivity only 19.8% -- NOT reliable |

**Exam pearl:** Do NOT rely on eosinophiluria to diagnose ATIN. Sensitivity 19.8%, PPV only 30% (CJASN 2013). This is a common exam distractor -- "eosinophiluria confirms AIN" is WRONG.

### ATIN vs ATN on Urine

| Feature | ATIN | ATN |
|---|---|---|
| WBC/leukocytes | Present (50-80%) | Absent |
| WBC casts | 3-14% (when present, very suggestive) | Absent |
| Leukocyte esterase | Positive | Negative |
| RTE cells + granular casts | Minimal | Predominant (muddy brown) |
| Proteinuria | Mild, tubular (< 1 g/day) | Mild |
| Eosinophils | May be present | Absent |

**Exam pearl:** Hyperkalemia + hyperchloremic metabolic acidosis DISPROPORTIONATE to AKI severity should raise suspicion for ATIN (tubular dysfunction disrupting acid/K+ secretion before GFR drops significantly).

### Top Drug Causes (Exam Frequency Order)
1. **Beta-lactam antibiotics** (onset: days to weeks)
2. **NSAIDs** (onset: weeks to months; rarely with hypersensitivity features)
3. **PPIs** (onset: weeks to months; omeprazole, lansoprazole)
4. **Immune checkpoint inhibitors** (onset: 1-8 months; pembrolizumab, nivolumab)
5. Sulfonamides, rifampin, fluoroquinolones
6. Allopurinol, phenytoin, carbamazepine

### Management Algorithm
1. **STOP offending drug** -- most important step
2. Wait for improvement (may defer biopsy if improving)
3. If no improvement in 3-7 days: **corticosteroids**
   - Prednisone 0.5-1 mg/kg/day, taper over 3-4 weeks
   - Optional: initial methylprednisolone pulse 250-500 mg/day x 2-4 days
4. **Early steroids (within 7 days)** associated with complete recovery in 60% vs delayed (> 7 days): higher risk of incomplete recovery (OR 6.6) and interstitial fibrosis (OR 8.68)
5. Biopsy if: diagnosis unclear, no improvement after drug withdrawal, not improving on steroids

**Exam pearl:** Interstitial fibrosis > 50% on biopsy = poor prognostic sign (HR 5.55 for decreased renal recovery). This is why EARLY steroid treatment matters.

### Special Types
- **TINU** (tubulointerstitial nephritis + uveitis): young females (median 15y, F:M 3:1), bilateral anterior uveitis in 88%; 52% develop uveitis AFTER renal disease onset
- **IgG4-related:** storiform fibrosis, IgG4+ plasma cells; look for pancreatitis, retroperitoneal fibrosis
- **DRESS syndrome:** 3-8 week latency, severe skin eruption, multi-organ; most common drugs = antimicrobials, PPIs, NSAIDs, antiseizure, allopurinol

## Rhabdomyolysis

### Diagnostic Criteria

| Test | Finding |
|---|---|
| CK | >= 500-1,000 U/L (some use > 5x ULN); diagnostic standard |
| Myoglobin | Elevated (but short half-life → may be false negative) |
| Urine dipstick | Positive for "blood" but NO RBCs on microscopy = myoglobinuria |
| Urine sediment | Brown pigmented casts, uric acid crystals |
| Serum | Hyperkalemia, hyperphosphatemia, hypocalcemia (early), hyperuricemia, elevated LDH/AST/ALT |

**Exam pearl:** Positive urine dipstick for blood + absence of RBCs on microscopy = either myoglobinuria or hemoglobinuria. Differentiate by clinical context (muscle injury vs hemolysis).

### Top Causes for Exam
1. Trauma/crush injury
2. Prolonged immobilization (found down, prolonged surgery -- especially bariatric with BMI > 50)
3. Statins (especially + CYP3A4 inhibitors: clarithromycin, erythromycin, azole antifungals, cyclosporine, fibrates)
4. Seizures (status epilepticus)
5. Cocaine, amphetamines, MDMA
6. Alcohol
7. Hyperthermia (heatstroke, NMS, malignant hyperthermia)
8. Sickle cell trait (HR 1.54 in Black soldiers, NEJM 2016)

### AKI Mechanism in Rhabdomyolysis (3 pathways)
1. **Renal vasoconstriction:** volume depletion (fluid sequesters in muscle) + RAAS activation + myoglobin scavenges NO
2. **Tubular obstruction:** myoglobin precipitates with Tamm-Horsfall protein in acidic/concentrated urine
3. **Direct tubular toxicity:** Fenton reaction (heme + H2O2 → reactive oxygen species); Fe3+/Fe4+ redox cycling → lipid peroxidation

### Management
1. **Aggressive IV NS** (0.9% NaCl): rate ~400 mL/h; target UO 1-3 mL/kg/h (up to 300 mL/h)
2. **Treat hyperkalemia** aggressively (q4h K+ checks if CK > 60,000): IV calcium, insulin/glucose, albuterol, K+ binders, dialysis
3. **Do NOT correct hypocalcemia** unless symptomatic (tetany, seizures) or hyperkalemia with ECG changes -- risk of rebound hypercalcemia in recovery phase
4. **NaHCO3 for urine alkalinization: NOT routinely recommended** (no proven benefit; may worsen hypocalcemia and volume overload)
5. Cardiac monitoring: continuous ECG if K+ >= 6.5 or ECG changes [Strong]
6. Compartment syndrome: surgical fasciotomy consult
7. RRT: if unable to correct volume overload, acidosis, or hyperkalemia

**Exam pearl:** The exam loves asking about NaHCO3 in rhabdomyolysis. Current evidence does NOT support routine urine alkalinization. The correct answer is aggressive isotonic saline, NOT bicarbonate.

## Hepatorenal Syndrome (HRS)

### ICA Diagnostic Criteria (MUST MEMORIZE)

All of the following:
1. Cirrhosis with ascites
2. AKI present (SCr >= 0.3 mg/dL in <= 48h OR >= 50% increase over 7 days)
3. **Failed 48-hour albumin challenge** (1 g/kg IV) + diuretic discontinuation
4. Absence of shock
5. No current/recent nephrotoxic drugs
6. No structural kidney injury:
   - Proteinuria < 500 mg/day
   - No microhematuria (< 50 RBC/HPF)
   - Normal renal ultrasound

**Exam pearl:** Presence of muddy brown casts or cellular casts on urinalysis EXCLUDES HRS as primary pathology. HRS = bland sediment.

### Type 1 vs Type 2

| Feature | Type 1 (HRS-AKI) | Type 2 (HRS-CKD) |
|---|---|---|
| Onset | Rapid (Cr doubles to > 2.5 in < 2 weeks) | Slow progressive |
| Precipitant | SBP, GI bleed, large paracentesis | Disease progression |
| Hallmark | Rapid decline | Refractory ascites |
| Prognosis | Very poor (weeks) | Better (months) |
| Treatment | Vasoconstrictors + albumin [Strong] | Vasoconstrictors NOT recommended [Strong] |

### Management Algorithm

**Step 1:** Stop ALL nephrotoxins (NSAIDs, ACEi/ARBs, diuretics, aminoglycosides)

**Step 2:** Albumin volume expansion challenge (1 g/kg IV x 48h)

**Step 3:** If no response (HRS-AKI > stage 1A, SCr >= 1.5 mg/dL):

| Agent | Recommendation |
|---|---|
| **Terlipressin + albumin** | First-line [Strong] -- V1 agonist, splanchnic vasoconstriction |
| Norepinephrine + albumin | Alternative to terlipressin [Conditional] |
| Midodrine + octreotide + albumin | ONLY if terlipressin AND norepinephrine unavailable [Strong] -- less effective |

**Step 4:** Liver transplant = definitive treatment regardless of drug response [Strong]

**Exam pearl:** Terlipressin is the FIRST-LINE vasoconstrictor for HRS-AKI. Midodrine + octreotide is inferior and should only be used when terlipressin is unavailable. The CONFIRM trial (NEJM 2021) showed terlipressin vs placebo: HRS reversal 32% vs 17%, but watch for respiratory failure (11% vs 2%).

## AKI Management Principles

### Medications NOT Recommended in AKI

| Agent | KDIGO Grade | Why NOT |
|---|---|---|
| Low-dose dopamine (1-3 mcg/kg/min) | 1A (Strong, against) | No benefit (61 RCTs, 3,359 patients); risk of tachyarrhythmias |
| Fenoldopam | 2C (Conditional, against) | Hypotension risk; no clinical outcome benefit |
| ANP | 2B (Conditional, against) | Low-dose may reduce RRT; high-dose causes hypotension |
| rhIGF-1 | 1B | No benefit |
| Diuretics for AKI treatment | 1B (not routine) | No effect on mortality/recovery; only for volume overload |
| NaHCO3 in AKI with lactic acidosis | -- | BICAR-ICU showed benefit only in AKI subgroup for reducing RRT; BICARICU-2 (JAMA 2025): no mortality benefit |

### RRT Indications

Start emergently when **life-threatening** changes in:
- Fluid (refractory pulmonary edema)
- Electrolyte (refractory hyperkalemia)
- Acid-base (severe metabolic acidosis)
- Uremia (encephalopathy, pericarditis, bleeding)

Consider entire clinical scenario; do NOT rely on BUN/Cr thresholds alone [KDIGO Not Graded].

### RRT Modalities

| Modality | Duration | Setting | When to use |
|---|---|---|---|
| IHD (IRRT) | 4h, 3-4x/week | Hemodynamically stable | Most AKI patients |
| SLED | 6-12h, 6-7d/week | Intermediate | Moderate instability |
| CRRT (CVVH/CVVHD/CVVHDF) | Continuous 24h | ICU, hemodynamically unstable | Shock, brain injury, fluid overload |

### RRT Anticoagulation
- IRRT: unfractionated or low-molecular-weight heparin [KDIGO 1C]
- CRRT: **regional citrate preferred** [KDIGO 2B] over systemic heparin (less bleeding)
- Buffer: **bicarbonate preferred over lactate** [KDIGO 2C]; especially in circulatory shock or liver failure [KDIGO 1B]

### Vascular Access for RRT
- First choice: right internal jugular vein
- Second: femoral vein
- Third: left internal jugular vein
- Avoid subclavian (stenosis risk for future AVF)
- Use ultrasound guidance [KDIGO 1A]

## High-Yield Exam Scenarios

1. **Post-cardiac surgery patient, SCr rises day 2, muddy brown casts on UA, FENa 3%:** ATN (ischemic). Management: supportive. Distractors: dopamine, fenoldopam.

2. **Patient on omeprazole x 3 months develops AKI, mild eosinophilia, bland UA with few WBCs:** PPI-induced ATIN. Stop PPI. If no improvement in 5-7 days, start prednisone.

3. **Alcoholic cirrhosis, SBP, rising Cr, urine Na 5 mEq/L, bland sediment, no response to 48h albumin:** HRS-AKI. Start terlipressin + albumin.

4. **Earthquake victim, tea-colored urine, CK 85,000, K+ 7.2, ECG peaked T waves:** Rhabdomyolysis with hyperkalemia. FIRST: IV calcium for cardiac stabilization. THEN: aggressive NS, insulin/glucose/albuterol for K+. Do NOT give NaHCO3 routinely.

5. **Patient on vancomycin + piperacillin-tazobactam, SCr doubles over 5 days, granular casts on UA:** Drug-induced ATN. The vancomycin + pip-tazo combination has the highest AKI risk (OR 3.4). Stop or change antibiotics.

6. **Patient started on ciprofloxacin 2 weeks ago, now has AKI + low-grade fever + rash + eosinophilia:** Classic drug-induced ATIN with full triad. Stop ciprofloxacin. Consider biopsy if no improvement. Early steroids improve outcomes.

7. **AKI in cirrhosis patient: how to differentiate HRS from ATN?** Check urine sediment (bland = HRS; muddy brown casts = ATN). Check FENa (< 0.1% suggests prerenal/HRS in cirrhosis). NGAL may help (AUC 0.87 with cutoff 220 mcg/g Cr for ATN). FEurea < 35% = HRS; > 35% = ATN.
