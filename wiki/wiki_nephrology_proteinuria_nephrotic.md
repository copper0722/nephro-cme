# Proteinuria and Nephrotic Syndrome in Adults

## Exam Logic Summary

Proteinuria/nephrotic syndrome questions test: (1) quantification methods (ACR vs PCR vs 24h), (2) KDIGO albuminuria categories A1-A3, (3) nephrotic syndrome definition and 4 complications, (4) pathology table matching histologic pattern to IF/EM findings, (5) when to biopsy vs empiric treatment, (6) nephrotic syndrome management (edema, thrombosis, hyperlipidemia, infection). Key distractor: using dipstick alone (misses light chains, underestimates dilute samples).

**Textbook ref:** Brenner Ch. 10 (Proteinuria), Rennke Ch. 3 (Proteinuria), KDIGO 2024 CKD

## Definitions and Classification

### Proteinuria Categories

| Degree | Protein/24h |
|---|---|
| Normal | < 150 mg |
| Moderate | 150-500 mg |
| Severe | > 500 mg |
| Nephrotic-range | >= 3,500 mg |

### KDIGO Albuminuria Categories in CKD

| Category | AER (mg/24h) | ACR (mg/g) | ACR (mg/mmol) | PER (mg/24h) | PCR (mg/g) | Dipstick |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| A1 (normal-mild) | < 30 | < 30 | < 3 | < 150 | < 150 | Negative to trace |
| A2 (moderate) | 30-299 | 30-299 | 3-29 | 150-500 | 150-500 | Trace to + |
| A3 (severe) | >= 300 | >= 300 | >= 30 | > 500 | > 500 | + or greater |

**Exam pearl:** "Microalbuminuria" and "macroalbuminuria" are outdated terms. Use A1/A2/A3. ACR is preferred over PCR for detection at lower levels (GFR < 60). PCR should be used instead of ACR once albuminuria exceeds 500-1,000 mg/g (AER > 1,000 mg/24h).

### Nephrotic Syndrome Definition

ALL of the following:
1. Proteinuria > 3.5 g/day (or spot UPCR > 3,000-3,500 mg/g)
2. Peripheral edema
3. Hypoalbuminemia

Commonly associated: hyperlipidemia, lipiduria, hypercoagulable state

### Nephritic vs Nephrotic Syndrome

| Feature | Nephrotic | Nephritic |
|---|---|---|
| Proteinuria | > 3.5 g/day, non-selective | < 3.5 g/day (usually), with hematuria |
| Sediment | Bland or lipiduria (oval fat bodies) | Active: RBC casts, dysmorphic RBCs |
| Edema | Prominent | Variable |
| Albumin | Low | Mildly low or normal |
| Complement | Usually normal (except membranous, lupus, MPGN) | Often low (postinfectious, lupus, MPGN) |
| BP | Variable | Often hypertensive |
| GFR | Often preserved initially | Often reduced |

## Types of Proteinuria

| Type | Mechanism | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Glomerular** | Increased permeability of GBM to large proteins (albumin) | Diabetic nephropathy, GN, MN, FSGS, MCD |
| **Tubular** | Impaired proximal tubule reabsorption of small filtered proteins | Fanconi syndrome, heavy metals, interstitial nephritis |
| **Overflow** | Increased plasma levels of small proteins exceed reabsorptive capacity | Multiple myeloma (FLC), hemoglobinuria, myoglobinuria |
| **Postural (orthostatic)** | Benign; occurs upright, resolves supine | Adolescents/young adults (< 30 years); 2%-5% prevalence |

**Exam pearl:** If glomerular disease = albumin > 40% of total protein. If tubular = albumin < 40%. If overflow (myeloma) = dipstick NEGATIVE (detects albumin only) but SSA or total protein POSITIVE.

## Measurement Methods

### First-Line: Spot Urine ACR or PCR

- First morning void preferred (correlates with 24h, low variability, excludes orthostatic proteinuria)
- ACR: more sensitive for low-level albuminuria, preferred in CKD screening
- PCR: preferred for nephrotic-range, glomerulonephritis, monoclonal gammopathies
- Confirm positive ACR 30-70 mg/g with repeat sample; ACR > 70 mg/g (> 700 mg/g) does not need confirmation
- ACR assumes creatinine excretion approximately 1 g/day; less accurate in very high/low muscle mass

### 24-Hour Collection

- Gold standard but inconvenient, collection errors common
- Use when: untimed ACR is unreliable, very high/low muscle mass, initiation of immunosuppression, monitoring disease activity requiring precise quantification

### Dipstick Limitations

- Detects albumin only (misses light chains, tubular proteins)
- Semi-quantitative, no correction for concentration
- False positive: alkaline urine, UTI, dehydration, exercise, ciprofloxacin, chloroquine
- False negative: dilute urine, non-albumin proteinuria
- Negative dipstick + ACR >= 300 mg/g = effectively rules out severely increased albuminuria

**Exam pearl:** Dipstick protein 1+ correlates roughly with ACR 30 mg/g. For ACR >= 300 mg/g detection: dipstick 1+ has sensitivity 98.9% and specificity 92.6%. Dipstick NEGATIVE has NPV 100% for ACR >= 300 mg/g. But sensitivity drops to 57.8% for detecting ACR >= 30 mg/g.

## Causes of Nephrotic Syndrome in Adults

### Primary Glomerular Diseases

| Disease | Frequency in NS | Key biopsy finding |
|---|---|---|
| MCD | Common (most common in children) | Normal LM, negative IF, foot process effacement on EM |
| FSGS | Common (most common in Black adults) | Segmental sclerosis, IgM/C3 trapping, FPE on EM |
| Membranous | Most common in White adults, elderly | GBM thickening, "spikes" on silver stain, subepithelial deposits, anti-PLA2R+ |
| MPGN | Less common | Capillary wall thickening, mesangial proliferation, subendothelial/intramembranous deposits |
| IgA nephropathy | 5%-25% of IgAN patients | Mesangial IgA deposits, variable histology |

### Secondary Causes

- Diabetic nephropathy (most common secondary cause overall)
- SLE (lupus nephritis class V)
- Amyloidosis (AL, AA)
- Plasma cell dyscrasias (LCDD, HCDD)
- Infections: HBV (membranous), HCV (MPGN/cryo), HIV (collapsing FSGS), syphilis, malaria
- Malignancy: solid tumors (membranous), lymphoma
- Drugs: NSAIDs, gold, penicillamine, captopril, pamidronate, lithium

### When Biopsy May NOT Be Needed

- Anti-PLA2R antibody positive membranous (with normal GFR)
- Monogenic glomerular disease with known mutation (Alport, Fabry, familial FSGS)
- Clear secondary cause (diabetic with retinopathy + proteinuria)

## Complications and Management

### 1. Edema Management

- Loop diuretics first-line; Na restriction 2 g/day (90 mmol/day)
- Twice-daily dosing preferred; increase to maximally effective dose
- If resistant: add thiazide (sequential nephron blockade) + consider amiloride/spironolactone
- If serum albumin < 2 g/dL and failed diuretics: IV albumin 25-50 g premixed with IV loop diuretic (transient effect)
- Torsemide or bumetanide if poor furosemide absorption (gut wall edema)
- Monitor for: AKI, hyponatremia, hypokalemia, volume depletion

### 2. Hypercoagulable State

- 40%-80% have reduced antithrombin III
- Increased factors V, VIII, vWF, fibrinogen
- Risk highest: membranous nephropathy, albumin < 2.0-2.5 g/dL, proteinuria > 10 g/day, age > 60, high Cr
- If thromboembolic event: full-dose anticoagulation x 6-12 months, then continue for duration of NS
- **Prophylactic anticoagulation:** Consider if albumin < 2.0-2.5 g/dL AND any of: proteinuria > 10 g/day, BMI > 35, genetic predisposition, NYHA III-IV, recent surgery, prolonged immobilization

### 3. Hyperlipidemia

- Elevated LDL, triglycerides, Lp(a); reduced HDL
- Statin first-line after ASCVD risk assessment
- Treatment of NS (proteinuria reduction) may improve lipid profile
- Evidence for statins in NS specifically is limited (Cochrane 2013: 5 RCTs, no significant effect on LDL or clinical outcomes)

### 4. Infection Risk

- Low IgG from urinary losses, reduced complement, impaired T-cell function
- Offer vaccines: herpes zoster, influenza, HBV, pneumococcal
- Live vaccines contraindicated on immunosuppression
- Screen: TB, HBV, HCV, HIV, syphilis before immunosuppression
- Consider TMP-SMX prophylaxis on high-dose prednisone

### 5. Hypertension and Proteinuria

- Target SBP < 120 mmHg (standardized office measurement)
- ACEi/ARB first-line (Strong Recommendation)
  - Uptitrate to maximally tolerated dose
  - Mild stable Cr increase <= 30%: do NOT stop
  - Proteinuria goal: < 1 g/day (varies by disease)
  - Combination ACEi + ARB: greater proteinuria reduction but increased AKI + hyperkalemia risk
- MRA (finerenone, spironolactone): second-line if persistent proteinuria despite max ACEi/ARB
- AVOID ACEi/ARB with abrupt-onset NS (increased AKI risk)

| Pattern | LM | IF | EM |
|---|---|---|---|
| MCD | Normal or minimal mesangial proliferation | Usually negative | Extensive foot process effacement |

## High-Yield Exam Scenarios

1. **Patient with edema, albumin 1.8, proteinuria 8 g/day, cholesterol 380. Biopsy: normal LM, negative IF, FPE on EM.** MCD. In adults: check for secondary causes (NSAIDs, lymphoma). Treat with steroids.

2. **65-year-old with NS, anti-PLA2R antibody positive, normal GFR.** Primary membranous nephropathy. Biopsy may not be needed (high specificity of anti-PLA2R). Monitor proteinuria; consider immunosuppression if persistent nephrotic despite 6 months of supportive care.

3. **Dipstick shows trace protein. ACR 250 mg/g. What category?** A2 (moderately increased albuminuria). Confirm with repeat early morning sample.

4. **Nephrotic patient develops acute leg swelling + dyspnea.** DVT + PE. NS is a hypercoagulable state. Renal vein thrombosis especially with membranous nephropathy. Full anticoagulation x duration of NS.

5. **Young athlete with proteinuria 1.5 g/day. First morning urine: normal. Daytime: elevated.** Orthostatic (postural) proteinuria. Benign. No treatment needed. Uncommon > 30 years old.

6. **Myeloma suspected: dipstick negative but SSA strongly positive.** Overflow proteinuria (free light chains). Dipstick detects albumin only. Order SPEP/UPEP, serum free light chains.
