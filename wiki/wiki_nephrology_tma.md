# Thrombotic Microangiopathies (TMA) — Focus on aHUS

## Exam Logic Summary

TMA questions test: (1) differential diagnosis of TMA triad (MAHA + thrombocytopenia + organ damage), (2) distinguishing TTP (ADAMTS13 < 10%) from aHUS (complement-mediated) from STEC-HUS (Shiga toxin), (3) complement pathway mutations and their prognosis, (4) eculizumab mechanism and indications, (5) identifying secondary TMA causes. The critical exam concept: ADAMTS13 activity is the FIRST test — if > 10%, TTP is excluded and aHUS becomes leading diagnosis.

**Textbook ref:** Brenner Ch. 29 (Thrombotic Microangiopathies), Harrison Ch. 111 (TTP/HUS)

---

## Definitions

- **TMA:** Clinical syndrome of non-immune hemolytic anemia with fragmented cells (schistocytes), thrombocytopenia, and organ damage from endothelial injury + small vessel thrombotic occlusion
- **HUS:** TMA subtype characterized by predominant renal failure
  - Primary HUS: no underlying disorder
  - Secondary HUS: manifestation of underlying condition
- **Atypical HUS (aHUS):** HUS occurring without coexisting infection or underlying disease; typically caused by uncontrolled complement activation
- **Typical (STEC-HUS):** Triggered by Shiga toxin-producing E. coli (usually O157:H7)

## Classification of TMA

### Primary TMA

| Type | Mechanism | Key differentiator |
|---|---|---|
| **TTP** | ADAMTS13 deficiency (autoantibody or genetic) | ADAMTS13 activity < 10% |
| **aHUS** | Complement alternative pathway dysregulation | ADAMTS13 > 10%; complement mutations/antibodies |
| **STEC-HUS** | Shiga toxin endothelial damage | Diarrhea prodrome; stool studies positive for STEC |
| **S. pneumoniae HUS** | T-antigen exposure | Positive direct Coombs; CSF/blood culture positive |
| **Cobalamin C (CblC) HUS** | Methylmalonic aciduria/homocystinuria | Elevated homocysteine + methylmalonic acid |
| **DGKE-HUS** | Complement-independent; protein kinase C activation | Age <= 1 year; persistent HTN/hematuria/proteinuria |

### Secondary TMA

- Drug-mediated: quinine, anti-VEGF, CNI (cyclosporine, tacrolimus), gemcitabine, mitomycin, clopidogrel, ticlopidine
- Infections: HIV, H1N1, CMV, HHV6
- Pregnancy: preeclampsia/eclampsia, HELLP
- Autoimmune: SLE, scleroderma, antiphospholipid syndrome
- Malignant hypertension
- Cancer: prostate, gastric, breast, lung, lymphoma
- Transplant: stem cell or solid organ

## Atypical HUS — Complement Pathway

### Prevalence of Mutations (Pooled Analysis, 795 patients)

| Gene | Single mutation | Combined | Function |
|---|---|---|---|
| *CFH* | 19.9% | 1.9% | Regulates C3 convertase; 150 kDa plasma protein |
| *MCP* (CD46) | 8.2% | 2.4% | Cofactor for CFI-mediated C3b/C4b cleavage |
| *CFI* | 5.8% | 2.1% | 88 kDa glycoprotein; degrades C3b and C4b |
| *C3* | 5.7% | 0.5% | Binds cell surfaces indiscriminately (gain of function) |
| *CFB* | 1.1% | 0.1% | Forms C3 convertase with C3b (gain of function) |
| Anti-CFH antibodies | 6.5% | 4.2% combined | Autoantibodies blocking CFH function |
| No identified mutation | approximately 30% | | |

**Exam pearl:** CFH mutation = most common (approximately 20% single, 28% in another cohort). The 2-hit model: genetic predisposition (complement mutation) + environmental trigger (infection, pregnancy, drugs). Incomplete penetrance — not all carriers develop disease.

### Key Complement Factors

| Factor | Function |
|---|---|
| CFH | Key regulator; competes with CFB for C3b binding; cofactor for CFI |
| MCP (CD46) | Membrane-bound; cofactor for CFI on cell surface |
| CFI | Serine protease; cleaves C3b/C4b with cofactors CFH/MCP |
| C3 | Central component; gain-of-function mutations = uncontrolled amplification |
| CFB | Forms C3 convertase (C3bBb); gain-of-function = enhanced activation |

**Exam pearl:** C5 is the therapeutic target of eculizumab. C3b binds cell surfaces -> activates C5 -> membrane attack complex -> cell lysis. Eculizumab blocks C5 cleavage, preventing MAC formation while preserving upstream complement function.

## Clinical Presentation

- Classic triad: MAHA + thrombocytopenia (usually mild) + AKI (predominant)
- Full triad NOT present in all patients: 15%-20% have normal platelets, 6% of children no anemia, 20% preserved renal function
- Onset: often abrupt, but 20% have progressive/insidious course over weeks-months
- Triggers identified in approximately 50% of children, 33% of adults: viral gastroenteritis, URI, pregnancy, drugs, vaccination

### MAHA Findings

- Hemoglobin < 10 g/dL
- Elevated LDH
- Undetectable haptoglobin
- Schistocytes on peripheral smear
- Negative direct Coombs test (NOT immune-mediated)
- Reticulocytosis

### Extrarenal Manifestations

| System | Prevalence | Features |
|---|---|---|
| Neurologic | 10%-48% | Seizures, confusion, encephalopathy, stroke, hemiparesis |
| GI | Common in children | Diarrhea, nausea, vomiting, pancreatitis, hepatitis |
| Cardiovascular | approximately 10% | Myocardial infarction, cardiomyopathy, ischemic vasculopathy |
| Skin | Uncommon | Ulcerative lesions, gangrene of digits |

### Presentation by Mutation Type

| Gene | Onset | Features |
|---|---|---|
| *C3* | Childhood or adulthood (50/50) | |
| *MCP* | Childhood (milder episodes) | Better prognosis |
| *CFB* | Either childhood or adulthood | |
| *CFH* | 70% childhood, 30% adulthood | Worst prognosis |
| *DGKE* | Age <= 1 year | Persistent HTN, hematuria, proteinuria; slow CKD progression |
| *THBD* | 90% childhood | |

## Diagnosis

### Diagnostic Algorithm

1. Suspect TMA: MAHA + thrombocytopenia + organ damage (kidney primary)
2. **ADAMTS13 activity STAT** (most critical test):
   - < 10% = TTP -> start plasma exchange + caplacizumab
   - > 10% = rules out TTP -> consider aHUS
3. Rule out STEC-HUS: stool studies for Shiga toxin/LPS (especially children with diarrhea)
4. Rule out S. pneumoniae HUS: blood/CSF cultures, direct Coombs
5. Rule out cobalamin C deficiency: homocysteine + methylmalonic acid (all children < 1-2 years)
6. Rule out secondary TMA: autoimmune serologies, drug history, pregnancy test, HIV/HBV/HCV, malignancy screening
7. aHUS = diagnosis of EXCLUSION

### Complement Testing (for confirmed/likely aHUS)

- Serum C3, C4, CFH, CFI, possibly CFB
- Anti-CFH autoantibodies
- MCP surface expression on leukocytes (FACS analysis)
- Genetic testing: *C3*, *CFB*, *MCP*, *CFH*, *CFI*, *THBD*
- Functional complement assays (monitor eculizumab response)

**Exam pearl:** DO NOT delay treatment for genetic testing results. Complement levels may be normal even with pathogenic mutations. Normal C3 does NOT exclude aHUS. Start treatment empirically based on clinical diagnosis.

### Coagulation Tests — Normal in aHUS

- Fibrinogen: normal
- D-dimer: normal
- PT/aPTT: normal
- This distinguishes aHUS from DIC (where coagulation is abnormal)

## Management

### Initial Management

1. **Therapeutic plasma exchange (TPE):** Start within 4-8 hours if TMA suspected and diagnosis uncertain
   - Continue until ADAMTS13 and other workup results available
   - Collect diagnostic samples BEFORE starting TPE

2. **Eculizumab (or ravulizumab):** Start as soon as aHUS clinically diagnosed
   - Anti-C5 monoclonal antibody; blocks terminal complement pathway
   - Do NOT wait for genetic testing
   - Switch from plasma exchange to eculizumab when aHUS confirmed

3. **Meningococcal vaccination:** Mandatory for ALL patients receiving eculizumab (risk of Neisseria meningitidis infection due to MAC blockade)
   - Consider continual antibiotic prophylaxis in children at high risk

### Eculizumab Duration

- May depend on: genetic risk of recurrence, disease severity, renal function status
- Consider long-term maintenance treatment
- Discontinuation may be considered in patients with complete recovery + continued disease remission (carefully monitored)
- Switching to eculizumab may not be necessary in patients who achieved complete recovery with plasma exchange alone

### Anti-CFH Antibody aHUS

- Consider TPE or eculizumab (or both for severe extrarenal complications)
- ADD immunosuppressive agents: rituximab or cyclophosphamide pulses
- Monitor anti-CFH antibody titers

### Supportive Care

- RRT as needed for AKI
- IV fluids for dehydration
- RBC/platelet transfusions (platelets controversial in TMA but may be necessary)
- Antihypertensive agents

### Kidney Transplantation

- Option for ESKD patients
- Prophylactic eculizumab for moderate-high recurrence risk
- Combined kidney/liver transplant: individualized for patients with mutations in liver-produced complement proteins
- Risk of recurrence depends on mutation: *CFH* mutations = high recurrence risk; *MCP* mutations = low (MCP expressed on kidney cells, new graft has normal MCP)

## TTP vs HUS vs aHUS Differentiation (CRITICAL EXAM TABLE)

| Feature | TTP | STEC-HUS | aHUS |
|---|---|---|---|
| ADAMTS13 | < 10% | Normal (> 10%) | Normal (> 10%) |
| Predominant organ | Brain (neuro > renal) | Kidney | Kidney |
| Thrombocytopenia | Severe (< 30K common) | Moderate | Mild-moderate (15%-20% normal) |
| Typical age | Adults (30-50) | Children (< 5 years) | Any age (50%-60% adults) |
| Trigger | None, or pregnancy/HIV | Bloody diarrhea (STEC O157:H7) | Infection, pregnancy, drugs, surgery |
| Renal failure | Mild-moderate | Severe (often oliguric) | Severe |
| Treatment | TPE + caplacizumab + steroids + rituximab | Supportive (NO antibiotics, NO TPE) | Eculizumab/ravulizumab |
| Complement testing | Normal | Normal | Often abnormal (but can be normal) |
| Recurrence | Common without maintenance | Rare | Common (mutation-dependent) |

**Exam pearl:** The single most important lab test in TMA is ADAMTS13 activity. If < 10% = TTP (start TPE urgently, do NOT give platelets without TPE). If > 10% = NOT TTP. In a child with bloody diarrhea + AKI + MAHA = STEC-HUS (supportive care only; antibiotics may WORSEN by releasing more toxin). In an adult without diarrhea, ADAMTS13 > 10% = aHUS until proven otherwise.

## High-Yield Exam Scenarios

1. **Woman postpartum develops MAHA, thrombocytopenia, AKI, seizures. ADAMTS13 = 45%.** This is NOT TTP (> 10%). Differential: aHUS (complement-mediated, triggered by pregnancy), HELLP (but usually presents pre-delivery), or secondary TMA. Start plasma exchange empirically, check complement workup, consider eculizumab.

2. **5-year-old with bloody diarrhea followed by oliguria, schistocytes, plt 80K.** STEC-HUS. Stool studies for E. coli O157:H7. Supportive care only. Do NOT give antibiotics (may increase toxin release). Do NOT give anti-diarrheal agents.

3. **Adult patient on cyclosporine post-transplant develops MAHA + AKI.** Drug-mediated TMA (secondary). CNI toxicity. Discontinue or reduce cyclosporine. ADAMTS13 will be normal.

4. **Child age 8 months with recurrent TMA episodes, persistent HTN, hematuria.** DGKE mutation (complement-independent). All reported cases <= 13 months old. Eculizumab may not be effective (complement-independent pathway).

5. **Patient with aHUS starting eculizumab. What vaccination is mandatory?** Meningococcal vaccine (MenACWY + MenB). Eculizumab blocks C5 -> MAC -> increased susceptibility to encapsulated organisms, especially Neisseria meningitidis.
