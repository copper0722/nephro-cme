# Renal Tubular Acidosis (RTA) and Fanconi Syndrome

## Exam Logic Summary

RTA is extremely high-yield. The exam tests: (1) differentiating types I/II/IV by urine pH, K+, and urine anion gap, (2) recognizing Fanconi syndrome as generalized proximal tubular dysfunction (NOT just type II RTA), (3) drug causes of each type, (4) complications (nephrocalcinosis in type I, osteomalacia), (5) treatment differences (alkali dose much higher in type II than type I). Key distractor: confusing type I (HIGH urine pH, CANNOT acidify) with type II (LOW urine pH once below threshold); type IV is the ONLY type with hyperkalemia.

**Textbook ref:** Brenner Ch. 18 (RTA), Harrison Ch. 315 (Tubulointerstitial Diseases), KDIGO 2024 CKD

---

## Core Definition

RTA = hyperchloremic, normal anion gap metabolic acidosis caused by renal tubular dysfunction resulting in H+ retention or HCO3- wasting, with normal or mildly reduced GFR.

## RTA Classification — Master Comparison Table

| Feature | Type I (Distal) | Type II (Proximal) | Type III (Combined) | Type IV (Hyperkalemic) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Defect** | Cannot secrete H+ in collecting duct | Cannot reabsorb HCO3- in PCT | Both defects | Aldosterone deficiency/resistance |
| **Urine pH** | > 5.5 (ALWAYS) | < 5.5 (after HCO3- threshold exceeded) | > 5.5 | < 5.5 |
| **Serum K+** | LOW (hypokalemia) | LOW (hypokalemia) | Low/normal | HIGH (hyperkalemia) |
| **Urine AG** | Positive (UAG > 0) | Negative (UAG < 0) | Positive | Positive |
| **Urine osmolal gap** | < 150 mOsm/kg | > 150 mOsm/kg | Low | Low |
| **24h NH4+ excretion** | < 40 mEq/day | Adequate | Low | < 40 mEq/day |
| **FE HCO3-** | < 5% (normal) | > 15% (diagnostic) | > 5% + acidification defect | > 5-10% with normal bicarbonatemia |
| **Serum HCO3-** | Can be very low (< 10) | Usually 14-20 (self-limited) | Variable | Usually 17-22 (mild) |
| **Nephrocalcinosis** | YES (classic) | Rare | Yes | No |
| **Nephrolithiasis** | YES (calcium phosphate) | Rare | Yes | No |
| **Bone disease** | Yes (osteomalacia) | Yes (osteomalacia, rickets) | Yes | No |
| **Alkali dose** | LOW: 1-2 mEq/kg/day | HIGH: 10-20 mEq/kg/day | Variable | Address underlying cause |

**Exam pearl:** The KEY differentiators: (1) K+ level: type IV = hyperkalemia; types I/II = hypokalemia. (2) Urine pH: type I = always > 5.5; types II/IV = < 5.5. (3) Urine AG = positive in type I and IV (impaired NH4+ excretion); negative in type II and GI losses.

## Urine Anion Gap and Osmolal Gap

### Urine Anion Gap (UAG)

UAG = (Na+ + K+) - Cl-

- **Positive UAG:** Impaired renal NH4+ excretion (types I and IV RTA)
- **Negative UAG:** Adequate renal NH4+ excretion (type II RTA, or GI HCO3- loss = diarrhea)

**Exam pearl:** UAG is an indirect estimate of urine NH4+. In NAGMA, if UAG is NEGATIVE = kidneys ARE secreting H+/NH4+ appropriately -> problem is LOSS of HCO3- (diarrhea or type II RTA early). If UAG is POSITIVE = kidneys CANNOT secrete acid properly -> type I or type IV RTA.

### Urine Osmolal Gap (UOG)

UOG = measured urine Osm - calculated urine Osm

Calculated = 2(Na+ + K+) + urea/2.8 + glucose/18

- UOG > 400 mOsm/kg suggests adequate NH4+ (excludes distal RTA)
- UOG < 150 mOsm/kg suggests impaired NH4+ excretion
- UOG is considered more reliable than UAG

## Type I (Distal) RTA — Detail

### Pathogenesis

- Failure to secrete H+ in alpha-intercalated cells of collecting duct
- Cannot lower urine pH below 5.5 even with acid load
- Results in: metabolic acidosis, hypokalemia (increased K+ secretion via ROMK to maintain electrochemical gradient), hypercalciuria, hypocitraturia

### Causes

**Hereditary (children):**
- AR mutations in H+-ATPase subunits (may have sensorineural deafness)
- AD/AR mutations in anion exchanger 1 (AE1/SLC4A1)

**Acquired (adults — more common):**
- Autoimmune: Sjogren (5%-25% prevalence), SLE, RA, PBC, autoimmune hepatitis, autoimmune thyroiditis
- Medications: amphotericin B, lithium, ifosfamide, toluene (glue sniffing), foscarnet, topiramate
- Hypercalciuria/nephrocalcinosis
- Kidney diseases: medullary sponge kidney, obstructive uropathy, chronic pyelonephritis, transplant rejection
- Sickle cell disease

**Voltage-dependent (hyperkalemic) distal RTA:**
- Reduced distal sodium delivery or impaired Na+ reabsorption
- Causes: lupus nephritis, sickle cell, obstructive uropathy, amyloidosis, drugs (amiloride, lithium, trimethoprim)
- These patients have urine pH < 5.5 and HYPERKALEMIA (looks like type IV but aldosterone levels normal)

### Complications

- Nephrocalcinosis (42% in hereditary)
- Nephrolithiasis (50%) — calcium phosphate stones
- Bone disease: osteomalacia (10%), osteoporosis (23%)
- Severe hypokalemia: muscle weakness, periodic paralysis, cardiac arrhythmia

### Treatment

- Alkali replacement: NaHCO3 or Na/K citrate 1-2 mEq/kg/day
- Correct K+ BEFORE giving alkali (alkali worsens hypokalemia)
- Potassium citrate preferred (replaces K+ and provides alkali + citrate inhibits stone formation)
- Diet counseling for stone prevention

## Type II (Proximal) RTA — Detail

### Pathogenesis

- Reduced HCO3- reabsorption threshold in PCT (normally 24-26 mmol/L)
- At normal HCO3-, bicarbonate spills into urine (FEHCO3- > 15%)
- Once serum HCO3- falls below reduced threshold (typically 14-20), kidneys CAN retain remaining HCO3-
- Then: urine pH drops < 5.5, UAG becomes negative (kidneys can acidify once below threshold)
- This is why type II is "self-limited" — acidosis stabilizes at lower setpoint

### Isolated vs Fanconi Syndrome

- **Isolated proximal RTA:** Only HCO3- reabsorption affected; very rare as isolated defect
- **Fanconi syndrome:** Generalized PCT dysfunction with MULTIPLE solute losses (see below)

### Causes in Adults

Most common: **monoclonal gammopathies** (myeloma, light chain disease, amyloidosis), drugs
- Carbonic anhydrase inhibitors (acetazolamide, topiramate, zonisamide)
- Chemotherapy (cisplatin, ifosfamide, streptozocin)
- Antiretrovirals (tenofovir disoproxil fumarate, adefovir)
- Heavy metals (lead, cadmium, mercury)
- Expired tetracycline

### Treatment

- Alkali replacement: 10-20 mEq/kg/day (MUCH higher than type I because HCO3- is continuously lost)
- Potassium citrate (to prevent hypokalemia from bicarbonaturia)
- Thiazide diuretics: volume contraction enhances proximal HCO3- reabsorption (helps reduce alkali dose)
- Additional electrolyte supplementation for Fanconi syndrome
- Goal: adequate control of acidosis (full normalization of HCO3- usually not achievable)

**Exam pearl:** Type II requires MUCH more alkali than type I (10-20 vs 1-2 mEq/kg/day) because giving HCO3- raises serum level above threshold -> immediate urinary loss -> need continuous high-dose replacement. This is a classic exam question.

## Type III (Combined/Mixed) RTA

- Rare; features of both proximal and distal RTA
- Caused by carbonic anhydrase II (CA2) deficiency
- AR inheritance; associated with osteopetrosis, cerebral calcification, intellectual disability
- Acquired: carbonic anhydrase inhibitors (topiramate, zonisamide, acetazolamide, zoledronic acid)

---

## Type IV (Hyperkalemic) RTA — Detail

### Pathogenesis

- Aldosterone deficiency or resistance -> impaired Na+ reabsorption + K+/H+ secretion in collecting duct
- Hyperkalemia -> impaired ammoniagenesis -> further impaired acid excretion
- Mild metabolic acidosis (HCO3- usually 17-22) with HYPERKALEMIA disproportionate to degree of acidosis

### Causes

**Most common in adults:** Hyporeninemic hypoaldosteronism (diabetic nephropathy) with mild renal impairment

**Medications (very commonly tested):**
- ACEi, ARBs
- K+-sparing diuretics (spironolactone, eplerenone, amiloride, triamterene)
- Calcineurin inhibitors (cyclosporine, tacrolimus)
- Heparin/LMWH (inhibit aldosterone synthesis)
- NSAIDs (reduce renin)
- Trimethoprim (blocks ENaC like amiloride)
- Pentamidine (blocks ENaC)
- Beta-blockers (reduce renin)

**Mineralocorticoid deficiency:**
- Addison disease
- Congenital adrenal hyperplasia (21-hydroxylase)
- Aldosterone synthase deficiency

**Mineralocorticoid resistance:**
- Obstructive uropathy
- Sickle cell nephropathy
- Lupus nephritis
- Amyloidosis
- Kidney transplant rejection

### Treatment

- Treat underlying cause; discontinue offending medication
- Manage hyperkalemia: dietary K+ restriction, loop diuretics, K+ binders (patiromer, SZC)
- Fludrocortisone (0.1-0.2 mg/day) for true mineralocorticoid deficiency
- Alkali replacement (NaHCO3 2-3 mEq/kg/day) if acidosis persists
- Treat metabolic acidosis (may help lower K+ indirectly)

**Exam pearl:** Type IV RTA is the MOST COMMON type overall (because of the prevalence of diabetes + ACEi/ARB use). Scenario: diabetic patient on ACEi with mild Cr elevation (2.0), K+ 5.8, HCO3- 18, AG normal = classic type IV RTA.

## Fanconi Syndrome — Comprehensive

### Definition

Generalized proximal convoluted tubule (PCT) dysfunction with excessive excretion of: amino acids, glucose, phosphate, bicarbonate, potassium, uric acid, low-molecular-weight proteins, and other solutes.

### Clinical Pentad of Fanconi Syndrome

1. **Normoglycemic glycosuria** (glucose in urine with normal blood glucose)
2. **Hyperaminoaciduria** (generalized, not specific amino acid)
3. **Low-molecular-weight proteinuria** (beta-2 microglobulin, retinol-binding protein)
4. **Proximal RTA/bicarbonaturia** (type II RTA component)
5. **Phosphaturia** (hyperphosphaturia leading to hypophosphatemia + rickets/osteomalacia)

Also: hypouricemia, hypokalemia, hypocalcemia, hyponatremia, hypomagnesemia

### Causes — Inherited (Children)

| Disease | Inheritance | Key features |
|---|---|---|
| Cystinosis | AR | Most common inherited cause; presents 6-12 months; cystine crystal accumulation; ESKD by 2nd-3rd decade |
| Lowe syndrome (oculocerebrorenal) | X-linked | Cataracts + intellectual disability + renal tubular dysfunction |
| Dent disease | X-linked | LMW proteinuria, hypercalciuria, nephrocalcinosis, partial Fanconi |
| Wilson disease | AR | Hepatolenticular degeneration + renal Fanconi |
| Galactosemia | AR | Failure to thrive + hepatomegaly + cataracts |
| Hereditary fructose intolerance | AR | Vomiting + hypoglycemia after fructose/sucrose |
| Tyrosinemia type I | AR | Liver disease + renal Fanconi |

### Causes — Acquired (Adults)

| Category | Agents |
|---|---|
| Drugs (most common in adults) | Tenofovir disoproxil fumarate (TDF), cisplatin, ifosfamide, valproate, aminoglycosides, expired tetracycline |
| Monoclonal gammopathies | Light chain disease (kappa predominant), MGRS, myeloma |
| Heavy metals | Lead, cadmium, mercury, uranium, copper |
| Autoimmune | Sjogren (3%-4%), TINU syndrome |
| Other | Amyloidosis, paroxysmal nocturnal hemoglobinuria |

**Exam pearl:** In adults, the two most common causes of acquired Fanconi are (1) drug toxicity (especially TDF in HIV patients) and (2) light-chain monoclonal gammopathy. TDF causes mitochondrial toxicity in PCT cells. TAF (tenofovir alafenamide) has largely replaced TDF because it is much less nephrotoxic.

### Evaluation

- Blood: hypophosphatemia (with normocalcemia), hypouricemia, hypokalemia, metabolic acidosis (HCO3- < 22)
- Urine: aminoaciduria, glycosuria, phosphaturia, LMW proteinuria, hyperuricosuria, hypercalciuria
- Additional: urine anion gap, fractional excretion of HCO3- (> 15% = proximal RTA), urine acidification test
- Identify underlying cause: kidney biopsy, genetic testing, drug history, serum/urine FLC

### Treatment

- Correct electrolyte losses: phosphate replacement, alkali (K+ citrate), vitamin D, magnesium, calcium
- Phosphate replacement: neutral phosphate solution preferred; monitor for nephrocalcinosis
- Avoid calcium with phosphate (reduces absorption)
- NaHCO3 or Na/K citrate for acidosis
- Treat underlying cause: remove offending drug, cysteamine for cystinosis
- Monitor: phosphate, calcitriol, HCO3-, K+, and renal function

## RTA Diagnostic Algorithm

1. Confirm NAGMA: AG normal (8-12), HCO3- < 22, pH < 7.35
2. Exclude GI HCO3- loss: diarrhea, fistula, ureteral diversion
3. Calculate urine AG (or urine osmolal gap):
   - Positive UAG (or UOG < 150) -> renal H+ excretion impaired -> types I or IV
   - Negative UAG (or UOG > 400) -> renal H+ excretion intact -> type II or GI loss
4. Check serum K+:
   - Hyperkalemia -> type IV (check aldosterone, renin, medications)
   - Hypokalemia -> type I or II
5. Check urine pH:
   - Always > 5.5 -> type I (distal)
   - < 5.5 -> type II (proximal, below threshold) or GI loss
6. Confirm with: FEHCO3- (> 15% = type II), NH4Cl load (type I cannot lower pH < 5.5)

## High-Yield Exam Scenarios

1. **Sjogren patient with NAGMA, K+ 2.8, urine pH 6.5, nephrocalcinosis:** Type I (distal) RTA. Sjogren = most common autoimmune cause. Cannot acidify urine below 5.5. Treat with potassium citrate 1-2 mEq/kg/day.

2. **HIV patient on TDF develops glycosuria with normal blood glucose, hypophosphatemia, aminoaciduria, metabolic acidosis:** Fanconi syndrome from TDF toxicity. Switch to TAF. Replace phosphate, alkali, electrolytes.

3. **Diabetic on lisinopril, Cr 1.8, K+ 6.1, HCO3- 19, AG 10:** Type IV RTA. Hyporeninemic hypoaldosteronism + ACEi. Management: hold ACEi, dietary K+ restriction, consider fludrocortisone if aldosterone truly deficient.

4. **Child with failure to thrive, rickets, LMW proteinuria, glycosuria, aminoaciduria, photophobia:** Cystinosis (most common inherited cause of Fanconi in children). Confirm with leukocyte cystine level. Treat with cysteamine.

5. **Patient on topiramate develops metabolic acidosis, normal K+, AG normal, HCO3- 16:** Type II RTA from carbonic anhydrase inhibition. May have mixed type II + type I features (combined/type III pattern). Discontinue topiramate if possible.

6. **Myeloma patient with proximal RTA + Fanconi features:** Light chain proximal tubulopathy. Kappa light chains form crystals in PCT cells (rhomboid crystals on biopsy). Clone-directed therapy (bortezomib-based).

7. **Differentiating type I from type IV in a patient with NAGMA + positive UAG:** Check K+. Type I = hypokalemia. Type IV = hyperkalemia. This is the single most reliable differentiator on exams.
