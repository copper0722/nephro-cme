# AKI Special Populations and Proteinuria — Board Review

## AKI in the Outpatient Setting

### Key Differences from Hospital AKI

| Feature | Outpatient AKI | Hospital AKI |
|---|---|---|
| Most common cause | **Prerenal** (dehydration, medications) | ATN (sepsis, surgery) |
| Drug-related | NSAIDs, ACEi/ARB, diuretics ("triple whammy") | Aminoglycosides, contrast, vancomycin |
| Obstruction | More common (BPH, stones, pelvic malignancy) | Less common |
| Recognition | Often delayed (no serial labs) | Earlier detection possible |
| Outcome | Better if recognized early | Higher mortality (sicker patients) |

### Community-Acquired AKI Etiologies

**Prerenal (most common, ~60%):**
- Dehydration (GI losses, poor intake, especially elderly)
- Medication-induced hemodynamic AKI: NSAIDs, ACEi/ARB, diuretics
- Heart failure exacerbation
- Sepsis (UTI, pneumonia)

**Intrinsic (~25%):**
- AIN: antibiotics (beta-lactams, fluoroquinolones), NSAIDs, PPIs
- ATN: prolonged prerenal, rhabdomyolysis
- Glomerulonephritis (RPGN presenting de novo)

**Postrenal (~15%):**
- BPH (most common in elderly males)
- Kidney stones (bilateral or unilateral in single kidney)
- Pelvic malignancy (cervical, bladder, prostate)

### Outpatient Workup

1. History: medications (NSAID, ACEi, recent antibiotics), volume status, urinary symptoms
2. Basic labs: BMP (sCr, BUN, K+, HCO3-), CBC, urinalysis
3. Renal US (rule out obstruction — MUST do in all unexplained AKI)
4. If glomerular disease suspected: UA with microscopy (dysmorphic RBCs, casts), UPCR, serology

### When to Hospitalize

- Hyperkalemia (> 6.0 mEq/L or ECG changes)
- Volume overload (pulmonary edema)
- Severe metabolic acidosis (pH < 7.2)
- Uremia symptoms (pericarditis, encephalopathy, bleeding)
- Rapidly rising sCr (> 0.5 mg/dL/day)
- Anuria
- Need for urgent dialysis

## AKI in Critically Ill Patients

### Epidemiology

- AKI occurs in 50-60% of ICU patients
- KDIGO stage 3 AKI in ~20% of ICU patients
- Independent risk factor for mortality (OR 2-4x)
- Sepsis is the most common cause of AKI in ICU (40-50%)

### KDIGO AKI Care Bundle (Prevention in High-Risk Patients)

1. Optimize volume status and hemodynamics
2. Avoid nephrotoxic drugs when possible
3. Avoid hyperglycemia (but avoid hypoglycemia)
4. Consider alternatives to radiocontrast
5. Monitor sCr and UOP closely
6. Avoid vancomycin-aminoglycoside combination if possible

### Sepsis-Associated AKI

- Mechanism: NOT purely ischemic. Involves:
  - Microvascular dysfunction (heterogeneous flow)
  - Inflammatory mediators (cytokines, DAMPs, PAMPs)
  - Mitochondrial dysfunction
  - Tubular cell injury (but often less necrosis than expected)
- "AKI with sepsis" = sepsis present when AKI develops
- May have PRESERVED or even INCREASED renal blood flow (vasodilation) — not always "ischemic"

### Fluid Management in AKI

- **Resuscitation:** balanced crystalloids preferred over NS (SMART trial, SALT-ED)
  - NS → hyperchloremic metabolic acidosis → possible ↓ RBF
  - Balanced solutions (LR, Plasma-Lyte): lower risk of death and kidney events
- **Albumin:** no benefit over crystalloid for AKI prevention (SAFE, ALBIOS trials)
- **Hydroxyethyl starch (HES):** CONTRAINDICATED — ↑ AKI and RRT need (CHEST, 6S trials)
- **Fluid overload:** associated with ↑ mortality in AKI; consider diuretics or RRT for fluid removal

### RRT Timing (STARRT-AKI 2020, AKIKI 2016, IDEAL-ICU 2018)

**Key trials:**
- **STARRT-AKI** (NEJM 2020, N=2,927): Early vs standard RRT initiation. No difference in 90-day mortality (43.9% vs 43.7%). Early group had more adverse events (hypotension, phosphate depletion).
- **AKIKI** (NEJM 2016, N=620): Early (within 6h of stage 3) vs delayed (wait for emergency indication). No mortality difference. 49% in delayed group never needed RRT.
- **IDEAL-ICU** (NEJM 2018, N=488): Septic shock AKI. Early (within 12h) vs delayed (48h wait). No mortality difference. 38% in delayed group recovered without RRT.

**Bottom line:** No benefit to early/prophylactic RRT initiation. Wait for standard indications unless emergency (refractory hyperkalemia, severe acidosis, pulmonary edema, uremia). ~40-50% of patients in "delayed" groups never need RRT.

### RRT Modality in ICU

| Feature | IHD | CRRT | SLED |
|---|---|---|---|
| Hemodynamic stability | Less (rapid solute/fluid shifts) | **Best** (gradual) | Intermediate |
| Indication | Hemodynamically stable | **Hemodynamically unstable** or cerebral edema | Either |
| Dose | Kt/V >= 1.2 per session, 3-6x/week | Effluent 20-25 mL/kg/h (KDIGO) | Variable |
| Evidence | Equivalent outcomes | No mortality benefit over IHD (ATN, RENAL trials) | Limited data |

**KDIGO recommended CRRT dose:** 20-25 mL/kg/h effluent rate. Higher doses (35-40 mL/kg/h) showed NO benefit (ATN trial, RENAL trial).

## Proteinuria in Adults — Approach

### Classification

| Type | Mechanism | Amount | Key features |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Transient** | Fever, exercise, stress, UTI | Usually < 1 g/day | Resolves when cause resolves |
| **Orthostatic** | Position-dependent | < 1 g/day; absent in supine | Common in young adults; benign; confirm with split collection |
| **Glomerular** | Podocyte/GBM damage | Often > 1 g/day; can be nephrotic | Albuminuria predominant |
| **Tubular** | ↓ proximal tubular reabsorption | Usually < 1-2 g/day | Low-molecular-weight proteins (beta-2-microglobulin, retinol-binding protein) |
| **Overflow** | Excess filtered protein | Variable | Light chains (myeloma), myoglobin, hemoglobin |

### Workup Algorithm

1. **Confirm:** repeat urine testing (spot UACR or UPCR on first morning void)
2. **Quantify:** UACR preferred for glomerular disease; UPCR acceptable
3. **Rule out transient causes:** repeat in 1-2 weeks if fever/exercise/UTI present
4. **Rule out orthostatic:** split collection (first morning vs afternoon) if young patient
5. If persistent:
   - Urine microscopy (dysmorphic RBCs, casts)
   - Serum albumin, lipid panel, complement levels
   - sCr and eGFR
   - Screen for secondary causes (ANA, ANCA, anti-GBM, hepatitis, HIV, SPEP/UPEP)
   - Renal biopsy if nephrotic or subnephrotic with concerning features

### Nephrotic-Range Proteinuria Thresholds

- Adults: UPCR >= 3.5 g/g (or 24h protein >= 3.5 g)
- Children: UPCR >= 2 g/g (or dipstick >= 3+)
- Nephrotic SYNDROME = nephrotic proteinuria + hypoalbuminemia (< 3 g/dL) + edema (+ hyperlipidemia)

## Nephrotic Syndrome in Adults

### Etiology by Age

| Cause | Age < 40 | Age 40-60 | Age > 60 |
|---|---|---|---|
| **MCD** | Common | Less common | Uncommon (but can occur) |
| **FSGS** | **Most common** | Common | Common |
| **Membranous** | Less common | **Most common** | **Most common** |
| **DKD** | Uncommon | Common | Very common |
| **Amyloidosis** | Rare | Increasing | Common |
| **MPGN** | Consider secondary causes | Consider secondary causes | Paraprotein-related |

**Exam pearl:** In adults > 60 with nephrotic syndrome, the top 3 are: membranous nephropathy, DKD, and amyloidosis. Always check SPEP/UPEP/free light chains in this age group.

### Primary vs Secondary Causes

**Primary glomerular:**
- MCD, FSGS (primary), membranous (PLA2R+), IgA nephropathy, MPGN (C3 glomerulopathy)

**Secondary:**
- Diabetic kidney disease (most common secondary cause overall)
- Lupus nephritis (class III-V)
- Amyloidosis (AL, AA)
- Paraprotein-related (light chain deposition, fibrillary, immunotactoid)
- Infections: hepatitis B (membranous), hepatitis C (MPGN/cryoglobulinemic), HIV (collapsing FSGS)
- Drugs: NSAIDs (MCD), gold, penicillamine (membranous), heroin (FSGS)
- Malignancy: solid tumors → membranous; lymphoma → MCD

### Complications of Nephrotic Syndrome (Adults)

| Complication | Mechanism | Management |
|---|---|---|
| **Thromboembolism** | ↓ antithrombin, ↑ clotting factors, ↓ protein C/S, hyperviscosity | Prophylactic AC if albumin < 2-2.5 g/dL + additional risk factor |
| **Infection** | ↓ IgG, ↓ complement factor B | Pneumococcal vaccine; treat infections promptly |
| **Hyperlipidemia** | ↑ hepatic lipoprotein synthesis | Statin if persistent |
| **AKI** | Prerenal (hypovolemia), bilateral RVT, ATN | Volume assessment; imaging if RVT suspected |
| **Hypoalbuminemia** | Urinary losses > hepatic synthesis | Treat underlying disease; albumin infusion only for acute hypovolemia |

### Renal Vein Thrombosis — Membranous Association

- Highest risk with membranous nephropathy (up to 25-30%)
- May be asymptomatic or present with flank pain, hematuria, acute sCr rise
- Diagnosis: CT venography or MR venography
- Treatment: anticoagulation (initially heparin, then warfarin or DOAC)

## High-Yield Exam Scenarios

1. **85-year-old on lisinopril + HCTZ + ibuprofen, found with sCr 4.2 (baseline 1.3), BUN 65:** Community-acquired AKI from "triple whammy." Stop NSAID. Hold ACEi and diuretic. Gentle IV fluids. Recheck in 48-72h.

2. **ICU patient, septic shock, KDIGO stage 3 AKI, K+ 5.2, pH 7.28, not anuric:** Based on STARRT-AKI/AKIKI evidence, no need for immediate RRT. Manage conservatively. Wait for emergency indication. ~50% chance of recovery without RRT.

3. **25-year-old with 1.5 g/day proteinuria, absent on first morning void, present on afternoon collection:** Orthostatic proteinuria. Benign. No biopsy needed. Reassure and monitor annually.

4. **65-year-old with nephrotic syndrome, UPCR 8, albumin 1.8:** Must rule out: membranous (PLA2R antibody), DKD (HbA1c, retinopathy), amyloidosis (SPEP/UPEP/free light chains), FSGS. Biopsy unless obvious DKD with retinopathy.

5. **Choosing RRT modality in hemodynamically unstable ICU patient with AKI:** CRRT preferred (gradual fluid/solute removal). Dose: 20-25 mL/kg/h effluent. No benefit from higher doses (ATN/RENAL trials).
