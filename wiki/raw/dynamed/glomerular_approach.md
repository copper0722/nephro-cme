# Glomerular Disease in Adults - Approach to the Patient (DynaMed)

Updated 10 Jul 2025. 48 pages.

## Overview
Glomerular diseases target filtration component of nephron; characterized by hematuria, proteinuria, decreased GFR. Kidney biopsy often needed. Broad initial testing required (no single test rules in/out). Classification: primary vs secondary; inflammatory (GN) vs noninflammatory; nephrotic vs nephritic.

## Key Definitions
- Proteinuria: normal <150 mg/24h; moderate 150-500; severe >500; nephrotic >=3500 mg/24h (or UPCR >3000 mg/g)
- Nephrotic syndrome: proteinuria >3.5 g/day + hypoalbuminemia (<2.5 g/dL) + edema + hyperlipidemia
- Microhematuria: >=3 intact RBCs/HPF on non-contaminated UA without benign cause
- Glomerular hematuria: dysmorphic RBCs >=40% of 100 RBCs, OR acanthocytes >=5%, OR >=1 RBC cast
- Nephritic syndrome: RBCs >5/HPF + acanthocytes + RBC/WBC casts

## Classification
Primary: MCD, MN, IgAN, C3 glomerulopathy, primary FSGS, anti-GBM, ANCA-limited. Secondary: diabetic, lupus, autoimmune, ANCA vasculitis, amyloidosis, infection-related, cryoglobulinemia, malignant HTN, TMA. Patterns of injury on biopsy: podocytopathy (MCD/FSGS), membranous, diffuse proliferative, MPGN, crescentic/necrotizing, mesangial proliferative, BM abnormalities, focal global sclerosis.

## Clinical Presentations
Nephrotic: MCD, FSGS, MN, diabetic, amyloidosis (bland sediment, massive proteinuria). Nephritic: post-infectious GN, IgAN, lupus class III/IV, ANCA (active sediment, HTN, reduced GFR). RPGN: anti-GBM, ANCA, IgAN, ANCA-negative vasculitis. Asymptomatic: IgAN, thin BM disease, Alport.

## Proteinuria Types
Glomerular selective (albumin, MCD) vs nonselective (albumin+IgG, MN/FSGS). Tubular (<2 g/day, LMW proteins). Overflow (light chains, myeloma). Nephrotic proteinuria in malignant HTN possible but resolves when HTN controlled.

## Epidemiology
IgAN most common primary GN worldwide (0.06-10.5/100K). MN 1.2/100K. FSGS 0.8/100K. MCD 0.6/100K adults. Anti-GBM 0.5-1/million. C3 glomerulopathy 0.2-3/million. IgAN highest in SE Asia. FSGS higher in Black/Hispanic populations. Anti-GBM more common in White populations.

## Approach
History: medications (NSAIDs, gold, lithium, hydralazine, anti-thyroid drugs), infections (HBV, HCV, HIV, endocarditis), autoimmune diseases (SLE, RA, scleroderma, vasculitis), malignancy, family history (Alport, FSGS, IgAN), social (heroin, cocaine). Physical: BP, edema, rash, joint swelling, lung crackles, murmurs. Labs: blood (BMP, CBC, albumin, lipids, ANA, ANCA, anti-GBM, complement, SPEP, hepatitis serologies), urine (UA, UPCR/24h, sediment), imaging (renal US), biopsy.
