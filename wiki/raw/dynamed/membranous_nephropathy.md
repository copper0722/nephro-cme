# Membranous Nephropathy (DynaMed)

Updated 08 Feb 2023. 54 pages.

## Overview
GN characterized by subepithelial immune deposits containing IgG and complement; little to no cellular proliferation. Most common cause of adult nephrotic syndrome in non-diabetics (20-40%). Primary (80%, anti-PLA2R) or secondary (20%, autoimmune/infection/malignancy/drugs). Incidence 1.2/100K. M:F 2:1. Median onset early 50s.

## Antigen-Based Classification
Primary: PLA2R (70-80%), THSD7A (1-3%), NELL-1 (2.5%, may be associated with malignancy), Sema3B (young children), NEP (antenatal). Secondary antigens: EXT-1/EXT-2 (lupus), others. Classification increasingly based on specific target antigen rather than primary/secondary.

## Causes of Secondary MN
Autoimmune: SLE (lupus class V, EXT-1/EXT-2), Sjogren, RA, sarcoidosis, mixed CTD, IgG4-related disease, dermatomyositis, ANCA vasculitis. Infections: HBV, HCV, HIV, syphilis, malaria, schistosomiasis, leprosy, endocarditis. Malignancy: lung, breast, colorectal, prostate, melanoma, non-Hodgkin lymphoma, CLL, plasma cell dyscrasias. Drugs: NSAIDs, gold, penicillamine, captopril, lithium, anti-TNF, lipoic acid. Toxins: mercury, formaldehyde, hydrocarbons.

## Pathogenesis
Primary: circulating IgG (mainly IgG4) antibodies form granular deposits in subepithelial space targeting podocyte antigens (PLA2R, THSD7A, etc). Complement activation via C5b-9 MAC (not C1q in primary). Secondary: exogenous antigens or planted antigens from infection/malignancy trigger similar immune response; EXT-1/EXT-2 in lupus. GBM thickening results from immune deposit accumulation.

## Clinical Presentation
Insidious onset of nephrotic syndrome (proteinuria >3.5 g/day in ~80%). Edema (LE, periorbital, genital), weight gain, fatigue, frothy urine, breathlessness. GFR normal at presentation in 90%. Hematuria (microscopic, trace to 1+); may be much higher if renal vein thrombosis. HTN uncommon initially. Abrupt onset suggests secondary cause or MCD.

## Diagnosis
Anti-PLA2R antibodies: sensitivity 65%, specificity 97% (ELISA); positive = diagnostic of primary MN. Glomerular PLA2R antigen staining: sensitivity 79%, specificity 90%. If anti-PLA2R negative: biopsy with PLA2R tissue staining. Screen for secondary causes regardless of PLA2R status: ANA, C3/C4, hepatitis B/C, HIV, malignancy screening, drug exposure.

## Biopsy Findings
LM: normal early; later GBM thickening with "spikes" on silver stain. IF: diffuse granular IgG4 + C3 along capillary walls (primary); IgG1/IgG3 + C1q suggests secondary. EM: subepithelial electron-dense deposits. Secondary MN features: mesangial/endothelial proliferation, crescents, C1q positivity, IgG1/IgG3 dominant, endothelial tubuloreticular inclusions (lupus).

## Risk Stratification (KDIGO 2021)
Low: normal eGFR + proteinuria <3.5 g/day + albumin >3 g/dL; or proteinuria decreasing >50% on conservative Rx. Moderate: normal eGFR + proteinuria >3.5 g/day + not decreasing >50% after 6 months ACEi/ARB. High: proteinuria >8 g/day for >6 months and/or eGFR <60; or proteinuria >3.5 not decreasing after 6 months + albumin <2.5 or PLA2R >50 RU/mL. Very high: life-threatening NS; unexplained rapid GFR decline.

## Treatment
All: supportive care (ACEi/ARB, BP <130/80, Na restriction, diuretics, statin, anticoagulation if high thrombosis risk, consider SGLT2i). Low risk: observe. Moderate: observe OR rituximab OR CNI +/- steroids. High: rituximab, OR CYC+steroids (modified Ponticelli), OR CNI+rituximab. Very high: CYC+steroids. Monitor anti-PLA2R titers (serologic remission precedes clinical remission). Relapsed/refractory: repeat same therapy or switch class.
