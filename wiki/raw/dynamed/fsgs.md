# Focal Segmental Glomerulosclerosis (FSGS) (DynaMed)

Updated 27 Nov 2023. 53 pages.

## Overview
Histologic pattern of kidney injury: podocyte injury/depletion + segmental glomerular scarring. Primary (idiopathic), secondary (adaptive, drug, viral), or genetic. FSGS 7-10% of childhood and 20-30% of adult nephrotic syndrome. Incidence 0.8/100K; prevalence 38.9% of biopsy-proven primary GN (Kaiser Permanente). More common in males (1.5-2x), Black patients (APOL1).

## Types
Primary: absence of other causes + FSGS on LM + diffuse FPE on EM + nephrotic syndrome. Secondary: FSGS lesion +/- FPE due to viral infection, drugs, adaptive hyperfiltration (usually subnephrotic, normal albumin). Genetic: podocyte or GBM gene mutations. Collapsing: variant of FSGS often related to APOL1 risk variants; worst prognosis.

## Causes of Secondary FSGS
Viruses: HIV-1, parvovirus B19, CMV, EBV, HCV, SARS-CoV-2 (APOL1). Drugs: heroin, interferons, lithium, bisphosphonates, mTOR inhibitors (sirolimus), calcineurin inhibitors, anabolic steroids, anthracyclines, NSAIDs, ledipasvir/sofosbuvir. Adaptive: reduced renal mass (unilateral agenesis, reflux nephropathy, nephrectomy), obesity, sickle cell, DM, HTN.

## Genetic FSGS
Key genes: NPHS1 (nephrin, AR, Finnish congenital NS), NPHS2 (podocin, AR, childhood FSGS), APOL1 (G1/G2, African descent, adult-onset), COL4A3/A4/A5 (Alport spectrum), WT1 (Denys-Drash/Frasier), ACTN4 (alpha-actinin-4, AD adult), INF2 (AD, Charcot-Marie-Tooth), MYH9 (Epstein-Fechtner), LMX1B (nail-patella), NUP93/NUP107/NUP205/XPO5 (nuclear pore complex, childhood steroid-resistant).

## Pathogenesis
Primary: circulating podocyte-toxic permeability factors (cardiotrophin-like cytokine 1, ApoA1b, anti-CD40 Ab; suPAR role controversial). Secondary: underlying cause damages podocytes directly or via hyperfiltration. Genetic: mutations disrupt podocyte structure. All lead to FPE, detachment, synechia, sclerosis. Podocytes lack repair capacity; damage is cumulative.

## Clinical Presentation
Primary: abrupt nephrotic syndrome (54-100%), progressive edema, hypoalbuminemia. Secondary/genetic: proteinuria (often subnephrotic) without full nephrotic syndrome. Children: 92% NS at presentation, 52% hematuria. Adults: progressive LE edema, weight gain, fatigue, frothy urine.

## Definitions
Complete remission (adults): proteinuria <0.3 g/day or UPCR <300 + stable Cr + albumin >3.5 g/dL. Partial remission: proteinuria 0.3-3.5 g/day + >50% decrease from baseline. Relapse: UPCR >3500 after CR or >50% increase during PR. Steroid-resistant (adults): persistent proteinuria >3.5 g/day with <50% reduction despite prednisone 1 mg/kg/day x >=16 weeks.

## Treatment
Primary FSGS: first-line high-dose corticosteroids (prednisone 1 mg/kg up to 80 mg daily or 2 mg/kg up to 120 mg QOD) for >=4 weeks and up to 16 weeks [Strong Recommendation]. Alternative first-line: CNI (cyclosporine 3-5 mg/kg/day or tacrolimus 0.05-0.1 mg/kg/day). Steroid-resistant: CNI for >=6 months [Strong Recommendation]. Relapse: CYC (if naive), rituximab, CNI, MMF. Secondary FSGS: treat underlying cause, RAAS blockade, no immunosuppression. Post-transplant recurrence: PLEX, immunoadsorption, rituximab.
