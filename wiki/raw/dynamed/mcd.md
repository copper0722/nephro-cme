# Minimal Change Disease (DynaMed)

Updated 09 May 2025. 60 pages.

## Overview
Most common cause of nephrotic syndrome in children (70-90% in age >1). 10-25% of adult NS. Characterized by normal LM, negative IF, diffuse FPE on EM. Incidence: 0.6/100K adults; 2-15.6/100K children. M:F 2:1 in children; female predominance in adults. Etiology unknown; hypothesized immune-related (T-cell dysfunction, B-cell involvement). Circulating nephrin autoantibodies detected in subset.

## Risk Factors
Allergies/atopy (pollen, dust, fungi, bee sting, food). Malignancies (Hodgkin lymphoma especially, NHL, leukemia, myeloma, thymoma). Drugs (NSAIDs, sulfasalazine, D-penicillamine, gold, tiopronin, lithium, tyrosine kinase inhibitors). Infections (viral, parasitic). Autoimmune (SLE, DM, myasthenia gravis, celiac). Immunizations. Mercury exposure.

## Presentation
Acute onset of nephrotic syndrome (most common). Edema (face, scrotum/labia, LE, anasarca). Adults: HTN and impaired renal function more common than children. AKI may occur (hypovolemia, sepsis, thrombosis). Microhematuria in ~20% (resolves with proteinuria). Children: may present with severe infection (sepsis, pneumonia, peritonitis).

## Diagnosis
Adults: biopsy required. Children: suspect if NS age 1-12 without atypical features; confirm with steroid response. LM: normal or minimal mesangial proliferation (<=3-4 cells/area). IF: usually negative; may have trace IgM, C3, C1q. EM: diffuse FPE WITHOUT electron-dense deposits. Complement: NORMAL. If focal IgM+C3 on IF, suspect FSGS. If IgA present, consider IgAN. MCD excluded if any additional pathology present (diagnosis of exclusion).

## Variants
IgM nephropathy: dominant diffuse mesangial IgM on IF; steroid-resistant especially in children; ~30% respond to steroids. C1q nephropathy: dominant mesangial C1q on IF; older children/young adults; heterogeneous LM (MCD, FSGS, or proliferative features on LM); variable treatment response; ESRD-free survival 95.7% at 5 years, 82.8% at 10 years in Korean adults.

## Lab Findings
Albumin often <2 g/dL. Elevated alpha-2 globulin, decreased gamma-globulin. Decreased IgG, slightly decreased IgA, elevated IgM, normal/elevated IgE. Elevated lipids. Na mildly decreased. Fibrinogen, factors V/VIII, protein C elevated. Calcium low (total, due to low albumin; ionized usually normal). Complement NORMAL. Thrombocytosis possible (urinary anticoagulant loss).

## Treatment
Adults initial: high-dose steroids (prednisone 1 mg/kg/day max 80 mg or 2 mg/kg QOD max 120 mg) x 4-16 weeks, taper >=24 weeks [KDIGO L1 C]. If contraindicated: CYC, CNI, MMF, or rituximab. Relapse: re-treat steroids; frequent relapsers add steroid-sparing agent. Steroid-resistant: repeat biopsy (exclude FSGS); CNI second-line (cyclosporine 5 mg/kg/day target 60-150 ng/mL, or tacrolimus 0.1 mg/kg/day target 5-10 ng/mL) x >=12 months [KDIGO L1 C]. Children initial: prednisone 60 mg/m2/day x 4-6 weeks then 40 mg/m2 QOD x 4-6 weeks [KDIGO L1 B]. Steroid-resistant children: CNI + low-dose steroids. Frequent relapsers: CYC 2-2.5 mg/kg/day x 8-12 weeks, levamisole, rituximab.
