# Dialysis -- Hemodialysis and Peritoneal Dialysis

## Exam Logic Summary

Dialysis is tested heavily. The exam tests: (1) HD vs PD indications/contraindications/comparison, (2) adequacy (Kt/V targets, URR), (3) vascular access hierarchy (AVF > AVG > CVC), (4) PD modalities (CAPD vs CCPD), (5) PET interpretation, (6) peritonitis management, (7) acute complications (dialysis disequilibrium, air embolism), (8) timing of dialysis initiation. Common distractor: starting dialysis based on GFR alone (IDEAL trial refutes this).

**Textbook ref:** Daugirdas Ch. 3-10 (HD), Ch. 25-32 (PD), KDOQI HD Adequacy 2015 (AJKD 2015;66(5):884)

---

## Indications for Dialysis Initiation

- Initiate emergently for life-threatening fluid, electrolyte, or acid-base disturbances
- Consider maintenance dialysis when >= 1 of the following occurs (typically eGFR 5-10):
  - Signs/symptoms of uremia (fatigue, anorexia, nausea, altered taste, cramps, pruritus, restless legs, cognitive changes)
  - Volume overload refractory to diuretics
  - Inability to control blood pressure
  - Malnutrition not responsive to dietary interventions
  - Persistent hyperkalemia, metabolic acidosis refractory to medical therapy
  - Pericarditis, serositis
- Plan for dialysis access when eGFR < 15-20 or KRT risk > 40% in 2 years
- Educate patients about ALL modalities (HD, PD, transplant, conservative care) when eGFR < 30 [KDOQI]

### IDEAL Trial (NEJM 2010)

- N=828, CKD stage 5, randomized to early (eGFR 10-14) vs late (eGFR 5-7) start
- NO significant difference in mortality (37.6% vs 36.6%)
- 75.9% of late-start group began dialysis with eGFR > 7 due to symptom development
- **Bottom line:** Do NOT start dialysis based on GFR number alone. Start based on symptoms and clinical indicators.

**Exam pearl:** This is the single most important trial on dialysis timing. The answer is ALWAYS "start when symptomatic" not "start when GFR reaches X."

## Hemodialysis

### Principles

- Blood pumped through a dialyzer (semipermeable membrane)
- Solute removal by diffusion (concentration gradient) and convection (hydrostatic pressure)
- Fluid removal by ultrafiltration (transmembrane pressure)
- Uremic solute classification:
  - Small molecular weight (< 500 Da): urea, K⁺, phosphate -- removed by standard HD
  - Middle molecules (500-60,000 Da): beta-2-microglobulin -- require high-flux membranes or HDF
  - Protein-bound (e.g., indoxyl sulfate, p-cresyl sulfate) -- poorly removed by any modality

### Adequacy -- Kt/V (MUST MEMORIZE)

| Parameter | Target |
|---|---|
| **spKt/V** (single-pool) | >= 1.4 per session (minimum delivered 1.2) [Strong Recommendation] |
| **stdKt/V** (for non-3x/week schedules) | >= 2.3 volumes/week (minimum 2.1) |
| **URR** (urea reduction ratio) | >= 65% for treatment < 5 hours |

- K = effective dialyzer urea clearance (mL/min)
- t = time on dialysis (minutes)
- V = volume of distribution for urea (mL), approximately total body water
- Target URR for HD given 3x/week with residual clearance < 2 mL/min: 70%
- For patients with significant residual renal function (RRF), dosing may be reduced if RRF is periodically evaluated

### Treatment Modalities

| Type | Setting | Schedule | Duration |
|---|---|---|---|
| Conventional HD | In-center | Daytime, 3x/week | 3-5 hours |
| Short frequent HD | In-center | Daytime, 5-7x/week | < 3 hours |
| Standard frequent HD | In-center | Daytime, 5-7x/week | 3-5 hours |
| Nocturnal HD (long) | Home | Nighttime, 5-7x/week | > 5 hours |
| Long intermittent HD | In-center/Home | Night/Day, 3x/week | > 5 hours |

- Standard schedule: 3 sessions/week, 3-5 hours each
- More frequent sessions: improved BP control, ↓ LVH, ↓ phosphate; but unclear effect on anemia/calcium metabolism
- Extended treatment (8-12 h/session): better BP and phosphate management
- Home HD: more patient autonomy, better QoL; barriers include lack of training, caregiver burden

### Vascular Access (ACCESS HIERARCHY)

**AVF > AVG > Tunneled CVC** [Strong Recommendation]

| Access | Timing | Advantages | Complications |
|---|---|---|---|
| **AVF** (arteriovenous fistula) | Place >= 6 months before anticipated HD start | Best long-term patency, lowest infection | Maturation failure (20-60%), steal syndrome |
| **AVG** (arteriovenous graft) | Place >= 3-6 weeks before HD | Faster maturation than AVF | Higher infection rate, stenosis, thrombosis |
| **Tunneled CVC** (central venous catheter) | Last resort or emergency | Immediate use | Highest infection/bacteremia, central stenosis, thrombosis |

- Preprocedural evaluation: history + physical, upper extremity arterial/venous duplex ultrasound
- Ultrasound criteria for AVF: arterial diameter > 1.6 mm, venous diameter > 2 mm without obstruction
- Monitor access patency: monthly physical exam, direct blood flow measurements, ultrasound surveillance [Strong Recommendation]
- Catheter dysfunction: inability to maintain blood flow >= 300 mL/min at prepump pressure < -250 mmHg

### Dialysate Composition

- Standard: Na⁺ 135-145, K⁺ 0-4, Ca²⁺ 1.25-1.5 mmol/L, HCO3⁻ 32-38 mmol/L, glucose 100-200 mg/dL
- Heparin for anticoagulation of extracorporeal circuit

### Complications

**Acute (during session):**
- Hypotension (most common): excessive UF rate, autonomic dysfunction, ↓ plasma refilling
- Muscle cramps: related to volume depletion and electrolyte shifts
- Dialysis disequilibrium syndrome: cerebral edema from rapid urea clearance → headache, nausea, seizures; prevent with shorter/slower initial sessions
- Air embolism: rare but fatal; clamp lines, Trendelenburg + left lateral decubitus, aspirate air
- Hemolysis: from overheated dialysate, chloramine contamination, or kink in blood line
- Allergic reaction: to dialyzer membrane or medications

**Chronic:**
- Vascular access complications: stenosis, thrombosis, infection, aneurysm, steal syndrome
- Amyloidosis (beta-2-microglobulin): carpal tunnel, bone cysts, arthropathy -- less common with high-flux membranes
- Volume overload between sessions: dietary Na/fluid noncompliance
- Cardiovascular mortality: remains leading cause of death in HD patients

## Peritoneal Dialysis

### Principles

- Peritoneal membrane (40 microns) serves as dialyzing membrane
- Dialysate fluid instilled into peritoneal cavity via indwelling catheter
- Solute removal: diffusion (concentration gradient) + convection (osmotic/hydrostatic pressure)
- Fluid removal (ultrafiltration): osmotic gradient from dextrose or icodextrin in dialysate
  - Osmotic gradient highest at beginning of dwell, diminishes as glucose absorbed
  - Icodextrin: slow osmotic agent for long dwells (overnight), maintains UF over 8-12 hours

### Types of PD

| Type | Description | Schedule |
|---|---|---|
| **CAPD** (Continuous Ambulatory) | Manual exchanges via gravity | 4-5 exchanges/day, 2-3 hour dwell, 2L volume |
| **CCPD/APD** (Continuous Cycling/Automated) | Automated cycler | 3-6 nighttime exchanges over 8-10 hours, 80-120 min dwell |

- CCPD preferred for most patients (frees daytime, 1 connect/disconnect)
- CAPD and APD appear to have similar mortality and hospital admissions (Cochrane 2024)
- Daytime exchange can be added to CCPD for additional clearance/UF

### Adequacy in PD

- Measure total Kt/V_urea (renal + peritoneal) within 1 month, then q4 months [Conditional Recommendation]
- Target: total small-solute clearance (Kt/V_urea) >= 1.7 per week [Conditional Recommendation]
- 24-hour urine collection q2 months if residual function contributes to clearance goal
- For patients without measurable RKF (urine < 100 mL/day): peritoneal Kt/V >= 1.7/week within 1 month of initiation [Conditional Recommendation]

### Peritoneal Equilibration Test (PET) -- MUST MEMORIZE

| Transport Type | D/P Creatinine (4h) | UF Volume | Clinical Implications |
|---|---|---|---|
| **High** | > 0.81 | Low (glucose absorbed fast) | Poor UF with standard dextrose; use icodextrin, short dwells (APD preferred) |
| **High-average** | 0.65-0.81 | Moderate | Standard prescription usually adequate |
| **Low-average** | 0.50-0.64 | Good | Adequate UF; may need longer dwells for solute clearance |
| **Low** | < 0.50 | High (glucose stays) | Excellent UF but poor solute clearance; longer dwells needed, higher volumes |

- PET = preferred method for evaluating peritoneal membrane transport capacity [Strong Recommendation]
- D/P creatinine = dialysate-to-plasma creatinine ratio at 4 hours
- UF failure = < 400 mL net UF using 4.25% dextrose over 4-hour dwell

**Exam pearl:** High transporters have the WORST outcomes on CAPD (poor UF → volume overload). They do better on APD with short dwells. Low transporters have excellent UF but need long dwells for adequate solute clearance.

### Catheter and Access

- Double-cuff Tenckhoff catheter (standard) [ISPD Grade 1C]
- Silicone rubber preferred [ISPD Grade 1B]
- Percutaneous, laparoscopic, or open surgical insertion
- Elective: wait >= 2 weeks after insertion before starting dialysis [ISPD Grade 1B]
- Break-in period < 2 weeks: low-volume supine exchanges with cycler to reduce leak risk [ISPD Grade 1C]
- Most common reasons for PD failure: infection and catheter complications

### Contraindications to PD

- Active or chronic inflammatory bowel disease
- Active abdominal cancer
- Recent major abdominal surgery or scarring
- Recently inserted ostomy
- Large abdominal wall hernias (relative -- repair at catheter placement may be possible)
- Severe cognitive/physical impairment without caregiver
- Inadequate sanitary living conditions
- Large polycystic kidneys (relative -- APD with low volumes may work)

### Residual Kidney Function (RKF) Preservation

- Monitor RKF q6 months with urine output [Strong Recommendation]
- RKF declines ~1-2 mL/min/1.73 m²/year (or Kt/V 0.2-0.4 per 6-month checkup)
- ACEi/ARB: administer to PD patients with measurable RKF as tolerated [Strong Recommendation]
- PD may preserve RKF better than HD (slower decline in residual function)

### Peritonitis Management (HIGH-YIELD)

- Definition: cloudy effluent + >= 100 WBC/mm³ with > 50% polymorphonuclear cells
- Most common organisms: coagulase-negative staphylococci (gram-positive), then S. aureus, then gram-negative
- Empiric therapy: IP vancomycin + IP gentamicin (or ceftazidime/cefepime for gram-negative coverage)
- Culture-guided therapy adjustment at 48-72 hours
- Duration: minimum 2 weeks for most organisms; 3 weeks for S. aureus and Pseudomonas
- Catheter removal indications: refractory peritonitis (no improvement by day 5), relapsing peritonitis, fungal peritonitis, mycobacterial peritonitis
- ISPD peritonitis rate goal: < 0.5 episodes per patient-year

**Exam pearl:** Fungal peritonitis = IMMEDIATE catheter removal + systemic antifungals. This is the most dangerous form. Relapsing peritonitis = same organism within 4 weeks of completing treatment. Recurrent = different organism within 4 weeks.

## HD vs PD -- Comparison (MUST KNOW)

| Feature | Hemodialysis | Peritoneal Dialysis |
|---|---|---|
| Setting | In-center (most) or home | Home |
| Frequency | 3-4x/week, 3-5 hours | Daily (CAPD) or nightly (APD) |
| Adequacy target | spKt/V >= 1.4/session | Total Kt/V >= 1.7/week |
| Access | AVF/AVG/CVC | Tenckhoff catheter |
| Fluid removal | Ultrafiltration (controlled) | Osmotic (dextrose/icodextrin) |
| Advantages | No peritonitis risk, lower technique failure, effective middle molecule clearance with HDF | Home-based, preserves RKF longer, better for early survival, patient autonomy |
| Disadvantages | Vascular access complications, hemodynamic instability, faster RKF loss | Peritonitis risk, technique burnout, glucose load → metabolic syndrome, limited solute clearance in high transporters |
| Survival | Similar overall (observational data) | May be better in first 1-2 years (younger, nondiabetic); HD better after 2-3 years in elderly/diabetic |
| Transplant | Similar QoL advantage of transplant over both | Same |

### Survival Evidence

- HD and PD associated with similar overall survival (observational studies only; no RCT exists)
- PD may have early survival advantage (first 1-2 years), especially in younger nondiabetic patients without comorbidities
- HD may have advantage in elderly (>= 65), diabetic patients with comorbidities after 2-3 years
- HD associated with ↑ risk of cerebrovascular accident vs PD (RR 1.08, 95% CI 1.01-1.15)
- Kidney transplant > both modalities for QoL and survival

### Mortality Rates (USRDS 2022)

- PD adjusted all-cause mortality: 129.8-155.8 per 1000 person-years (2019-2020)
- HD adjusted all-cause mortality: 162.7-190.1 per 1000 person-years (2019-2020)
- COVID-19 year (2020) mortality ↑ 20% for PD, ↑ 17% for HD

## High-Yield Exam Scenarios

1. **Dialysis adequacy question**: Patient on HD 3x/week, spKt/V measured at 1.1. This is BELOW minimum of 1.2. Options: increase blood flow rate, increase dialysis time, increase dialyzer size (surface area), or increase frequency.

2. **New HD patient with headache, confusion, seizure during first session**: Dialysis disequilibrium syndrome. Caused by rapid urea clearance → osmotic shift → cerebral edema. Prevention: short initial sessions (2 hours), low blood flow, small dialyzer.

3. **PD patient with cloudy effluent, WBC 500/mm³ (80% PMN)**: Peritonitis. Start IP vancomycin + IP ceftazidime empirically. Send effluent for culture and Gram stain.

4. **PD patient with poor UF despite 4.25% dextrose**: Check PET. If high transporter → switch to APD with short dwells + icodextrin for long dwell. If membrane failure → consider switch to HD.

5. **Which access to place for a patient starting HD in 8 months?** AVF (needs >= 6 months for maturation). If only 4 weeks available → AVG (3-6 weeks). If emergent → tunneled CVC.

6. **Obese patient with ESKD, IBD history**: PD is relatively contraindicated (IBD). Recommend HD. If PD is insisted upon, discuss ↑ risk of peritonitis and catheter complications.

7. **PD patient with peritonitis, culture grows Candida**: Fungal peritonitis = IMMEDIATE catheter removal + systemic antifungals (fluconazole or echinocandin). Do NOT attempt to treat through the catheter.

8. **IDEAL trial question**: 60-year-old with eGFR 12, asymptomatic. Should you start dialysis? NO. The IDEAL trial showed no benefit to early initiation. Monitor symptoms and start when clinically indicated.
