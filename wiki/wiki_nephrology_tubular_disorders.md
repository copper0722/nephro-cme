# Inherited Tubular Disorders — Bartter, Gitelman, Liddle, SAME

## Exam Logic Summary

These four inherited tubulopathies are repeatedly tested because they require integrating nephron physiology, genetics, and electrolyte patterns. The exam tests: (1) Bartter vs. Gitelman differentiation (urine calcium is the KEY distinguisher), (2) Liddle vs. primary aldosteronism (both HTN + hypoK but different renin/aldo patterns and treatment response), (3) SAME vs. Liddle (both low renin + low aldo but different cortisol:cortisone ratio and treatment response), (4) which diuretic each disorder mimics, (5) genetic mutations and inheritance patterns. Common distractors: confusing Bartter Type 3 with Gitelman (both have hypochloremia/hypomagnesemia); treating Liddle with spironolactone (won't work — ENaC is constitutively active).

**Textbook ref:** Brenner Ch. 16 (Inherited Tubular Disorders), Harrison Ch. 315 (Hereditary Renal Disease), Daugirdas Ch. 10 (Electrolytes)

# BARTTER SYNDROME

## Definition

Rare inherited salt-wasting tubulopathy caused by loss-of-function mutations in ion transport proteins in the **thick ascending limb (TAL)** of the loop of Henle. Common endpoint: loss of NKCC2 function.

Characterized by: salt wasting, hypokalemic metabolic alkalosis, **hypercalciuria**, normal or low BP, elevated renin and aldosterone.

## Pathophysiology — TAL Transport

Normal TAL function:
1. **NKCC2** (apical) = primary driver: reabsorbs Na⁺, K⁺, 2Cl⁻ from lumen
2. **ROMK** (apical) = recycles K⁺ back to lumen → maintains lumen-positive potential
3. **ClC-Ka and ClC-Kb** (basolateral) = chloride exit channels, require **Barttin** protein
4. **Na⁺-K⁺-ATPase** (basolateral) = drives Na⁺ out of cell
5. Lumen-positive voltage drives **paracellular reabsorption of Ca²⁺ and Mg²⁺**
6. TAL reabsorbs ~25% of filtered NaCl + generates medullary hypertonicity (concentrating mechanism)

**Loss of any component → salt wasting + loss of lumen-positive potential → calcium and magnesium wasting.**

## Types and Genetics

| Type | Gene | Protein | Key Features | Onset |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **1** | *SLC12A1* | NKCC2 | Prenatal/antenatal; polyhydramnios, prematurity; severe | Antenatal |
| **2** | *KCNJ1* | ROMK (Kir1.1) | Prenatal; TRANSIENT hyperkalemia at birth → then hypokalemia | Antenatal |
| **3** (classic) | *CLCNKB* | ClC-Kb | Most common surviving type; presents childhood 0-5y; severe hypochloremia; may resemble Gitelman (hypocalciuria, hypomagnesemia overlap) | Childhood |
| **4a** | *BSND* | Barttin | + Sensorineural deafness; severe | Antenatal |
| **4b** | *CLCNKA + CLCNKB* | ClC-Ka + ClC-Kb | + Sensorineural deafness; digenic | Antenatal |
| **5** | *MAGED2* | MAGE-D2 | **X-linked**; transient antenatal Bartter; may resolve in infancy | Antenatal (transient) |

**Exam pearl:** Type 2 (ROMK) = unique transient HYPERKALEMIA at birth (before hypokalemia develops). ROMK is needed for K⁺ recycling in TAL AND for K⁺ secretion in collecting duct — neonatal collecting duct function temporarily impaired. Type 5 = X-linked recessive and may be transient.

## Epidemiology

- Annual incidence ~1 per 1,000,000
- Type 1 gene carrier frequency: 1/360 (Framingham)
- Type 2 gene carrier frequency: 1/670 (Framingham)
- Types 1, 2, 4, 5 present earlier (antenatal/neonatal) with more severe symptoms

## Clinical Features

- **Antenatal:** maternal polyhydramnios (24-36 weeks), prematurity, elevated amniotic Cl⁻
- **Neonatal:** volume depletion, dehydration, failure to thrive, fever
- **Childhood/adult:** salt craving, polyuria, polydipsia, muscle weakness, cramps
- **Lab:** hypokalemia, metabolic alkalosis, hypercalciuria (→ nephrocalcinosis), elevated renin + aldosterone
- **Nephrocalcinosis** develops in most forms of Bartter

## Diagnosis

1. Serum electrolyte panel: K⁺, Na⁺, Cl⁻, Mg²⁺, Ca²⁺, HCO₃⁻
2. Blood pH
3. Urine: Na⁺, Cl⁻, Ca²⁺ excretion, fractional excretion of Na and Cl
4. Plasma renin activity + aldosterone (both elevated)
5. Renal ultrasound: nephrocalcinosis
6. ECG: prolonged QT (hypokalemia)
7. **Genetic testing** = confirmatory

**Prenatal diagnosis:** unexplained polyhydramnios + elevated amniotic Cl⁻ between 24-36 weeks → suspect Bartter → genetic testing [Conditional Rec]. Consider amniotic Bartter index (AFP x total protein) if genetic testing unavailable.

## Management

1. **Acute life-threatening complications** (neonatal): immediate fluid and electrolyte replacement
2. **Lifelong therapy:** diet + electrolyte supplementation (KCl, NaCl)
3. **NSAIDs** = mainstay pharmacotherapy [Conditional Rec]:
   - Indomethacin 1-4 mg/kg/day in 3-4 doses
   - Ibuprofen 15-30 mg/kg/day in 3 doses
   - Celecoxib 2-10 mg/kg/day in 2 doses
   - + Gastric acid protection (PPI) [Conditional Rec]
   - Mechanism: inhibit prostaglandin synthesis → ↓ renal blood flow → ↓ GFR → ↓ salt wasting
4. **Avoid:** loop diuretics (potassium-sparing diuretics controversial), ACEi/ARBs [Conditional Rec against — risk of hypovolemia and worsened salt wasting]
5. **Growth delay:** optimize metabolic control before recombinant GH
6. **Follow-up** in centers with renal tubular disorder expertise [Conditional Rec]
7. **Genetic counseling** for families

# GITELMAN SYNDROME

## Definition

Rare autosomal recessive tubulopathy caused by loss-of-function mutations in **NCC** (thiazide-sensitive sodium chloride cotransporter) in the **distal convoluted tubule (DCT)**.

Characterized by: salt wasting, hypokalemic metabolic alkalosis, **hypomagnesemia**, **hypocalciuria**, normal or low BP.

## Key Differences from Bartter

| Feature | Bartter (TAL) | Gitelman (DCT) |
|---|---|---|
| Protein affected | NKCC2 (and partners) | NCC |
| Mimics | Loop diuretic | **Thiazide diuretic** |
| Age of onset | Earlier (prenatal-childhood) | **Later** (adolescence/adulthood) |
| Severity | More severe | **Less severe** |
| Urine calcium | **Elevated** (hypercalciuria) | **Reduced** (hypocalciuria) |
| Magnesium | Normal or mildly low | **Significantly LOW** |
| Nephrocalcinosis | Yes (common) | **No** (may have chondrocalcinosis) |

**Exam pearl:** The single best lab test to distinguish Bartter from Gitelman = **urine calcium excretion**. Hypercalciuria = Bartter. Hypocalciuria = Gitelman. Urine Ca/Cr ratio > 0.26 mg/mg → Bartter/loop diuretic. Urine Ca/Cr ratio < 0.02 mg/mg → Gitelman/thiazide.

## Genetics

- *SLC12A3* gene (chromosome 16q13): encodes NCC in DCT
- Present in ~70% of patients: 236 compound heterozygous, 79 homozygous (in 448-patient cohort)
- *CLCNKB* mutations can produce Gitelman phenotype (overlap with Bartter type 3)
- 15-20% have only 1 detectable *SLC12A3* mutation (intronic/regulatory mutations suspected)
- SLC12A3 heterozygous carrier frequency: 1% White, **2.9% Asian** populations

## Epidemiology

- Incidence: 1-10 per 40,000
- One of the most frequently occurring inherited renal tubular disorders
- Symptoms appear age >= 6 years; diagnosis typically adolescence/adulthood

## Pathophysiology

- Loss of NCC function in DCT → impaired NaCl reabsorption → volume contraction
- Volume contraction → RAA activation → ↑ renin, ↑ aldosterone → K⁺ and H⁺ secretion in CCD → hypokalemia + metabolic alkalosis
- **Hypocalciuria mechanism:** DCT NaCl reabsorption normally reduces intracellular Na⁺ → drives basolateral Na⁺/Ca²⁺ exchange (NCX) → Ca²⁺ exit. With NCC loss: less intracellular Na⁺ entry → MORE NCX activity → MORE Ca²⁺ reabsorption → hypocalciuria
- **Hypomagnesemia mechanism:** chronic DCT dysfunction → downregulation of TRPM6 Mg²⁺ channel → renal Mg²⁺ wasting

## Clinical Features

- Often asymptomatic (discovered incidentally)
- Salt craving, muscle cramps/weakness, fatigue, dizziness
- Nocturia, thirst, polydipsia
- Paresthesia, numbness, palpitations
- Low blood pressure
- **Chondrocalcinosis** (calcium pyrophosphate deposits in cartilage) — unique to Gitelman

## Associated Conditions

- Epilepsy, growth retardation
- Pubertal delay (both sexes)
- Neuromuscular disorders

## Diagnosis

1. Lab: hypokalemia + metabolic alkalosis + hypomagnesemia + hypocalciuria
2. Elevated plasma renin + aldosterone
3. Fractional excretion of electrolytes: Na⁺, Cl⁻, Mg²⁺
4. Renal ultrasound: typically normal (no nephrocalcinosis)
5. Exclude diuretic use, laxative abuse, chronic vomiting
6. **Genetic testing** (*SLC12A3*) = confirmatory

## Management

1. **Asymptomatic patients:** ambulatory monitoring by nephrologist, no pharmacotherapy required
2. **Dietary NaCl:** ad libitum (encourage salt craving)
3. **Magnesium supplementation** = FIRST priority:
   - Oral MgCl₂ (preferred — also replaces Cl⁻): 300 mg/day (12.24 mmol/day Mg) or 5 mg/kg in children
   - Slow-release tablets in 2-4 divided doses with meals
   - Target Mg²⁺ >= 0.6 mmol/L (1.46 mg/dL)
   - Dose titration based on blood levels and GI tolerance
   - IV Mg²⁺ for: GI intolerance, acute tetany, cardiac arrhythmia
4. **Potassium supplementation:**
   - Oral KCl: >= 2,980 mg/day (40 mmol) in adults; 74-149 mg/kg/day (1-2 mmol/kg/day) in children
   - Dose titration based on symptoms
   - IV KCl if: unable to take oral, severe deficit (arrhythmia, quadriplegia, respiratory failure, rhabdomyolysis)
5. **Refractory hypokalemia** despite adequate Mg²⁺ + KCl:
   - **K⁺-sparing diuretics:** amiloride, spironolactone, potassium canrenoate, eplerenone
   - RAASi (ACEi/ARBs)
   - NSAIDs (less evidence than for Bartter)
6. **Chondrocalcinosis:** NSAIDs, low-dose colchicine, corticosteroids
7. **Genetic counseling** for all patients

**Exam pearl:** Magnesium repletion facilitates potassium correction. Always correct Mg²⁺ first in Gitelman (or any refractory hypokalemia). KCl alone won't work if Mg²⁺ is depleted.

# LIDDLE SYNDROME

## Definition

Rare autosomal dominant disorder caused by **gain-of-function mutations** in ENaC (epithelial sodium channel) subunit genes in the **collecting tubule**, resulting in constitutive ENaC hyperfunction → excessive Na⁺ reabsorption and K⁺/H⁺ secretion.

Pseudohyperaldosteronism type 1.

## Genetics

- *SCNN1A* (alpha-ENaC), *SCNN1B* (beta-ENaC), *SCNN1G* (gamma-ENaC)
- **Autosomal dominant** (unlike most other tubulopathies which are AR)
- Mutations usually in beta or gamma subunit → prevent Nedd4-2 mediated ENaC degradation → channels stay on membrane longer

## Pathophysiology

1. Gain-of-function ENaC → constitutive Na⁺ reabsorption in CCD
2. Na⁺ reabsorption → lumen-negative potential → K⁺ and H⁺ secretion
3. Volume expansion → **hypertension** (early-onset)
4. Negative feedback: volume expansion suppresses renin → suppresses aldosterone
5. Result: **HTN + hypokalemia + metabolic alkalosis + LOW renin + LOW aldosterone**

## Clinical Features

- **Early-onset hypertension** (childhood or adolescence, sometimes not detected until adulthood)
- Family history of early-onset HTN
- Hypokalemia (K⁺ < 3.5)
- Metabolic alkalosis (HCO₃⁻ > 26)
- Low plasma renin activity (< 0.65 ng/mL/hour)
- Low aldosterone (< 5 ng/dL)
- Normal urinary cortisol:cortisone ratio (KEY distinguisher from SAME)
- Does NOT respond to mineralocorticoid antagonists (spironolactone/eplerenone)
- **DOES respond to amiloride/triamterene** (direct ENaC blockers)

**Exam pearl:** HTN + hypoK + low renin + low aldo + no response to spironolactone + responds to amiloride = LIDDLE. This is the most tested aspect: spironolactone blocks the mineralocorticoid receptor, but in Liddle the ENaC is constitutively active DOWNSTREAM of the receptor → spironolactone has no effect.

## Diagnosis

1. Clinical suspicion: young patient with hypertension + hypokalemia + metabolic alkalosis + family history
2. Lab: low renin, low aldosterone
3. Normal urinary cortisol:cortisone ratio (rules out SAME)
4. Low urinary Na⁺ and aldosterone
5. **Genetic testing** for *SCNN1B*, *SCNN1G*, *SCNN1A* mutations = confirmatory
6. **Therapeutic trial:** amiloride normalizes K⁺ and BP → supports diagnosis

## Treatment

- **Low sodium diet** (essential)
- **Amiloride** = drug of choice (directly blocks ENaC)
- **Triamterene** = alternative ENaC blocker
- Amiloride safe during pregnancy
- **Spironolactone/eplerenone are INEFFECTIVE** (problem is DOWNSTREAM of MR)
- Monitor during treatment and pregnancy

# SYNDROME OF APPARENT MINERALOCORTICOID EXCESS (SAME/AME)

## Definition

Rare autosomal recessive disorder caused by **loss-of-function mutations** in *HSD11B2* gene encoding **11-beta-hydroxysteroid dehydrogenase type 2 (11b-HSD2)** in the kidney. This enzyme normally converts cortisol to cortisone in the collecting duct, preventing cortisol from activating the mineralocorticoid receptor (MR). Without 11b-HSD2, cortisol activates MR → mineralocorticoid excess state.

Also called: Ulick syndrome

## Pathophysiology

1. Normal: cortisol has equal affinity for MR as aldosterone, but **11b-HSD2 in CCD converts cortisol → cortisone** (inactive at MR) → prevents cortisol from acting as mineralocorticoid
2. AME: 11b-HSD2 loss-of-function → cortisol reaches MR → activates it → same effect as aldosterone excess
3. Cortisol (present at 100-1000x aldosterone concentration) overwhelms MR
4. Result: Na⁺ retention, K⁺/H⁺ secretion, volume expansion → HTN
5. Negative feedback: volume suppresses renin → suppresses aldosterone

## AME Mimics (Acquired)

- **Licorice (glycyrrhizin) ingestion** = inhibits 11b-HSD2 → exact same phenotype as genetic AME
- Carbenoxolone, grapefruit juice (naringenin)
- **Exam pearl:** liquorice-induced pseudohyperaldosteronism has the SAME lab pattern as AME but is acquired and reversible with cessation. Always ask about dietary history.

## Clinical Features

- Low birth weight, severe hypertension (can present in infancy)
- Hypokalemia, metabolic alkalosis
- **Low renin, low aldosterone** (same pattern as Liddle)
- **HIGH urinary cortisol:cortisone ratio** = pathognomonic (THF+alloTHF)/THE ratio markedly elevated
- Polyuria, polydipsia (hypokalemia + nephrogenic DI component)

## Liddle vs. SAME — The Exam Differentiator

| Feature | Liddle | SAME |
|---|---|---|
| Gene | SCNN1B/1G/1A (ENaC gain-of-function) | HSD11B2 (11b-HSD2 loss-of-function) |
| Inheritance | **AD** | **AR** |
| Mechanism | Constitutive ENaC activation | Cortisol activates MR (cortisol not inactivated) |
| Renin | Low | Low |
| Aldosterone | Low | Low |
| **Urinary cortisol:cortisone** | **Normal** | **Markedly elevated** |
| Responds to spironolactone | **No** | **Yes** (blocks MR that cortisol is activating) |
| Responds to amiloride | **Yes** (blocks ENaC) | **Yes** (blocks downstream ENaC) |
| Responds to dexamethasone | No | **Yes** (suppresses ACTH → ↓ cortisol → ↓ MR activation) |

**Exam pearl:** KEY distinguisher = urinary cortisol:cortisone ratio. Normal in Liddle (enzyme is intact, problem is ENaC), elevated in SAME (enzyme is defective). Second distinguisher: spironolactone works in SAME but NOT in Liddle.

## Treatment of SAME

- **Low sodium diet**
- **Mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists:** spironolactone, eplerenone (WORKS — blocks MR that cortisol is activating)
- **Potassium-sparing diuretics:** amiloride, triamterene (also works — blocks ENaC downstream)
- **Dexamethasone:** suppresses ACTH → reduces cortisol production → less MR activation
- Potassium supplements for initial management
- **Renal transplantation** may be beneficial (new kidney has functioning 11b-HSD2)
- Treat acquired form (licorice) by **cessation of licorice intake** (reversible)

## Comprehensive Differential: Low-Renin Hypertension with Hypokalemia

| Disorder | BP | K⁺ | Renin | Aldo | Cortisol:Cortisone | Gene | Treatment |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Primary aldosteronism** | High | Low/normal | Low | **Elevated** | Normal | CYP11B1/CYP11B2/KCNJ5 | Surgery or spironolactone |
| **Glucocorticoid resistance** | High | Low | Low | **Elevated** | Normal | NR3C1 | - |
| **Congenital adrenal hyperplasia (11b-OH)** | High | Low | Low | Low | Normal | CYP11B1, CYP17A1 | Glucocorticoid replacement |
| **Liddle syndrome** | High | Low | Low | Low | **Normal** | SCNN1B/1G/1A | Amiloride |
| **SAME (AME)** | High | Low | Low | Low | **Elevated** | HSD11B2 | Spironolactone + dexamethasone |
| **Licorice ingestion** | High | Low | Low | Low | **Elevated** | None | Stop licorice |
| **Gordon syndrome (PHA2)** | High | **High** | Low | Low/normal | Normal | WNK1/WNK4/CUL3/KLHL3 | Thiazide diuretics |

**Exam pearl:** Gordon syndrome (pseudohypoaldosteronism type 2) is the ONLY disorder with HTN + HYPERkalemia + metabolic acidosis + low renin. Caused by WNK kinase mutations → NCC overactivation → thiazide-responsive. It is essentially the mirror image of Gitelman.

## High-Yield Exam Scenarios

1. **Neonate with polyhydramnios, hypokalemia, metabolic alkalosis, hypercalciuria:** Bartter syndrome (likely type 1 or 2). Check for nephrocalcinosis on renal US. Treatment: NSAIDs (indomethacin) + KCl + NaCl supplementation.

2. **20-year-old with chronic hypoK, metabolic alkalosis, normal BP, low urine Ca, low Mg:** Classic Gitelman. SLC12A3 mutation. Lifelong MgCl₂ + KCl. Consider amiloride if refractory.

3. **12-year-old with severe HTN, hypoK, low renin, low aldo, responds to amiloride but NOT spironolactone:** Liddle syndrome. SCNN1B/G mutation. AD inheritance.

4. **Infant with severe HTN, hypoK, low renin, low aldo, high urinary cortisol:cortisone ratio:** SAME (AME syndrome). HSD11B2 mutation. Treat with spironolactone + dexamethasone.

5. **Middle-aged man eating lots of licorice → HTN + hypoK + low renin + low aldo:** Acquired AME from glycyrrhizin. Same lab pattern as SAME. Stop licorice → resolves.

6. **HTN + hyperkalemia + metabolic acidosis + low renin:** Gordon syndrome (PHA2), not in this group but critical differential. Responds to thiazide.

7. **Bartter type 3 vs. Gitelman overlap:** Type 3 Bartter (CLCNKB) can present with hypocalciuria + hypomagnesemia (resembling Gitelman). Genetic testing is definitive. Clinical clue: Bartter type 3 typically presents earlier (childhood) and may have more severe salt wasting.
