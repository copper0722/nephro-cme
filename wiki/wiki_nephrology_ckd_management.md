# CKD Management -- Cardiovascular Disease, Comorbidities, and End-of-Life Care

## Exam Logic Summary

CKD management questions test: (1) CVD as leading cause of death in CKD/ESKD, (2) BP targets in CKD (KDIGO vs JNC vs ACC/AHA), (3) statin use in CKD (SHARP trial, no initiation on dialysis), (4) dialysis initiation timing (IDEAL trial -- no benefit from early start), (5) conservative care vs dialysis decision-making in elderly, (6) dialysis withdrawal ethics and prognosis. Common distractor: starting statins de novo on dialysis patients (SHARP showed benefit only in pre-dialysis CKD, not for patients already on dialysis).

**Textbook ref:** Brenner Ch. 55-56 (CKD Management, CVD in CKD), Daugirdas Ch. 1-2 (CKD Management), KDIGO 2024 CKD Guidelines

## CVD in CKD -- Overview

- CVD = leading cause of death in CKD and ESKD patients
- CKD is an independent risk factor for CVD (even after adjusting for traditional risk factors)
- Annual cardiovascular mortality: ~10-20x higher in dialysis patients vs age-matched general population
- CKD patients more likely to die from CVD than progress to ESKD
- Non-traditional CVD risk factors in CKD: uremic toxins, anemia, CKD-MBD (vascular calcification), volume overload, inflammation, oxidative stress, sympathetic overactivity

### CVD Risk Factor Management in CKD

#### Hypertension

| Guideline | BP Target in CKD |
|---|---|
| **KDIGO 2024** | SBP < 120 mmHg (if tolerated) in CKD not on dialysis; < 130/80 in transplant |
| **ACC/AHA 2017** | < 130/80 mmHg |
| **JNC 8** | < 140/90 mmHg |
| **ESC/ESH** | SBP 130-139 in CKD |
| **Hypertension Canada** | Individualized; consider < 120 in SPRINT-eligible |

- ACEi/ARB = first-line for CKD with proteinuria [Strong]
- Do NOT combine ACEi + ARB (hyperkalemia, worsening renal function)
- Stop ACEi if K+ > 5.6 mmol/L despite potassium-lowering strategies
- SCr rise > 30% above baseline on ACEi/ARB: consider stopping (but stable patients can tolerate up to 5.7 mEq/L)
- Intensive BP control (< 130/80) reduces ESRD in proteinuric CKD but no significant difference in major CVD events vs standard (< 140/90)

**Exam pearl:** KDIGO recommends SBP < 120 mmHg for CKD patients NOT on dialysis (based on SPRINT CKD subgroup). This is more aggressive than ACC/AHA (< 130) and JNC 8 (< 140). The exam tests which target to apply in CKD -- KDIGO 2024 is the nephrology-specific guideline.

#### Key BP Trials in CKD

| Trial | Population | Finding |
|---|---|---|
| **SPRINT (CKD subgroup)** | CKD with increased CV risk | Intensive (SBP < 120) reduced composite CVD; CKD subgroup consistent |
| **AASK** | African Americans with hypertensive nephropathy | Intensive MAP <= 92 did NOT reduce renal endpoints vs standard MAP 102-107 |
| **ACCORD** | Type 2 DM with high CV risk | SBP < 120 reduced stroke but not primary composite; increased adverse events |
| **REIN-2** | Nondiabetic nephropathy | Intensive (< 130/80) vs standard (< 90 DBP): no significant difference |

#### Dyslipidemia
- Statins reduce CVD events in CKD stages 1-4 (SHARP: simvastatin/ezetimibe reduced major atherosclerotic events by 17% in CKD)
- Do NOT initiate statins de novo in patients already on dialysis (4D, AURORA showed no benefit)
- If already on statin when starting dialysis: continue
- KDIGO Lipids: treat with statin (or statin/ezetimibe) in adults >= 50 with eGFR < 60 not on dialysis [Grade 1A]; age < 50 with CKD: if another risk factor present [Grade 2A]

**Exam pearl:** The critical distinction: statins benefit pre-dialysis CKD patients but NOT dialysis patients when initiated de novo. If a question asks about starting a statin in a new HD patient with no prior statin use, the answer is that current evidence does NOT support this. If they were already on a statin before starting dialysis, continue it.

#### Antiplatelet Therapy
- Aspirin for secondary prevention in CKD patients with established CVD
- Primary prevention with aspirin: no clear net benefit in CKD (bleeding risk may outweigh)

#### Diabetes Management
- Target HbA1c approximately 7% (individualize in elderly/comorbid)
- SGLT2 inhibitors: cardiorenal benefits in CKD with or without diabetes (DAPA-CKD, EMPA-KIDNEY, CREDENCE)
- Finerenone (nonsteroidal MRA): reduces CVD events + slows CKD progression in diabetic kidney disease (FIDELIO-DKN, FIGARO-DKN)

## Dialysis Initiation Timing

### IDEAL Trial (Landmark)
- N = 828; early start (GFR 10-14) vs late start (GFR 5-7)
- No difference in mortality, CVD events, infections, or dialysis complications
- Mean actual GFR at dialysis start: 12.0 (early) vs 9.8 (late)
- Bottom line: start dialysis based on symptoms, NOT based on arbitrary GFR threshold

**Exam pearl:** The IDEAL trial is the only major RCT on dialysis timing. It showed NO survival benefit from early initiation. The correct answer for "when to start dialysis" is: based on uremic symptoms, volume overload, refractory hyperkalemia/acidosis -- NOT at a specific GFR cutoff.

## Dialysis Withdrawal and Palliative Care

### Who Considers Forgoing/Withdrawing Dialysis
- Patients with ESRD/advanced CKD where burdens of treatment outweigh benefits
- Decision regret is common among those who chose dialysis
- Ethically and legally permissible; guided by patient autonomy, beneficence, nonmaleficence

### Rates
- US: 14.5% of veterans with advanced CKD decided against dialysis initiation
- 24.3% of US dialysis patients discontinued maintenance dialysis before death (2018)
- Forgoing more common with older age: 46.7% of adults >= 85 vs 3.8% of < 45

### Factors Favoring Conservative Care Over Dialysis (Adults > 65 with GFR 15-45)

| GFR Decline Rate | Comorbidity Burden | Recommendation |
|---|---|---|
| Slow (< 3 mL/min/1.73 m²/year) | Low | Low likelihood of needing dialysis |
| Slow | High | High probability of dying from nonrenal cause before needing dialysis |
| Moderate (3-5) | High | More likely to die before dialysis needed → consider conservative care |
| Rapid | Any | May need dialysis; shared decision-making |

### Survival: Dialysis vs Conservative Care

| Population | Dialysis Survival | Conservative Survival |
|---|---|---|
| Adults >= 55 years (1-year) | 73%-78% | 70.6% |
| Adults >= 70 (median) | 3.1 years | 1.5 years |
| Adults >= 80 (median) | 2.1 years | 1.4 years (NS) |
| Without CV comorbidity | 7.3 years | 1.9 years |
| With CV comorbidity | 2.3 years | 1.5 years |

- Among adults >= 80 or with severe comorbidity (Davies score >= 3): survival advantage of dialysis is NONSIGNIFICANT
- Conservative care associated with: more days at home, greater likelihood of dying at home/hospice (OR 4.15)
- HD initiation NOT associated with improved QoL (SF-36 physical/mental health) vs conservative care

### Risk Factors for Poor Outcomes Even With Dialysis (>= 2 of following)
- Age >= 75 years
- High comorbidity (modified Charlson >= 8)
- Marked functional impairment (Karnofsky < 40%)
- Severe chronic malnutrition (albumin < 2.5 g/dL by bromocresol green)

### Prognostic Tools
- **Bansal score:** 5-year mortality in CKD 3-5 (adults >= 65); modest utility
- **REIN score:** 3-month mortality with dialysis in nonfrail older adults
- Frailty = independent risk factor for mortality

### Symptom Burden in Advanced CKD
- High symptom burden regardless of dialysis vs conservative care
- Both groups: fatigue, difficulty sleeping, cold aversion, lower torso weakness, pruritus
- Dialysis-specific: skin changes (54.5% vs 28.9%), halitosis (33.6% vs 17.8%), sexual issues (34.3% vs 8.9%)
- Conservative care-specific: more dyspnea (46.7% vs 29.9%)
- HD may accelerate cognitive decline (reduced cerebral blood flow → ischemic brain injury)

### After Dialysis Withdrawal
- Median survival approximately 8-12 days (range 1-40+)
- Focus: symptom management (pain, nausea, pruritus, dyspnea, agitation), psychosocial/spiritual support

**Exam pearl:** The exam may present an elderly patient (> 80) with multiple comorbidities (diabetes, CHF, dementia) considering dialysis. The correct approach: shared decision-making, discuss that survival advantage of dialysis is modest at best in this population, conservative care may provide similar QoL with less treatment burden. Do NOT automatically recommend dialysis for all ESKD patients.

## High-Yield Exam Scenarios

1. **65-year-old with CKD stage 3b (eGFR 38), diabetes, HTN. Current SBP 148 mmHg on lisinopril and amlodipine. K+ 4.8. What is BP target?** KDIGO: SBP < 120 if tolerated. ACC/AHA: < 130/80. Add third agent (thiazide or chlorthalidone). Monitor K+ and SCr after dose adjustment.

2. **72-year-old on HD for 5 years, LDL 160 mg/dL, no prior statin use. Should you start a statin?** Current evidence (4D, AURORA) does NOT support initiating statins de novo on dialysis. However, some guidelines suggest it is reasonable. The EXAM answer based on KDIGO Lipids: do NOT routinely initiate in adults on chronic dialysis.

3. **85-year-old with eGFR 12, Karnofsky 30%, advanced dementia, CHF NYHA IV. Family asks about dialysis.** Discuss conservative care. In this population (>= 2 high-risk factors), dialysis offers minimal survival advantage with significant burden. Conservative renal care focuses on symptom management and QoL.

4. **Patient on HD for 3 years with advanced cancer, decides to withdraw dialysis. Is this ethical?** Yes. Dialysis withdrawal is ethically and legally permissible. Decision guided by patient autonomy. Median survival after withdrawal: 8-12 days. Transition to palliative/hospice care.

5. **58-year-old with CKD stage 4 (eGFR 22), no uremic symptoms. GFR declining 2 mL/min/year. When to start dialysis?** IDEAL trial: no benefit from early initiation. Start based on symptoms (uremia, volume overload, refractory electrolyte abnormalities), NOT at a fixed GFR threshold. Current practice: most start around eGFR 5-10 when symptomatic.
