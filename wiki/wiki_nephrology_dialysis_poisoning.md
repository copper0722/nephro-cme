# Dialysis for Acute Poisonings

## Exam Logic Summary

Dialysis for poisoning is a Section 07 topic (AKI & toxicology). The exam tests: (1) which poisons are dialyzable and which are not, (2) physicochemical properties that determine dialyzability, (3) specific EXTRIP indications and cutoffs for common toxins, (4) when to choose IHD vs CRRT vs hemoperfusion. Common distractors: suggesting dialysis for highly protein-bound or high-Vd toxins, not knowing the specific serum level thresholds.

**Textbook ref:** Daugirdas Ch. 27 (Dialysis in Poisoning), Brenner Ch. 33 (Drug Dosing/Poisoning)

---

## Principles of Toxin Dialyzability

### Physicochemical Properties (MUST MEMORIZE)

| Property | Favors dialysis | Against dialysis |
|---|---|---|
| Molecular weight | Low (< 500-1,000 Da) | High (> 5,000 Da for HD) |
| Protein binding | Low (free drug dialyzed) | High (drug bound to albumin) |
| Volume of distribution | Low (< 1-2 L/kg; drug in vascular space) | High (> 1-2 L/kg; drug in tissues) |
| Water solubility | High | Low (lipophilic → distributes to fat) |
| Endogenous clearance | Low (< 4 mL/min/kg) | High (liver metabolizes rapidly anyway) |

**Exam pearl:** "Is this drug dialyzable?" Think SMALL, FREE, WET, STUCK IN BLOOD. Small molecule + low protein binding + low Vd + water soluble = dialyzable. Large + protein-bound + high Vd + lipophilic = NOT dialyzable.

### EXTRIP Dialyzability Categories

| Category | Toxin Removed in 6h ECTR |
|---|---|
| Dialyzable | > 30% |
| Moderately dialyzable | 10-30% |
| Slightly dialyzable | 3-10% |
| Not dialyzable | < 3% |

---

## ECTR Modalities

| Modality | Mechanism | Best for | Clearance |
|---|---|---|---|
| **IHD** (preferred) | Diffusion across semipermeable membrane | Small molecules (< 5,000 Da), water-soluble, low protein binding | 200-250 mL/min |
| **CRRT** | Diffusion + convection, continuous | Hemodynamically unstable patients | 2-3x less per hour than IHD |
| **Hemoperfusion** | Adsorption to charcoal/resin column | Protein-bound + larger molecules (100-40,000 Da) | Variable |
| **HF/HDF** | Convection +/- diffusion | Middle molecules (up to 50,000 Da) | Less common for poisoning |

**Exam pearl:** IHD is the PREFERRED modality for poisoning [Strong Recommendation]. CRRT = backup if IHD unavailable or patient hemodynamically unstable. Hemoperfusion = consider for highly protein-bound toxins but higher complication rate (hypocalcemia, thrombocytopenia).

## Common Dialyzable Poisonings (Exam High-Yield Table)

| Toxin | EXTRIP Grade | Key Threshold for ECTR | Antidote | Stop ECTR when |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Ethylene glycol** | Dialyzable | > 50 mmol/L + ethanol; > 10 mmol/L + no antidote; AG > 27 | Fomepizole (preferred) or ethanol | AG < 18 mmol/L |
| **Methanol** | Dialyzable | Coma/seizures/visual deficits; pH <= 7.15; level > 700 mg/L (fomepizole) or > 500 mg/L (no antidote) | Fomepizole or ethanol | Level < 200 mg/L + clinical improvement |
| **Lithium** | Grade A | Severe toxicity; renal impairment + Li > 4 mEq/L; decreased consciousness/seizures/dysrhythmias | None (supportive) | Clinical improvement OR Li < 1 mEq/L; if unable to measure, after >= 6h |
| **Salicylate** | Grade B | Level > 7.2 mmol/L (100 mg/dL); > 6.5 mmol/L if impaired renal function; AMS; new hypoxemia | NaHCO3 (urinary alkalinization) | Level < 1.4 mmol/L (19 mg/dL) |
| **Metformin** | Grade C | Lactate > 20 mmol/L; pH <= 7; refractory | NaHCO3 | Lactate < 3 mmol/L + pH > 7.35 |
| **Acetaminophen** | Grade C | Severe poisoning; level > 1,000 mg/L (no NAC) [Strong]; > 900 mg/L (with NAC) [Strong] | NAC (N-acetylcysteine) | Clinical improvement |
| **Valproic acid** | Mod. dialyzable | Level > 1,300 mg/L + shock/cerebral edema [Strong]; > 900 mg/L; pH <= 7.1 | L-carnitine | Clinical improvement OR level 50-100 mg/L |
| **Carbamazepine** | -- | Refractory seizures or life-threatening dysrhythmias [Strong] | None specific | Clinical improvement OR level < 10 mg/L |
| **Phenytoin** | -- | Severe: prolonged coma or ataxia [Conditional] | None specific | Clinical improvement |
| **Barbiturates** | -- | Severe long-acting: prolonged coma [Strong], refractory shock [Strong] | None specific | Clinical improvement |

## NOT Dialyzable (Exam Distractor List)

These toxins are NOT effectively removed by dialysis (high protein binding, high Vd, or lipophilic):
- **Opioids** (high Vd, protein-bound) -- treat with naloxone
- **Tricyclic antidepressants** (high Vd, protein-bound) -- treat with NaHCO3 for QRS widening
- **Benzodiazepines** (high protein binding, high Vd) -- treat with flumazenil (caution in seizure patients)
- **Carbon monoxide** -- treat with 100% O2 or hyperbaric O2
- **Cyanide** -- treat with hydroxocobalamin or nitrite/thiosulfate
- **Lead** -- treat with chelation (succimer, CaEDTA)
- **Arsenic** (chronic) -- chelation
- **Hydrocarbons** -- supportive care
- **Anticholinergics** -- physostigmine
- **Digoxin** -- treat with digoxin-specific Fab fragments (NOT dialyzable, but Fab-digoxin complex is eventually renally cleared)

**Exam pearl:** If a question asks "which of these toxins is best treated with dialysis?" and the options include an opioid, TCA, benzodiazepine, lithium, and digoxin -- the answer is LITHIUM. It is the most dialyzable common drug (low MW, zero protein binding, low Vd, water soluble).

## Toxic Alcohol Poisoning -- Detailed Algorithm

### Ethylene Glycol
- Metabolized to glycolaldehyde → glycolate → oxalate
- Classic findings: AG metabolic acidosis + elevated osmolal gap + calcium oxalate crystals in urine
- Treatment: fomepizole (blocks ADH) > ethanol; HD for severe cases
- **Exam pearl:** Calcium oxalate crystals = pathognomonic for ethylene glycol. Envelope-shaped (monohydrate) or needle-shaped (dihydrate).

### Methanol
- Metabolized to formaldehyde → formic acid
- Classic findings: AG metabolic acidosis + elevated osmolal gap + **visual disturbances** (optic disc hyperemia, blindness)
- Treatment: fomepizole > ethanol; HD for severe cases
- **Exam pearl:** Visual symptoms differentiate methanol from ethylene glycol. "The patient drank from an unlabeled bottle and now can't see" = methanol.

### Key Differences

| Feature | Ethylene glycol | Methanol |
|---|---|---|
| Source | Antifreeze | Windshield washer fluid |
| Toxic metabolite | Oxalate, glycolate | Formate |
| Unique finding | Ca oxalate crystals, renal failure | Visual loss, optic disc changes |
| AG acidosis | Yes | Yes |
| Osmolal gap | Elevated (early) | Elevated (early) |
| HD clearance | ~200 mL/min | ~208 mL/min |
| Stop HD criterion | AG < 18 | Level < 200 mg/L + improvement |

## Rebound Phenomenon

After ECTR cessation, plasma toxin concentration may RISE as drug redistributes from tissue compartments to blood. This does NOT necessarily mean increased toxicity.

Management:
- Extend dialysis sessions > 4 hours
- Repeat sessions (especially for lithium, methanol)
- Consider continuous therapy for substances with large Vd

**Exam pearl:** Lithium is the classic example of rebound. Li has low Vd but slow tissue-to-plasma equilibration. Always check post-dialysis rebound level 6-8 hours after stopping HD. May need multiple sessions.

## High-Yield Exam Scenarios

1. **Patient with AG metabolic acidosis, osmolal gap 35, calcium oxalate crystals in urine:** Ethylene glycol poisoning. Start fomepizole immediately. Add HD if severe (AG > 27, coma, seizures, AKI, concentration > 50 mmol/L with ethanol or > 10 mmol/L without antidote).

2. **Bipolar patient on lithium, SCr rising, Li level 5.5 mEq/L, confused:** Lithium toxicity with renal impairment. Provide ECTR [Strong]. IHD preferred. Stop when Li < 1 mEq/L or clinical improvement. Check rebound level 6-8h later.

3. **Aspirin overdose, level 110 mg/dL, altered mental status, pH 7.25:** Salicylate poisoning meeting ECTR criteria (level > 100 mg/dL [Strong]; AMS [Strong]). Start HD immediately in addition to urinary alkalinization.

4. **Metformin-associated lactic acidosis, lactate 25 mmol/L, pH 6.95:** ECTR indicated [Strong] (lactate > 20, pH <= 7). Use IHD with bicarbonate buffer. Continue until lactate < 3 mmol/L + pH > 7.35.

5. **Which of these is NOT dialyzable: lithium, ethylene glycol, amitriptyline, methanol, salicylate?** Amitriptyline (TCA) -- high Vd, highly protein-bound, lipophilic. All others are dialyzable.

6. **Patient on valproic acid, level 1,500 mg/L, in coma with cerebral edema:** ECTR indicated [Strong] (level > 1,300 + severe poisoning). HD preferred. Stop when clinical improvement or level 50-100 mg/L.
