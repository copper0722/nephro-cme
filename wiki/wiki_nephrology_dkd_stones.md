# Diabetic Kidney Disease and Nephrolithiasis

## PART 1: Diabetic Kidney Disease (DKD)

### Exam Logic Summary

DKD is the leading cause of ESKD globally. The exam tests: (1) KDIGO pharmacotherapy cascade (metformin → SGLT2i → GLP-1RA → finerenone), (2) landmark trial recall (CREDENCE, DAPA-CKD, EMPA-KIDNEY, FIDELIO-DKD), (3) DKD staging (Mogensen stages for T1DM), (4) pathology classification (Class I-IV), (5) when to suspect non-DKD in a diabetic patient, (6) screening intervals. Common distractor: assuming all kidney disease in diabetics is DKD (active sediment, rapid decline, absence of retinopathy in T1DM → consider alternative diagnosis).

**Textbook ref:** Brenner Ch. 39 (Diabetic Nephropathy), KDIGO 2024 CKD Guideline, ADA Standards of Care 2025

---

### Definition and Epidemiology

- DKD = kidney disease attributable to diabetes mellitus, characterized by albuminuria and/or progressive loss of kidney function
- Prevalence: 20-40% of diabetic patients
  - T1DM: 25-40% prevalence; kidney failure at 20-30 years post-diagnosis in 4-17%
  - T2DM: 5-40% prevalence at diagnosis; additional 30-40% develop it within 10 years
- DKD = #1 cause of ESKD in most countries
- ~50% of adults with T2DM and reduced GFR (< 60) have normoalbuminuria → DKD can exist WITHOUT albuminuria

### Presumptive Diagnosis (Clinical)

Consider DKD presumptively in patients with:
- Diabetes > 5 years (T1DM) or any duration (T2DM)
- Albuminuria and/or reduced eGFR
- Absence of active sediment, hematuria, or signs of other causes
- Gradual progressive decline (not rapid)

Suspect alternative/additional cause if:
- T1DM duration < 5 years
- Active urinary sediment (RBC/WBC casts)
- Well-controlled glucose throughout
- Rapidly decreasing eGFR
- Rapidly increasing or nephrotic-range proteinuria (UACR > 300 mg/g rapidly)
- Absence of retinopathy (in T1DM -- retinopathy usually precedes nephropathy)

**Exam pearl:** In T1DM, diabetic retinopathy almost always precedes DKD. A T1DM patient with nephropathy but NO retinopathy → kidney biopsy to rule out other glomerular disease. In T2DM, retinopathy is less reliable as a marker.

### Staging (Mogensen Classification for T1DM)

| Stage | Description | GFR | Albuminuria | Timing |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Hyperfiltration | ↑↑ | Normal | At diagnosis |
| 2 | Silent/latent | Normal | Normal | 2-5 years |
| 3 | Incipient nephropathy | Normal | Mild-moderate (30-300 mg/day) | 5-15 years |
| 4 | Overt nephropathy | ↓ Declining | Severe (> 300 mg/day) | 10-15 years |
| 5 | Kidney failure | < 15 | Severe | 20-30 years |

### Pathology Classification (Tervaert 2010)

| Class | Description |
|---|---|
| I | Mild or nonspecific light microscopy + GBM thickening on EM |
| IIa | Mild mesangial expansion (< 25% of mesangium) |
| IIb | Severe mesangial expansion (> 25% of mesangium) |
| III | Nodular sclerosis (Kimmelstiel-Wilson lesion) -- at least 1 convincing nodule |
| IV | Advanced diabetic glomerulosclerosis (> 50% global glomerulosclerosis) |

**Exam pearl:** Kimmelstiel-Wilson nodules = pathognomonic for DKD. But biopsy is NOT routinely needed. Reserve for uncertain diagnosis.

### Management -- KDIGO Pharmacotherapy Cascade (MUST MEMORIZE)

```
Step 1: Lifestyle + Metformin (eGFR >= 30)
Step 2: + SGLT2 inhibitor (eGFR >= 20)
Step 3: + GLP-1 receptor agonist (if glycemic target not met)
Step 4: + Nonsteroidal MRA (finerenone) if albuminuria persists
Parallel: ACEi/ARB (maximally tolerated dose) + BP < 130/80 + Statin
```

### Blood Pressure

- Target < 130/80 mmHg in all CKD + diabetes [Strong Recommendation]
- ACEi or ARB titrated to max dose in:
  - T1DM or T2DM with UACR >= 300 and/or eGFR < 60 [Strong Recommendation]
  - T2DM with UACR 30-299 [Strong Recommendation]
- Do NOT use ACEi/ARB in patients without HTN with normal UACR (< 30) and normal eGFR [Strong Recommendation]
- Avoid ACEi/ARB/MRA/renin inhibitors in pregnancy [ADA Grade A]

### SGLT2 Inhibitors in DKD

- Offer SGLT2i for kidney or CV benefit in T2DM + CKD with eGFR >= 20 [Strong Recommendation]
- Continue even if eGFR falls below 20 once established on therapy (especially if UACR > 300)

| Trial | Drug | N | Population | Primary Outcome | Key Result |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **CREDENCE** | Canagliflozin | 4,401 | T2DM, eGFR 30-90, UACR 300-5000 | Composite renal (ESKD, doubling Cr, renal/CV death) | 30% ↓ composite (HR 0.70) |
| **DAPA-CKD** | Dapagliflozin | 4,304 | CKD (eGFR 25-75, UACR 200-5000), 67% DM | Sustained ↓ eGFR >= 50%, ESKD, renal/CV death | 39% ↓ (HR 0.61); benefit REGARDLESS of diabetes |
| **EMPA-KIDNEY** | Empagliflozin | 6,609 | CKD (eGFR 20-45 or 45-90 + UACR >= 200) | CKD progression or CV death | 28% ↓ (HR 0.72) |

**Exam pearl:** DAPA-CKD showed SGLT2i benefit in CKD patients WITHOUT diabetes (first to demonstrate this). EMPA-KIDNEY extended evidence to lower eGFR range (20-45) and lower albuminuria. These trials changed practice from DM-only to ALL CKD.

### Metformin

- eGFR >= 30: continue (reduce dose if eGFR 30-45)
- eGFR < 30: stop
- Maximum dose halved if eGFR < 45

### GLP-1 Receptor Agonists

- Offer long-acting GLP-1RA if glycemic target not met on metformin + SGLT2i [Strong Recommendation]
- Semaglutide: expanded FDA approval for reducing sustained eGFR decline in T2DM + CKD

### Nonsteroidal MRA (Finerenone)

- Offer finerenone in T2DM + CKD with eGFR >= 25, normal K⁺, continued albuminuria (UACR >= 30) despite max ACEi/ARB [Strong Recommendation]

| Trial | N | Population | Key Result |
|---|---|---|---|
| **FIDELIO-DKD** | 5,734 | T2DM, UACR 30-300 + eGFR 25-60 or UACR 300-5000 + eGFR 25-75 | 18% ↓ composite kidney outcome (HR 0.82) |
| **FIGARO-DKD** | 7,437 | T2DM, wider CKD range | 13% ↓ composite CV outcome (HR 0.87) |

- Monitor K⁺: finerenone raises K⁺ less than spironolactone but still requires monitoring
- Use steroidal MRA (spironolactone/eplerenone) instead of nonsteroidal MRA for resistant HTN only [Conditional Recommendation]

### Glycemic Control

- HbA1c target: individualized, generally < 6.5-7% if not at risk of hypoglycemia; < 8% if comorbidities/limited life expectancy [Strong Recommendation]
- HbA1c may be inaccurate in advanced CKD (shortened RBC lifespan, ESA use) → use glycated albumin or fructosamine
- Metformin: adjust for eGFR as above
- Insulin: dose ↓ needed as GFR declines (↓ renal insulin clearance → hypoglycemia risk)

### Dietary

- Protein: 0.8 g/kg/day for non-dialysis CKD [Conditional Recommendation]; 1.0-1.2 g/kg/day on dialysis
- Sodium: < 2-2.3 g/day [Conditional Recommendation]
- Weight loss >= 5-7% if overweight/obese [Strong Recommendation]
- Consider metabolic surgery if BMI >= 30 (27.5 for Asian descent) [Strong Recommendation]

### Statin Therapy

- Recommend statin for ALL patients with DKD for atherosclerotic CVD risk reduction [Strong Recommendation]

## PART 2: Nephrolithiasis in Adults

### Exam Logic Summary

Nephrolithiasis tests: (1) stone composition types and their clinical associations, (2) 24-hour urine metabolic workup interpretation, (3) imaging approach (non-contrast CT gold standard), (4) management by stone size/location, (5) prevention strategies by stone type. Common distractor: assuming all stones are calcium oxalate (must check composition); treating uric acid stones with allopurinol alone (alkalinization is primary therapy).

**Textbook ref:** Brenner Ch. 41 (Nephrolithiasis), EAU 2024 Urolithiasis Guidelines

---

### Epidemiology

- Prevalence: 1-20% globally; ~10% in US (10.9% male, 9.5% female, NHANES 2017-2018)
- Incidence increasing worldwide
- Recurrence: ~50% have single recurrence; >10% are highly recurrent
- Male > female (narrowing gap)

### Stone Composition (MUST MEMORIZE)

| Stone Type | Frequency | X-ray | Urine pH | Key Associations |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Calcium oxalate** | 70-80% | Radiopaque | Any | Hypercalciuria, hyperoxaluria, hypocitraturia |
| **Calcium phosphate** | 5-10% (often mixed with CaOx) | Radiopaque | Alkaline (> 6.5) | Distal RTA, primary hyperparathyroidism |
| **Uric acid** | 5-10% | **Radiolucent** | < 5.5 (acidic) | Gout, metabolic syndrome, chronic diarrhea, myeloproliferative disorders |
| **Struvite** (MgNH4PO4) | 5-15% | Poorly radiopaque | Alkaline (> 7.0) | UTI with urease-producing bacteria (Proteus), staghorn calculi |
| **Cystine** | 1% | Poorly radiopaque | Acidic | Cystinuria (autosomal recessive), hexagonal crystals on UA |
| **Drug-induced** | Rare | Variable | Variable | Indinavir, acyclovir, triamterene, sulfadiazine |

**Exam pearl:** Radiolucent stone on CT = think uric acid first. Hexagonal crystals on UA = pathognomonic for cystine. Staghorn calculus = think struvite (infection stone). Calcium phosphate stones in young patient with NAGMA = think distal RTA.

### Evaluation

**Imaging:**
- Initial: ultrasound for flank pain [Strong Recommendation]
- Confirmatory: non-contrast CT (gold standard for stone detection) [Strong Recommendation]
- Pregnant patients: ultrasound → MRI → low-dose CT (last resort) [Strong Recommendation]
- Findings suggesting structural abnormality: staghorn calculus, medullary nephrocalcinosis (medullary sponge kidney)

**Blood tests [Strong Recommendation]:**
- Metabolic panel: Ca²⁺ (ionized or corrected), Na⁺, K⁺, HCO3⁻, Cl⁻, creatinine, BUN, phosphorus, Mg²⁺
- Uric acid
- CBC and CRP (if intervention planned)
- Coagulation tests (if procedure planned)
- iPTH and vitamin D [Conditional Recommendation]

**Stone analysis:** Perform composition analysis on ALL first stones [Strong Recommendation]

**24-Hour Urine Collection:**
- Consider within 6 months of treatment initiation [Conditional Recommendation]
- Annual 24-hour urine to assess adherence and metabolic response [Conditional Recommendation]
- Measure: volume, Ca²⁺, oxalate, citrate, uric acid, Na⁺, K⁺, Mg²⁺, phosphorus, pH, creatinine, cystine (if suspected)

### 24-Hour Urine Interpretation

| Finding | Normal | Stone Risk If... | Associated Stone |
|---|---|---|---|
| Volume | > 2 L/day | < 2 L | All types |
| Calcium | < 250 mg/day (F), < 300 (M) | Elevated | CaOx, CaP |
| Oxalate | < 40 mg/day | Elevated | CaOx |
| Citrate | > 320 mg/day | Low (< 320) | CaOx, CaP |
| Uric acid | < 800 mg/day (M), < 750 (F) | Elevated | Uric acid, CaOx |
| Sodium | < 150 mEq/day | Elevated | All (↑ Na → ↑ Ca excretion) |
| pH | 5.5-7.0 | < 5.5 or > 7.0 | < 5.5 → uric acid; > 7.0 → CaP/struvite |
| Cystine | < 75 mg/day | > 250 mg/day | Cystine |

### Management by Stone Size and Location

**Ureteral stones:**
- <= 10 mm, no complications: observation + MET (medical expulsive therapy) [Strong Recommendation]
  - Alpha-blocker (tamsulosin) for distal ureteral stones 5-10 mm [Strong Recommendation]
  - Spontaneous passage rate: < 5 mm = ~68%, 5-10 mm = ~47%
- > 10 mm or failed observation: URS (ureteroscopy) [Strong Recommendation]
- SWL: option for proximal/mid-ureteral stones < 10 mm [Strong Recommendation]

**Kidney stones:**
- Asymptomatic calyceal stone: active surveillance [Conditional Recommendation]
- Symptomatic or growing: intervention
- Stone burden > 20 mm: PCNL first-line [Strong Recommendation], NOT SWL
- Stone 10-20 mm: SWL or URS, consider location (lower pole → URS preferred over SWL)
- Stone < 10 mm: SWL or URS

### Prevention by Stone Type (HIGH-YIELD)

**General (ALL stone types):**
- Fluid intake to produce > 2.5 L urine/day [Strong Recommendation]
- Dietary: increase fruits/vegetables, moderate protein, limit Na < 2.3 g/day
- Normal dietary calcium (1000-1200 mg/day from food, NOT supplements)

**Calcium oxalate:**
- Thiazide diuretic (hydrochlorothiazide 25-50 mg or chlorthalidone 25 mg) for hypercalciuria
- Potassium citrate for hypocitraturia (30-60 mEq/day)
- Reduce dietary oxalate (spinach, rhubarb, nuts, chocolate, tea)
- Allopurinol if hyperuricosuria (uric acid promotes CaOx crystallization)

**Uric acid:**
- PRIMARY therapy = urinary alkalinization with potassium citrate (target urine pH 6.0-6.5)
- Allopurinol (300 mg/day) if serum uric acid elevated or high urine uric acid
- Dissolution therapy possible (uric acid stones dissolve in alkaline urine)

**Struvite:**
- Complete surgical removal (including fragments) -- no medical dissolution effective
- Treat underlying UTI (urease-producing organisms)
- Acetohydroxamic acid (urease inhibitor) for prevention if surgery incomplete

**Cystine:**
- High fluid intake (> 3 L urine/day)
- Urinary alkalinization (K citrate, target pH > 7.0)
- Tiopronin or D-penicillamine (thiol-binding agents) for refractory cases

**Exam pearl:** Uric acid stones are the ONLY common stones that can be dissolved medically (alkalinization). This is a frequent exam question. Struvite stones CANNOT be dissolved -- surgical removal is essential. Thiazides work for calcium stones by reducing urinary calcium excretion.

### Nephrocalcinosis

- Calcium deposition within renal parenchyma (interstitial or tubular)
- Metabolic causes: hypercalcemia (any cause), primary hyperparathyroidism, distal RTA, primary hyperoxaluria
- Genetic causes: Bartter syndrome, ARPKD, Dent disease, FHHNC, Williams-Beuren syndrome
- Distinguished from nephrolithiasis: nephrocalcinosis = parenchymal deposition; nephrolithiasis = collecting system stones

### Risk Factors for Stone Recurrence

- Early onset (pediatric)
- Familial stone disease
- Short interval since last stone
- Brushite-containing or uric acid/urate-containing stones
- Infection stones
- Anatomic abnormalities (medullary sponge kidney, horseshoe kidney, UPJ obstruction)

## High-Yield Exam Scenarios

### DKD Scenarios

1. **T2DM, eGFR 35, UACR 800, on max lisinopril**: Add SGLT2i (dapagliflozin/empagliflozin). If albuminuria persists → add finerenone. If glycemia not at target → add GLP-1RA. Statin for all.

2. **T1DM, 8 years duration, new albuminuria, NO retinopathy**: Red flag. In T1DM, retinopathy typically precedes nephropathy. Biopsy to rule out IgA nephropathy, FSGS, or membranous.

3. **DKD patient with eGFR 22, on empagliflozin**: Continue SGLT2i (can continue below 20 once established). Expected initial eGFR dip of 3-5 mL/min (hemodynamic, reversible).

4. **Diabetic on insulin with eGFR declining from 30 to 20**: Reduce insulin dose (renal insulin clearance decreases → hypoglycemia risk). HbA1c may be falsely low or high (use glycated albumin).

### Nephrolithiasis Scenarios

5. **7 mm distal ureteral stone, mild pain, no infection**: Offer MET (tamsulosin 0.4 mg daily) + observation for 4-6 weeks. If no passage → URS.

6. **Radiolucent stone on CT in patient with gout**: Uric acid stone. Start potassium citrate to alkalinize urine (target pH 6.0-6.5). Add allopurinol if hyperuricemia. Stone may dissolve with alkalinization alone.

7. **Staghorn calculus with UTI (Proteus mirabilis)**: Struvite/infection stone. Treat UTI first, then PCNL for complete stone removal. Incomplete removal → recurrence. Acetohydroxamic acid for prevention.

8. **Young patient with bilateral calcium phosphate stones, NAGMA, urine pH 6.8**: Suspect distal RTA (Type I). Check urine pH (> 5.5 despite acidosis), serum K⁺ (low). Treat with potassium citrate.

9. **Recurrent CaOx stone former, 24h urine: Ca 380 mg, citrate 180 mg, volume 1.2 L**: Multiple abnormalities. (a) ↑ fluid to achieve > 2.5L urine. (b) Thiazide for hypercalciuria. (c) K-citrate for hypocitraturia. (d) Reduce sodium and animal protein.

10. **First kidney stone, 4 mm, passed spontaneously**: Send stone for composition analysis [Strong Recommendation]. Basic metabolic panel. If calcium stone → 24h urine collection within 6 months. General prevention: fluids > 2.5 L urine/day.
