# Non-Infectious Complications of Pediatric Peritoneal Dialysis (PD)

## Exam Logic
*   **The "Mechanical vs. Technique" Distinction:** Exams often test the ability to differentiate between **mechanical failures** (catheter-related/anatomical) and **technique-related failures** (membrane biology). 
*   **Outflow vs. Inflow Obstruction:** 
    *   **Outflow failure** is most commonly linked to **constipation** (↑ intra-abdominal pressure/↓ peristalsis) or catheter migration.
    *   **Inflow failure** is typically due to **fibrin clots** or kinking.
*   **The "14-Day Rule":** A high-yield point is the **break-in period**. Starting PD <14 days post-insertion significantly ↑ risk of **peri-catheter leaks**.
*   **Omentectomy Logic:** Omentectomy is performed to prevent the omentum from wrapping around the catheter (extraluminal obstruction), which is a common cause of late-stage failure.
*   **Common Distractors:** Confusing **Ultrafiltration Failure (UFF)** with **Mechanical Obstruction**. UFF is a membrane property (↑ permeability/↓ osmotic gradient), whereas obstruction is a physical blockage.

## Mechanical Complications

### 1. PD Fluid Flow Obstruction
*   **Outflow Failure:** Incomplete drainage. 
    *   **Causes:** Constipation (most common), catheter migration, kinking.
    *   **Risk Factors:** Age >1 year, weight >10 kg.
*   **Inflow Failure:** Fluid cannot enter cavity.
    *   **Causes:** Intraluminal **fibrin** or blood clots, displacement.
*   **Management:**
    *   **Diagnostics:** Abdominal X-ray (position), Ultrasound (malfunction).
    *   **Interventions:** Saline flushing $\rightarrow$ **tPA (tissue plasminogen activator)** for fibrin $\rightarrow$ Guidewire manipulation $\rightarrow$ Surgical revision.

### 2. Catheter Selection & Placement
*   **Gold Standard:** **Double-cuff Tenckhof catheter** (for children >3 kg).
*   **Surgical Optimization:**
    *   **Omentectomy:** ↓ extraluminal obstruction.
    *   **Laparoscopic Technique:** Rectus sheath tunneling and suture fixation preferred.
    *   **Configuration:** Straight tunnels are generally preferred over "swan neck" (which may ↑ revision odds).

### 3. Fluid Leakage
*   **Types:** 
    *   **Early:** <30 days post-op (often peri-catheter).
    *   **Late:** Subtle signs (edema, weight gain, UFF).
*   **Risk Factors:** High **Intra-abdominal Pressure (IPP)**, abdominal wall weakness, young age.
*   **Prevention:**
    *   **Break-in Period:** Wait $\ge$ 14 days before initiating PD.
    *   **Volume Management:** Start with low fill volumes; maintain IPP <18 cm H₂O.

### 4. Other Mechanical Issues
*   **Hernias:** Result from chronic ↑ IPP.
*   **Pleuro-peritoneal Leaks:** Fluid migrates to pleural/pericardial space via diaphragmatic defects. Risk ↑ in patients with **low albumin** or prior thoracic surgery.

## Technique-Related & Other Complications

### 1. Peritoneal Membrane Failure
*   **Ultrafiltration Failure (UFF):** Loss of ability to remove fluid.
*   **Drivers:** Prolonged PD duration, high **glucose load** (osmotic stress), and recurrent peritonitis.
*   **Encapsulating Peritoneal Sclerosis (EPS):** Rare, severe fibrotic process encasing bowel loops. High morbidity/mortality.

### 2. Metabolic & Systemic Issues
*   **Pancreatitis:** Linked to hypercalcemia or hyperglycemia.
*   **Hemoperitoneum:** Blood in cavity; causes include catheter trauma, menstruation, or coagulation disorders.
*   **Ischemic Colitis/NEC:** Result of vascular compromise or severe intraperitoneal inflammation.
*   **Electrolyte Shifts:** Risk of fluid overload or electrolyte imbalance due to loss of residual renal function (RRF) and sodium/potassium sieving.
