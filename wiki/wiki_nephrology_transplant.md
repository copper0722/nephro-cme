# Kidney Transplantation -- Rejection, Immunosuppression, and Allograft Dysfunction

## Exam Logic Summary

Transplant is a discrete Section 04 topic. The exam tests: (1) rejection classification (hyperacute/acute/chronic, ABMR vs TCMR), (2) Banff criteria, (3) induction vs maintenance immunosuppression selection, (4) CNI toxicity vs rejection on biopsy, (5) BK virus nephropathy management, (6) DSA significance and crossmatch interpretation, (7) time-based differential of graft dysfunction. Common distractors: confusing CNI toxicity with rejection, not knowing that BKV treatment = REDUCE immunosuppression (not increase).

**Textbook ref:** Brenner Ch. 68-70 (Transplant), Daugirdas Ch. 36 (Transplant), KDIGO 2009 Transplant Guidelines

## Rejection Classification

### By Timing

| Type | Onset | Mechanism | Incidence |
|---|---|---|---|
| Hyperacute | Minutes to hours | Preformed DSA → complement → cortical necrosis/thrombosis | Very rare (modern crossmatching) |
| Acute | Days to months | ABMR or TCMR | < 15% at 1 year |
| Chronic | Months to years | Slow immune-mediated injury → IF/TA, transplant glomerulopathy | Leading cause of late graft loss |

### By Mechanism

| Type | Mechanism | Key biopsy finding | Key lab finding |
|---|---|---|---|
| **ABMR** (antibody-mediated) | DSA → complement → endothelial injury | C4d deposition, microvascular inflammation (glomerulitis, peritubular capillaritis) | DSA positive |
| **TCMR** (T cell-mediated) | T cells infiltrate graft → tubulitis, arteritis | Tubulitis +/- intimal arteritis | DSA may be negative |
| **Mixed** | Both ABMR + TCMR features | Both patterns | Variable |
| **Subclinical** | Histological changes without clinical dysfunction | Found on protocol biopsy | Stable SCr |
| **Borderline** | Suspicious but insufficient for rejection diagnosis | Minimal changes | May need follow-up biopsy |

### Banff 2015 Criteria (MUST MEMORIZE)

**ABMR requires ALL 3:**
1. Histological evidence of acute or chronic tissue injury
2. Evidence of current/recent antibody-endothelium interaction (C4d+, microvascular inflammation)
3. Donor-specific antibodies (DSA)

**TCMR requires >= 1:**
- Tubulitis (T cells invading tubular epithelium)
- Intimal arteritis (T cells in vessel intima)

**Exam pearl:** C4d-negative ABMR exists. Microvascular injury (glomerulitis, peritubular capillaritis, TMA) in presence of DSA even without C4d staining = still ABMR.

## Donor-Specific Antibodies (DSA) -- Exam Critical

| DSA Feature | Clinical Significance |
|---|---|
| Preformed DSA (pre-transplant) | RR 1.98 for ABMR; RR 1.76 for graft failure (JASN 2012) |
| De novo DSA (post-transplant) | 15% develop by mean 4.6 years; 10-year graft survival 57% vs 98% |
| DSA with C1q binding capacity | 48% ABMR rate (vs 16% without C1q); 5-year graft survival 54% (NEJM 2013) |
| DSA by solid phase assay (SPA) only | More predictive than CDC or flow cytometry crossmatch |

**Exam pearl:** DSA with complement-fixing ability (C1q binding) is FAR more pathogenic than non-complement-fixing DSA. This is the highest-yield transplant immunology fact.

### Crossmatch Types

| Test | Detects | Sensitivity | Clinical use |
|---|---|---|---|
| CDC crossmatch | Complement-fixing antibodies | Low | Hyperacute rejection risk |
| Flow cytometry crossmatch | All antibodies (including non-complement-fixing) | High | Better prediction of ABMR |
| Solid phase assay (Luminex) | HLA-specific antibodies | Highest | DSA identification, virtual crossmatch |

## Immunosuppression Protocol

### Induction (Perioperative)

| Agent | Type | Use | Key points |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Basiliximab** (Simulect) | Anti-IL-2R-alpha mAb | Low immunologic risk [KDIGO 1B] | 20 mg IV x 2 doses (pre-transplant + day 4) |
| **Rabbit ATG** (Thymoglobulin) | Polyclonal anti-T cell | High immunologic risk [KDIGO 2B] | 1.5 mg/kg IV daily x 7-14 days |
| **Alemtuzumab** (Campath) | Anti-CD52 mAb | Off-label | 30 mg IV single dose; intense lymphodepletion; NOT FDA-approved for transplant |

**Exam pearl:** IL-2Ra (basiliximab) vs ATG: ATG reduces BPAR more effectively (11.8% vs 20.9%, NNT 11) but ATG has more infections and higher malignancy risk (RR 0.25 favoring IL-2Ra). For HIGH-RISK patients, ATG is preferred. For LOW-RISK, basiliximab is sufficient.

### Risk Stratification for Induction Selection

| Factor | Low Risk | High Risk |
|---|---|---|
| HLA mismatch | Zero | Increased |
| Donor | Living | Deceased, older |
| PRA | Low | High (> 20%) |
| DSA | Absent | Present |
| Prior transplant | First | Retransplant |
| Ethnicity | Non-African American | African American |
| Cold ischemia | Short | Long |

### Maintenance Immunosuppression

**Standard triple therapy [KDIGO 1B]:**
1. **CNI** (tacrolimus preferred [KDIGO 2A]) + 
2. **Antiproliferative** (MMF preferred [KDIGO 2B]) +
3. **Corticosteroid** (may withdraw in low-risk with induction [KDIGO 2B])

### CNI Target Levels

**Tacrolimus (C0 trough):**

| Time post-transplant | No induction | With ATG induction | With mTORi |
|---|---|---|---|
| 0-3 months | 10-15 ng/mL | 5-10 ng/mL | 3-7 ng/mL |
| 3-12 months | 5-15 ng/mL | 5-10 ng/mL | 3-7 ng/mL |
| > 12 months | 5-10 ng/mL | 5-10 ng/mL | 3-7 ng/mL |

**Exam pearl:** Know that tacrolimus target trough is LOWER when combined with ATG induction (because ATG provides more immunosuppression upfront) and LOWEST with mTORi maintenance.

### CNI Toxicity (Exam Favorite -- CNI Toxicity vs Rejection)

| Feature | CNI Toxicity | Acute Rejection |
|---|---|---|
| CNI trough level | Often elevated (but can be at target) | Usually at target or low |
| Biopsy | **Isometric vacuolization** of tubular cells, arteriolar hyalinosis, TMA | Tubulitis, interstitial infiltrate (TCMR); C4d+, microvascular inflammation (ABMR) |
| Urine | May be bland | May have proteinuria |
| Management | Reduce CNI dose | Increase immunosuppression |
| Key pearl | Arteriolar hyalinosis = CHRONIC CNI toxicity (vs arteritis in rejection) | Intimal arteritis = vascular rejection |

**Exam pearl:** Isometric vacuolization of proximal tubular cells on biopsy = pathognomonic for CNI toxicity. Arteriolar hyalinosis (nodular deposits in afferent arterioles) = chronic CNI nephrotoxicity.

### CNI Adverse Effects

| Effect | Cyclosporine | Tacrolimus |
|---|---|---|
| Nephrotoxicity | Both (afferent arteriolar vasoconstriction) | Both |
| Hypertension | Both | Both |
| Hyperkalemia | Both | Both |
| Hypomagnesemia | Both | Both |
| PTDM | Less common | **More common** |
| Gingival hyperplasia | **Yes** | No |
| Hirsutism | **Yes** | Alopecia |
| Neurotoxicity | Both | Both (more tremor) |
| Hyperlipidemia | Both | Both |

### Drug Interactions with CNIs (CYP3A4)

| Increase CNI levels (DANGER) | Decrease CNI levels |
|---|---|
| Macrolides (clarithromycin, erythromycin) | Rifampin |
| Azole antifungals (ketoconazole, fluconazole) | Phenytoin, carbamazepine, phenobarbital |
| Nondihydropyridine CCBs (diltiazem, verapamil) | St. John's wort |
| Protease inhibitors | |
| Grapefruit juice | |

### Other Maintenance Agents

| Agent | Mechanism | Key exam points |
|---|---|---|
| **Belatacept** (Nulojix) | CD80/86 blocker → blocks CD28 costimulation | CNI-free; ONLY for EBV-seropositive patients (PTLD risk if EBV-negative); better long-term GFR |
| **Sirolimus/Everolimus** (mTORi) | Inhibit mTOR → block IL-2-mediated proliferation | Start only after wounds healed; AEs: hyperlipidemia, proteinuria, impaired wound healing, oral ulcers, pneumonitis |
| **MMF** | IMPDH inhibitor → blocks purine synthesis in lymphocytes | AEs: GI (diarrhea), leukopenia; preferred over azathioprine |
| **Azathioprine** | 6-MP prodrug | NEVER combine with allopurinol (severe myelosuppression) |

## Treatment of Rejection

### Acute TCMR
1. **Corticosteroids = first-line** [Strong]: methylprednisolone 250-500 mg IV x 3 days
2. Add/restore prednisone maintenance [Conditional]
3. If grade >= 2A or steroid-resistant: **lymphocyte-depleting antibodies (ATG)** [Conditional]
4. Switch azathioprine to MMF if applicable [Conditional]

### Acute ABMR
- Treat with >= 1 of the following (+/- steroids) [Conditional]:
  - **Plasma exchange/plasmapheresis** (remove circulating DSA)
  - **IVIG** (neutralize DSA + immunomodulate)
  - **Rituximab** (anti-CD20; deplete B cells)
  - Lymphocyte-depleting antibody

### Chronic Allograft Injury
- Biopsy all patients with declining GFR [Strong]
- If CNI toxicity on biopsy: reduce/withdraw/replace CNI [Conditional]
- Consider CNI → mTORi if eGFR > 40 mL/min + proteinuria < 500 mg/g [Conditional]

## BK Virus Nephropathy (Exam High-Yield)

| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| Timing | Usually first year post-transplant |
| Presentation | Asymptomatic; rising SCr |
| Viral load | Serum > 10,000 copies/mL suggests active infection |
| Biopsy | Intranuclear inclusions in tubular cells (pathognomonic); tubulointerstitial nephritis |
| Key point | Can mimic rejection on biopsy → biopsy MUST include SV40 staining |
| Treatment | **REDUCE immunosuppression** (not increase!) |
| Specific options | Eliminate/substitute MMF with leflunomide or azathioprine; low-dose cidofovir; IVIG |
| Late complication | Ureteral stenosis |

**Exam pearl:** BKV nephropathy treatment = REDUCE immunosuppression. This is the OPPOSITE of rejection treatment. The exam tests this distinction explicitly. A transplant patient with rising Cr and intranuclear inclusions on biopsy who is treated with MORE immunosuppression (pulse steroids, ATG) = WRONG answer.

## Time-Based Differential of Graft Dysfunction

### Within First Week
- Hyperacute rejection (minutes; graft becomes flaccid/mottled)
- Renal artery thrombosis (1-3%; sudden anuria; urgent thrombectomy rarely salvages → nephrectomy)
- Renal vein thrombosis (pain + engorgement; better prognosis than arterial)
- Delayed graft function (ATN from ischemia-reperfusion; most common cause; usually resolves)

### Early (< 6 Months)
- Acute rejection (TCMR or ABMR)
- BK virus nephropathy
- CNI toxicity
- Urinary leak (drain Cr > blood Cr)
- Collecting system obstruction

### Late (>= 6 Months)
- Chronic rejection (ABMR most common)
- BK virus nephropathy
- Transplant renal artery stenosis (refractory HTN)
- CNI toxicity (chronic: IF/TA, arteriolar hyalinosis)
- Recurrent or de novo glomerulonephritis (36.6% of death-censored graft failures)
- PTLD (EBV-associated)

## High-Yield Exam Scenarios

1. **Post-transplant day 1, graft is flaccid and mottled after unclamping:** Hyperacute rejection. Preformed DSA at high titer. Should have been caught on crossmatch. Immediate nephrectomy.

2. **6 months post-transplant, SCr rising, biopsy shows tubulitis + interstitial infiltrate, no C4d:** Acute TCMR. Treat with pulse methylprednisolone. If grade >= 2A or steroid-resistant, add ATG.

3. **1 year post-transplant, SCr rising, biopsy shows C4d+, peritubular capillaritis, DSA positive:** Acute ABMR. Treat with plasmapheresis + IVIG +/- rituximab.

4. **Rising SCr, biopsy shows isometric vacuolization of tubular cells, tacrolimus trough 18 ng/mL:** CNI toxicity. REDUCE tacrolimus dose. This is NOT rejection.

5. **8 months post-transplant, SCr rising, biopsy shows intranuclear inclusions, SV40 stain positive:** BK virus nephropathy. REDUCE immunosuppression. Switch MMF to leflunomide. Do NOT pulse steroids.

6. **Patient on tacrolimus started on clarithromycin for pneumonia, SCr rises over 1 week, tacrolimus trough 25 ng/mL:** Drug interaction. Clarithromycin inhibits CYP3A4 → tacrolimus accumulation → nephrotoxicity. Stop clarithromycin or use azithromycin instead (does not inhibit CYP3A4).

7. **Young patient with DSA detected by Luminex (SPA) but negative CDC crossmatch:** Proceed with transplant with caution. SPA-detected DSA increases ABMR risk (RR 1.98). Consider ATG induction + closer surveillance. CDC crossmatch negative means low hyperacute rejection risk.

## HLA System and Crossmatching (Exam Critical)

### HLA Classes

| Class | Location | Loci | Presents to | Function |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Class I** | All nucleated cells | HLA-A, HLA-B, HLA-C | CD8 T cells | Intracellular peptide presentation |
| **Class II** | APCs (B cells, dendritic, macrophages) | HLA-DR, HLA-DQ, HLA-DP | CD4 T cells | Extracellular peptide presentation |

- HLA genes on chromosome 6 (6p21); half inherited from each parent = haplotype
- > 28,000 alleles identified; > 18,000 proteins

### HLA Sensitization

Causes: pregnancy, blood transfusion, prior transplantation. Mismatched transplant produces strongest/broadest/longest-lasting sensitization.

- Sensitized patients: rapid rejection risk, reduced compatible donor pool, increased waitlist mortality
- Blood transfusion post-tx: RR 1.4 for de novo DSA; RR 1.41 for ABMR; RR 1.75 for graft loss
- 36.2% of candidates exposed to >= 1 sensitizing event had positive HLA antibodies

**Exam pearl:** A patient with multiple prior transplants and high cPRA (> 85%) = highly sensitized. Options: acceptable mismatch allocation (10-year graft survival 72.8% vs 62.4%), kidney paired donation, desensitization.

### HLA Matching Impact on Outcomes

| HLA Mismatches | HR for Allograft Failure (vs 0 mismatch) |
|---|---|
| 1 | 1.13 (1.06-1.21) |
| 2 | 1.29 (1.23-1.37) |
| 3 | 1.36 (1.30-1.43) |
| 4 | 1.48 (1.41-1.55) |
| 5 | 1.56 (1.49-1.64) |
| 6 | 1.64 (1.56-1.73) |

- HLA-DQ mismatch: independently increases acute rejection (aOR 1.13-1.14 for both deceased and living donor)
- In deceased donor allocation: HLA-A, HLA-B, HLA-DR matching prioritized

### Eplet Matching

- Eplets = small amino acid stretches (2-5 residues) defining epitope specificity
- > 20 eplet mismatches: aHR 2.16 for acute rejection
- Useful for highly sensitized candidates: identifies low-risk donor mismatches, expands donor pool

**Exam pearl:** Eplet matching is a newer concept going beyond traditional antigen-level matching. The exam may test the principle: shared epitopes between donor and recipient do NOT generate DSA against that shared epitope.

### Crossmatch Interpretation (MUST MEMORIZE)

| Test | Mechanism | Sensitivity | Key Point |
|---|---|---|---|
| **CDC** | Recipient serum + donor lymphocytes + complement → lysis if DSA present | Lowest | Positive = contraindication to transplant (hyperacute rejection risk) |
| **Flow cytometry** | Anti-human IgG detects DSA bound to donor lymphocytes | Higher | More sensitive; detects non-complement-fixing antibodies |
| **Solid-phase assay (Luminex SAB)** | Microbeads coated with single HLA antigens + recipient serum | Highest | Virtual crossmatch; identifies specific DSA; detects in 31% vs CDC 4.6% |

- **Virtual crossmatch** = combining recipient SAB antibody profile with donor HLA typing
- Cannot substitute physical crossmatch unless no anti-HLA antibodies detected
- C1q-binding SAB modification detects complement-fixing DSA (most pathogenic)

**Exam pearl:** Positive CDC crossmatch = absolute contraindication to transplant. Positive flow crossmatch with negative CDC = proceed with caution (increased ABMR risk but low hyperacute risk). Luminex-only DSA with negative CDC and flow = lowest risk but still monitor.

## Post-Transplant Infection Timeline (Exam High-Yield)

### Infection Risk by Time Period

| Period | Common Infections | Key Pathogens |
|---|---|---|
| **< 4 weeks** | Surgical site, donor-derived, hospital-acquired | C. difficile, Candida, wound infections, UTI, donor-derived viruses |
| **1-12 months** | Opportunistic infections (peak immunosuppression) | CMV, BK, PJP, Aspergillus, Listeria, Nocardia, Toxoplasma |
| **> 12 months (good graft)** | Community-acquired | Respiratory viruses, foodborne, Aspergillus, Cryptococcus |
| **> 12 months (poor graft)** | Recurring serious infections | PJP, Aspergillus, Cryptococcus, Listeria, Nocardia, JC virus (PML), NTM |

- 50% develop infection in first year; highest risk in first 3 months (57.6%)
- Most common organism overall: CMV (29.1%) and E. coli (24.5%)
- Most common infection site: urinary tract (39.1%)
- Risk factors for infection: deceased donor (non-ECD RR 2.15, ECD RR 1.69), CMV D+/R- (RR 1.45), more HLA mismatches (RR 1.08)

### Prophylaxis Protocol (MUST MEMORIZE)

| Prophylaxis | Agent | Duration | Recommendation |
|---|---|---|---|
| **PJP** | TMP-SMX | >= 6 months post-tx | KDIGO Level 2 Grade B; also reduces UTI and bacteremia |
| **CMV** | Valganciclovir | >= 3 months post-tx | Strong (especially D+/R-) |
| **UTI** | TMP-SMX (same as PJP) | >= 6 months | Reduces bacteriuria RR 0.41, sepsis RR 0.13 |
| **EBV** | Monitoring (NAT) | High-risk patients | Conditional |

**Exam pearl:** TMP-SMX prophylaxis post-transplant serves DUAL purpose: PJP prophylaxis AND UTI prevention. Single agent, two benefits. This is commonly tested.

### CMV -- Key Exam Facts

- Highest risk: D+/R- (seronegative recipient with seropositive donor)
- Peak onset: 1-6 months post-transplant (during/after prophylaxis completion)
- Prophylaxis vs preemptive: both acceptable strategies
- Treatment: valganciclovir or IV ganciclovir; reduce immunosuppression

### UTI Post-Transplant

- Most common infectious complication (prevalence 7%-80%)
- Asymptomatic bacteriuria: do NOT routinely treat (AST IDCOP Strong, Moderate)
- Recurrent UTI: associated with decreased survival (HR 1.87), decreased graft survival (HR 1.47), increased MDR (RR 1.49)
- UTI > 6 months post-tx: increased mortality (aHR 2.93) and graft loss (aHR 1.85)

**Exam pearl:** The exam tests whether asymptomatic bacteriuria in transplant patients should be treated. Answer: NO (except in first 1-2 months when some centers treat). This is counterintuitive -- transplant patients ARE immunocompromised, but evidence shows treatment does not improve outcomes and increases antibiotic resistance.

## Living Kidney Donation Evaluation

### Donor Assessment Framework

| Domain | Key Points |
|---|---|
| **Kidney function** | GFR >= 90: accept; 60-89: individualize; < 60: decline. Confirm GFR with measured method (not eGFR alone) |
| **Albuminuria** | AER < 30 mg/day: accept; 30-100: individualize; > 100: decline |
| **Blood pressure** | Normal: accept; HTN controlled on 1-2 agents without TOD: accept; Uncontrolled: decline |
| **Diabetes** | Type 1: decline; Type 2/prediabetes: individualize by program threshold |
| **APOL1** | Offer genotyping to sub-Saharan African ancestry donors; 2 risk alleles = increased lifetime kidney failure risk |
| **Cancer** | Active malignancy: decline; ADPKD: decline; IgA nephropathy: decline |
| **Pregnancy** | Do not donate while pregnant; counsel about increased gestational HTN/preeclampsia risk post-donation |

### Contraindications to Living Donation

**Absolute:** kidney disease, diabetes (type 1), active malignancy, HTN with end-organ damage, uncontrolled psychiatric conditions, ADPKD

**Relative:** BMI >= 30-35, controlled HTN, prediabetes, recent nephrolithiasis, FMD, proteinuria, thin basement membrane disease

### Long-Term Donor Risk
- Donor 90-day mortality: 0.02% (vs 0.18% for laparoscopic cholecystectomy)
- ESRD risk: does not greatly exceed general population but small increase documented
- HTN risk: may increase
- No increased long-term risk: CV mortality, kidney stones, fractures, GI bleeding

### ABO/HLA Incompatibility Options
- Kidney paired donation (swap incompatible pairs)
- Desensitization: rituximab + apheresis (isoagglutinin removal) + maintenance immunosuppression
- ABO-incompatible outcomes comparable to ABO-compatible deceased donor transplant

**Exam pearl:** Living donor transplant > deceased donor transplant for graft survival. Even ABO-incompatible living donor transplants with desensitization have outcomes comparable to ABO-compatible deceased donor transplants. The exam may test: "what is the best option for a patient with an ABO-incompatible willing living donor?" Answer: proceed with desensitization or kidney paired donation -- do NOT default to deceased donor waitlist.

| Kaposi sarcoma | 84.9 (300-fold) | HHV-8; mTORi may treat |
| SCC skin | 43.6 | UV exposure; SCC:BCC ratio reversed (2.5:1) |
| Lip cancer | 29.45 | UV + immunosuppression |
| Non-Hodgkin lymphoma (PTLD) | 10.66 | EBV; T-cell depleting induction |
| Kidney cancer (native) | 10.77 | ESRD, acquired cystic disease |
| Thyroid | 5.04 | Immunosuppression |
| Bladder | 3.52 | Especially Asian population (SIR 14.74) |
| Melanoma | 2.48 | Immunosuppression |

- Cumulative incidence: 4%-5% at 5 years, 10% at 10 years, > 25% at 20 years
- No clearly increased risk: breast, ovarian, prostate, uterine, rectal, pancreatic, testicular, brain

### PTLD Risk Factors
- EBV D+/R- (seronegative recipient): aHR 3.33
- Diabetes: aHR 3.49
- Deceased donor: aHR 1.62
- Peak PRA > 80%: aHR 5.39 for PTLD
- HLA-DR match: associated with DECREASED DLBCL risk (aIRR 0.76)

### Immunosuppression and Cancer Risk

| Agent | Cancer Risk |
|---|---|
| **Azathioprine** | STRONGEST association with head/neck cancers and SCC |
| **Alemtuzumab** | Thyroid (aIRR 3.37), colorectal (aIRR 2.46), NHL (aIRR 1.79) |
| **Cyclosporine** | May increase PTLD risk vs tacrolimus-based regimens |
| **Tacrolimus + MMF** | More favorable cancer safety profile |
| **Basiliximab (IL-2Ra)** | NOT associated with increased malignancy |
| **mTOR inhibitors** | DECREASED cancer risk (IRR 0.5); dual anticancer + immunosuppressive |

### Management Principles
- Reduce immunosuppression when managing malignancy [Conditional]
- Switch to mTOR inhibitors (simultaneous anticancer + immunosuppressive effect) [Conditional]
- Kaposi sarcoma: mTOR inhibitors + reduced immunosuppression [Conditional]
- CAUTION with immune checkpoint inhibitors: may cause allograft rejection and immune complex-induced nephropathy

**Exam pearl:** The exam may ask about management of Kaposi sarcoma in a transplant recipient. Answer: switch to mTOR inhibitor (sirolimus) + reduce overall immunosuppression. mTOR inhibitors have direct anti-angiogenic/anti-tumor properties. Do NOT increase immunosuppression.

### Pretransplant Malignancy Waiting Time

| Recurrence Risk | Wait Time | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| High (>= 26%) | >= 5 years | Melanoma, symptomatic RCC, bladder, sarcoma, myeloma |
| Intermediate (11%-25%) | 2-5 years | Breast, colorectal, prostate, uterine body, Wilms |
| Low (<= 10%) | 0-2 years | Lymphoma, thyroid, cervical, testicular, incidental RCC |

## Medical Management of Transplant Recipients

### Post-Transplant HTN

- Present in > 50% of transplant recipients
- BP target: < 130/80 mmHg (KDIGO Level 2, Grade C)
- Causes by timing:
  - Immediate post-op: hypervolemia, steroids, pain
  - Early (24-52 weeks): CNIs, weight gain, steroids, transplant RAS
  - Late (> 1 year): chronic allograft dysfunction, failed native kidney, OSA
- Avoid ACEi/ARB in first week post-transplant (risk to newly transplanted kidney GFR)
- ACEi/ARB may be added after >= 3 months if proteinuria >= 1 g/day
- CCBs may reduce all-cause mortality and graft loss (Cochrane 2024, 97 trials)

**Exam pearl:** Diltiazem (non-DHP CCB) intentionally increases CNI levels -- some centers use this to allow lower CNI doses. The exam may test the interaction: diltiazem/verapamil INCREASE CNI levels (CYP3A4 inhibition), while amlodipine/nifedipine have elevated CCB levels WITH CNI but do not significantly raise CNI levels.

### Post-Transplant CVD
- CVD = leading cause of death after first year (24.7% of deaths)
- Manage at least as intensively as general population [KDIGO]
- Aspirin 65-100 mg/d in all with atherosclerotic CVD (KDIGO Level 2, Grade B)
- Statin dose limits with cyclosporine: lovastatin 20 mg/d, atorvastatin 10 mg/d, rosuvastatin 5 mg/d; avoid simvastatin/pitavastatin; use fluvastatin/pravastatin

### Key Drug Interactions (EXAM FAVORITE)

| Interacting Drug | Effect on CNI/mTORi | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Azole antifungals | Markedly INCREASED levels | Monitor levels closely |
| Rifampin | Markedly DECREASED levels | Monitor; may need large dose increase |
| Allopurinol + azathioprine | Severe myelosuppression | **NEVER combine** (reduce AZA dose 75% or use febuxostat) |
| NSAIDs | Nephrotoxicity | **Do not use** with immunosuppressants |
| St. John's wort | Markedly DECREASED levels | **Contraindicated** |
| Grapefruit juice | INCREASED levels | **Avoid** |
| Carbamazepine/phenytoin | DECREASED levels | Monitor; enzyme induction over weeks |

## Pregnancy After Kidney Transplantation

### Optimal Timing and Criteria

- Wait >= 1-2 years post-transplant (AST 2005: >= 1 year; European 2002: ~2 years)
- Criteria: stable graft (SCr < 1.5 mg/dL), no/minimal proteinuria (< 500 mg/day), normal BP or 1 drug, no recent rejection, stable immunosuppression, no teratogenic medications

### Immunosuppression Safety in Pregnancy

| Safe | Contraindicated (stop >= 6 weeks before) |
|---|---|
| Prednisone < 15 mg/day | **Mycophenolate** (FDA Category D: cleft lip/palate, limb/heart/kidney malformations) |
| Azathioprine <= 2 mg/kg/day | **Sirolimus, everolimus** |
| Cyclosporine at therapeutic levels | Alemtuzumab (wait >= 6 months) |
| Tacrolimus at therapeutic levels | Basiliximab (wait >= 4 months) |

- CNI dose may need 20%-25% increase during pregnancy (increased metabolism/volume of distribution)
- Replace mycophenolate with azathioprine before conception

### Pregnancy Outcomes (vs General Population)

| Outcome | Transplant | General Population |
|---|---|---|
| Live birth | 73.5% | ~70% |
| Preeclampsia | **27%** | 3.8% |
| Preterm delivery | **45.6%** | 12.5% |
| Cesarean delivery | **56.9%** | 31.9% |
| Low birthweight | **42%** | 8.2% |
| Miscarriage | 14% | 15%-20% |

- Prepregnancy SCr >= 1.2 mg/dL: significantly worse outcomes (preeclampsia 52.6% vs 18.9%; graft loss at 3 years 21% vs 0%)
- Pregnancy does NOT increase graft loss/rejection risk in absence of risk factors

**Exam pearl:** The exam tests MMF teratogenicity in pregnancy. MMF MUST be stopped >= 6 weeks before conception and replaced with azathioprine. If a transplant patient becomes pregnant on MMF, the answer is to STOP MMF immediately and switch to azathioprine. Tacrolimus and cyclosporine are SAFE during pregnancy.
