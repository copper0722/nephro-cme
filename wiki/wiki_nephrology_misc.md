## Common Laboratory Tests in Renal Failure

### Creatinine Pitfalls

| Factor | Effect on sCr | Effect on eGFR | Clinical scenario |
|---|---|---|---|
| Muscle wasting (CKD cachexia) | ↓ sCr (falsely low) | ↑ eGFR (overestimates) | Malnourished ESKD patient with "normal" sCr |
| High muscle mass | ↑ sCr | ↓ eGFR (underestimates) | Young athletic male |
| Trimethoprim, cimetidine | ↑ sCr (blocks tubular secretion) | ↓ eGFR (false alarm) | Not true GFR change |
| Cooked meat (large meal) | Transient ↑ sCr | Transient ↓ eGFR | Fasting sample preferred |
| Ketoacidosis | Falsely ↑ sCr (Jaffe method interference) | ↓ eGFR (artifact) | DKA patient |
| Cefoxitin, flucytosine | Falsely ↑ sCr (Jaffe interference) | ↓ eGFR (artifact) | Enzymatic method avoids this |

### BUN Pitfalls

| Factor | BUN effect | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| GI bleeding | ↑ | Protein absorption from blood in gut |
| High-protein diet | ↑ | ↑ urea production |
| Catabolic state (sepsis, burns) | ↑ | ↑ protein breakdown |
| Corticosteroids | ↑ | ↑ protein catabolism |
| Liver failure | ↓ | ↓ urea synthesis |
| Malnutrition | ↓ | ↓ protein substrate |

**BUN/Cr ratio:**
- > 20:1 → prerenal (dehydration, CHF, GI bleed)
- 10-15:1 → intrinsic renal or normal
- < 10:1 → liver disease, malnutrition, rhabdomyolysis

### Cystatin C

- Freely filtered, completely reabsorbed and catabolized by proximal tubule
- NOT affected by muscle mass, diet, sex
- Better than creatinine in: elderly (sarcopenia), amputees, obesity, vegetarian diet
- Combined CKD-EPI creatinine-cystatin C equation = most accurate eGFR
- Limitations: affected by thyroid function (↑ in hyperthyroidism, ↓ in hypothyroidism), corticosteroids (↑), inflammation

### Urine Studies in Renal Failure

| Parameter | Prerenal | ATN | Postrenal |
|---|---|---|---|
| FENa | < 1% | > 2% | Variable |
| FEUrea | < 35% | > 50% | Variable |
| UNa | < 20 mEq/L | > 40 mEq/L | Variable |
| Urine osm | > 500 mOsm/kg | < 350 mOsm/kg | Variable |
| BUN/Cr | > 20:1 | 10-15:1 | Variable |
| Urine sediment | Bland or hyaline casts | **Muddy brown casts, epithelial cell casts** | Bland |

**Exam pearl:** FENa < 1% is unreliable when patient is on diuretics. Use **FEUrea** instead (< 35% = prerenal, > 50% = ATN). FENa also falsely low in contrast nephropathy, myoglobinuria, early sepsis, and acute GN (despite being intrinsic disease).

## Kidney Function in Older Adults

### Age-Related GFR Decline

- GFR declines ~0.75-1.0 mL/min/1.73 m2/year after age 40
- By age 70: mean GFR ~75 mL/min/1.73 m2
- By age 80+: mean GFR ~60 mL/min/1.73 m2
- Structural: ↓ nephron number (nephrosclerosis), ↓ renal mass, cortical thinning
- Functional: ↓ renal plasma flow, ↓ concentrating ability, ↓ sodium conservation

### CKD Diagnosis in Elderly — Controversy

**Problem:** Applying standard KDIGO definition (GFR < 60 = CKD G3a) to elderly → ~30-40% of those > 70 years would be labeled "CKD" despite no proteinuria, stable function, and no adverse outcomes.

**Arguments for age-adapted thresholds:**
- GFR decline is physiologic and expected
- Label of CKD causes unnecessary anxiety, testing, referrals
- EKFC equation provides age-adapted GFR reporting
- European perspective (Delanaye, CJASN 2019): suggests GFR < 45 as CKD threshold in elderly

**Arguments against (current KDIGO position):**
- Even "mild" GFR reduction associates with ↑ cardiovascular risk
- GFR < 60 predicts AKI risk, drug toxicity, contrast nephropathy regardless of age
- Proteinuria risk assessment is more important than GFR threshold debate
- 2012 KDIGO maintains GFR < 60 = CKD regardless of age

**Exam pearl:** KDIGO does NOT endorse age-adapted GFR thresholds. GFR < 60 = CKD at any age. BUT know the controversy exists. The exam may test: elderly person with stable GFR 55, no proteinuria → still meets KDIGO CKD definition but prognosis differs from younger patient with same GFR.

### Drug Dosing in Elderly CKD

- Elderly at highest risk for drug-related AKI (↓ GFR + polypharmacy)
- NSAIDs, metformin, DOACs, digoxin, lithium: require dose adjustment
- Metformin: current guidelines allow use down to eGFR 30 (with dose reduction at eGFR 30-45)
- Gabapentin, pregabalin: significant accumulation in CKD; dose reduce

## Hyperuricemia and Gout in CKD

### Epidemiology

- Hyperuricemia prevalence increases with CKD stage: ~40% in CKD G3, ~70% in CKD G4-5
- Gout prevalence: 15-20% in advanced CKD (vs 3-4% general population)
- Mechanisms: ↓ renal urate excretion (primary), ↑ production (cell turnover), diuretic use

### Hyperuricemia → CKD Causation Debate

**Observational data:** hyperuricemia associated with CKD incidence and progression (multiple cohorts)

**But causation unproven:** RCTs of urate-lowering therapy (ULT) in CKD show **NO benefit** on GFR preservation:
- **CKD-FIX** (NEJM 2020, N=369): Allopurinol vs placebo in CKD stage 3-4. No difference in eGFR decline at 2 years.
- **PERL** (NEJM 2020, N=530): Allopurinol in type 1 diabetes with early CKD. No benefit on GFR.
- **FEATHER** (CJASN 2018, N=443): Febuxostat in CKD stage 3. No significant benefit.

**Bottom line:** Urate-lowering therapy does NOT slow CKD progression. Treat hyperuricemia for gout symptoms, NOT for kidney protection.

**Exam pearl:** Do NOT prescribe allopurinol/febuxostat for asymptomatic hyperuricemia in CKD to "protect kidneys." CKD-FIX and PERL definitively showed no benefit. This is a common exam distractor.

### Gout Management in CKD

- **Acute gout:** colchicine (dose-reduce in CKD; avoid if GFR < 10); corticosteroids (preferred in advanced CKD); avoid NSAIDs
- **ULT (for recurrent gout):** allopurinol (start low 50-100 mg, titrate to target urate < 6 mg/dL; max dose lower in CKD but can still titrate carefully); febuxostat (no dose adjustment needed for mild-moderate CKD)
- **Probenecid:** ineffective when GFR < 50 (requires functioning tubular secretion)

## Hypouricemia

### Definition and Causes

- Serum uric acid < 2 mg/dL
- Causes:
  - Renal hypouricemia (hereditary: SLC22A12 or SLC2A9 mutations — URAT1 or GLUT9 deficiency)
  - SIADH (dilutional + ↑ renal clearance)
  - Fanconi syndrome (proximal tubular dysfunction)
  - Drugs: losartan (uricosuric ARB), high-dose salicylates, SGLT2 inhibitors
  - Xanthine oxidase deficiency (xanthinuria — very rare)

### Clinical Significance

- Often asymptomatic and incidental
- **Exercise-induced AKI:** hereditary renal hypouricemia → ↑ uric acid excretion during exercise → intratubular uric acid crystal precipitation → AKI (reported in Japanese/Korean populations)
- **Uric acid nephrolithiasis:** possible with massive uricosuria

## Incidental Renal Mass — Bosniak Classification

### Bosniak Classification (2019 Update)

| Class | Features | Malignancy risk | Management |
|---|---|---|---|
| I | Simple cyst: thin wall, no septa/calcification/enhancement | ~0% | No follow-up |
| II | Thin septa (few), fine calcification, high-attenuation (< 3 cm), no enhancement | ~0% | No follow-up |
| IIF | Multiple thin septa, minimal smooth wall/septal thickening, thick/nodular calcification, no enhancement | 5-10% | Follow-up imaging (6, 12 months then annually x5 years) |
| III | Thick/irregular wall/septa, measurable enhancement | ~50% | Surgical excision or active surveillance |
| IV | Enhancing soft-tissue component | ~90% | Surgical excision |

### Solid Renal Mass

- Solid enhancing renal mass = RCC until proven otherwise
- Small renal mass (< 4 cm, stage T1a): options include partial nephrectomy, ablation, or active surveillance (especially in elderly/comorbid)
- Biopsy: consider for masses < 4 cm when management would change; required before ablation
- Oncocytoma and angiomyolipoma are benign but can be difficult to distinguish from RCC on imaging

**Exam pearl:** Bosniak I and II cysts = simple/minimally complex. No follow-up needed. Bosniak IIF = needs surveillance. Bosniak III/IV = surgery or active surveillance. AML with fat = pathognomonic on CT (fat density), no further workup.

## COVID-19 and CKD/ESKD

### CKD as Risk Factor

- CKD = independent risk factor for severe COVID-19, hospitalization, ICU admission, and death
- Risk increases with CKD stage (highest in ESKD/dialysis)
- Impaired vaccine response in advanced CKD and dialysis (↓ seroconversion rates)
- Additional booster doses recommended for immunocompromised CKD patients

### COVID-19 Kidney Manifestations

- AKI in 20-40% of hospitalized COVID patients (multifactorial: hemodynamic, direct viral, inflammation)
- Collapsing FSGS ("COVID-associated nephropathy"): particularly in APOL1 risk allele carriers
- Proteinuria and hematuria common (even without AKI)
- Thrombotic microangiopathy
- Tubulointerstitial nephritis

### Dialysis Considerations

- Infection control: isolation, cohorting, PPE
- HD patients: higher mortality than general population (~20-30% in early waves; improved with vaccination/treatment)
- PD: may have advantages (home-based, less healthcare contact)
- Transplant recipients: highest risk (immunosuppression); consider modifying IS during acute illness (reduce MMF/anti-metabolites; maintain CNI and steroids)

## Urinary Tract Obstruction and Hydronephrosis

### Causes by Location

| Level | Causes |
|---|---|
| **Upper (UPJ/ureter)** | Stones (most common), UPJ obstruction (congenital), ureteral stricture, retroperitoneal fibrosis, extrinsic compression (tumor, AAA, pregnancy) |
| **Lower (bladder/urethra)** | BPH (most common in elderly males), neurogenic bladder, urethral stricture, posterior urethral valves (neonates), pelvic malignancy |

### Bilateral Obstruction → AKI

- Bilateral obstruction (or unilateral in single functioning kidney) required for significant AKI
- Post-obstructive diuresis: after relief of bilateral obstruction, massive diuresis can occur (physiologic + pathologic components)
  - Physiologic: excretion of retained solutes/water (appropriate)
  - Pathologic: impaired concentrating ability → inappropriate salt/water losses (may need IV replacement)
- Monitor UOP, electrolytes, volume status closely for 24-48 hours after relief
- Replace approximately 50% of UOP with IV fluids (avoid excessive replacement which perpetuates diuresis)

### Imaging

- **US:** first-line; hydronephrosis grading (mild/moderate/severe); 90% sensitivity for obstruction but may be negative in early obstruction (< 24 hours), dehydrated patients, or retroperitoneal fibrosis
- **CT (non-contrast):** gold standard for kidney stones; detects level and cause of obstruction
- **Nuclear renal scan (MAG3 with diuretic):** assesses obstruction vs dilation (especially UPJ)

### Post-Obstructive Nephropathy

- CKD may develop after prolonged obstruction even after relief
- Duration and severity of obstruction determine reversibility
- Complete bilateral obstruction > 2 weeks → likely irreversible damage
- Partial obstruction: variable, may improve months after relief

## Calciphylaxis (Calcific Uremic Arteriolopathy)

### Key Facts

- Calcification of small dermal/subcutaneous arterioles → thrombosis → ischemic tissue necrosis
- Predominantly in ESKD/dialysis patients but can occur in earlier CKD or even non-CKD (rare)
- Mortality: 50-80% at 1 year (extremely high)
- Female predominance; obesity, diabetes, warfarin use = major risk factors

### Risk Factors

- ESKD/dialysis (strongest)
- Warfarin use (inhibits Matrix Gla protein carboxylation → loss of vascular calcification inhibition)
- Elevated Ca x P product (but not always)
- Obesity
- Diabetes
- Hypoalbuminemia
- Recent trauma/injection at affected sites
- Female sex
- Hypercoagulable states (protein C/S deficiency)

### Clinical Features

- **Exquisitely painful** subcutaneous plaques/nodules
- Progress to livedo reticularis → necrotic ulcers with eschar
- Distribution: areas with highest subcutaneous fat (thighs, abdomen, buttocks = proximal; fingers, toes = distal)
- **Proximal lesions** (trunk/thighs): worse prognosis than distal (digits)
- Superinfection → sepsis (common cause of death)

### Diagnosis

- Clinical diagnosis in appropriate setting (ESKD + characteristic lesions)
- **Skin biopsy** (confirmatory but risk of non-healing wound): medial arteriolar calcification, intimal hyperplasia, microthrombi
- Bone scan: increased uptake in soft tissue at lesion sites
- **Avoid deep biopsy through necrotic tissue** (infection risk)

### Management

- **Wound care:** multidisciplinary (surgery, dermatology, nephrology, wound care, pain management)
- **Sodium thiosulfate:** 25 g IV during last 30-60 minutes of HD (or after HD), 3x/week
  - Chelates calcium, antioxidant properties
  - Side effects: metabolic acidosis, nausea, hypotension
  - Best evidence: observational; no RCTs but widely used
- **Stop warfarin:** switch to non-vitamin K anticoagulant (DOAC) or heparin if anticoagulation needed
- **Optimize CKD-MBD:** lower Ca x P product, avoid calcium-based phosphate binders (use sevelamer), avoid excessive vitamin D, cinacalcet if PTH elevated
- **Intensify dialysis:** increase HD frequency/duration
- **Avoid tissue trauma:** no subcutaneous injections in affected areas
- **Debridement:** careful surgical debridement of necrotic tissue; avoid large excisions
- **Pain management:** often requires opioids (significant morbidity from pain alone)
- **Parathyroidectomy:** consider if severe hyperparathyroidism contributing; but calciphylaxis can occur with LOW PTH too

**Exam pearl:** Calciphylaxis management = stop warfarin + sodium thiosulfate IV + non-calcium phosphate binders + wound care. Warfarin is both a risk factor AND must be discontinued. This is the opposite of usual anticoagulation management.

## Lactic Acidosis

### Classification

| Type | Mechanism | Causes |
|---|---|---|
| **Type A** (hypoxic) | Tissue hypoperfusion/hypoxia → anaerobic glycolysis | Sepsis/shock, cardiac arrest, severe anemia, CO poisoning, mesenteric ischemia, hemorrhage |
| **Type B** (non-hypoxic) | Impaired lactate metabolism or ↑ production without global hypoxia | B1: underlying disease (liver failure, malignancy, thiamine deficiency, DKA); B2: drugs (metformin, linezolid, NRTIs, propofol, epinephrine); B3: inborn errors (mitochondrial disorders) |

### D-Lactic Acidosis

- Produced by gut bacteria in patients with short bowel syndrome or jejunoileal bypass
- Standard lactate assay measures **L-lactate** only → D-lactate NOT detected
- Presents: AG metabolic acidosis with "normal" lactate on standard assay + neurologic symptoms (confusion, slurred speech, ataxia)
- **Exam pearl:** AG metabolic acidosis + normal L-lactate + short bowel syndrome + neurologic symptoms → D-lactic acidosis. Need specific D-lactate assay.

### Metformin-Associated Lactic Acidosis (MALA)

- Risk increases with CKD (impaired metformin clearance)
- Accumulation → inhibits mitochondrial complex I → ↑ anaerobic glycolysis → ↑ lactate
- Current guidelines: safe down to eGFR 30; contraindicated < 30; dose reduce at 30-45
- Treatment: IV NaHCO3 for severe acidosis; **HD very effective** (removes metformin which is a small, water-soluble molecule with low protein binding)
- **Exam pearl:** Metformin overdose or MALA with severe acidosis → HD is both specific treatment (removes metformin) and supportive (corrects acidosis, removes lactate).

### Lactate in CKD/ESKD

- CKD patients often have mild baseline ↑ lactate (impaired clearance)
- Lactate-based PD dialysate → absorbed lactate → converted to HCO3- in liver → metabolic alkalosis possible
- HD: standard dialysate uses bicarbonate, not lactate

## High-Yield Exam Scenarios

1. **CKD patient on warfarin develops extremely painful purple plaques on thighs with necrotic centers:** Calciphylaxis. Stop warfarin immediately. Start sodium thiosulfate 25 g IV 3x/week during HD. Switch to sevelamer. Wound care. Switch anticoagulation to DOAC.

2. **78-year-old with stable GFR 52, no proteinuria, no hematuria — does this patient have CKD?** By KDIGO definition: yes (GFR < 60 for > 3 months). But prognosis is much better than 50-year-old with same GFR. Know the age-adapted threshold controversy.

3. **Patient with CKD stage 4 and asymptomatic uric acid 9.5 mg/dL — treat?** No. CKD-FIX (2020) showed no benefit of allopurinol on eGFR decline. Treat only if symptomatic gout.

4. **CT shows 2.5 cm renal cyst with thin septum, fine calcification, no enhancement:** Bosniak II. No follow-up needed. Benign.

5. **Bilateral ureteral obstruction from pelvic tumor relieved by nephrostomy, UOP 800 mL/h for first 6 hours:** Post-obstructive diuresis. Physiologic (appropriate excretion of retained solutes) + pathologic (concentrating defect). Replace ~50% of UOP with IV NS. Monitor electrolytes q4-6h.

6. **AG metabolic acidosis, short bowel syndrome, "normal" lactate, patient confused and ataxic:** D-lactic acidosis. Standard assay measures only L-lactate. Order D-lactate level. Treat with oral antibiotics to reduce D-lactate-producing gut bacteria + low-carbohydrate diet.
