# Renal Artery Stenosis and Renovascular Hypertension

## Exam Logic Summary

RAS questions test: (1) clinical clues distinguishing atherosclerotic vs FMD, (2) indications for diagnostic testing, (3) ACEi-induced AKI as diagnostic clue for bilateral RAS, (4) ASTRAL/CORAL trial results (medical therapy = stenting for most atherosclerotic RAS), (5) flash pulmonary edema (Pickering syndrome), (6) imaging modality selection. Key distractor: assuming all RAS needs revascularization (CORAL showed no benefit over medical therapy for most patients).

**Textbook ref:** Brenner Ch. 42 (Renovascular Hypertension), Harrison Ch. 278 (Vascular Diseases of the Kidney)

---

## Definitions

- **Significant RAS:** ESC/ESVS: > 60% diameter reduction + hemodynamic significance. ACC/AHA: >= 70% OR 50%-69% with hemodynamic confirmation
- **Hemodynamic criteria:** Resting systolic translesional gradient >= 20 mmHg; resting mean gradient >= 10 mmHg; hyperemic systolic gradient >= 20 mmHg; FFR < 0.8
- **Renovascular hypertension:** Secondary HTN from sustained kidney hypoperfusion -> RAAS activation
- **Ischemic nephropathy:** Renal dysfunction from decreased perfusion associated with stenosis
- **Pickering syndrome:** Flash pulmonary edema in bilateral RAS or unilateral RAS + solitary kidney

## Etiology

| Cause | Prevalence | Demographics | Vessel segment | Key features |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Atherosclerosis** | approximately 90% | Men > 65 years, smoking, DM, CAD | Ostium + proximal 1/3 | Systemic atherosclerosis; renal insufficiency common |
| **FMD** | approximately 10% | Women 15-50 years (F:M = 9:1) | Distal 2/3 | "String of beads" on angiography; preserved GFR; other vascular beds affected |
| **Other** | < 1% | Various | Various | Arteritis (Takayasu, PAN), dissection, aneurysm, NF1, radiation, transplant anastomosis |

### Risk Factors — Atherosclerotic RAS

Male, age >= 65, tobacco, HTN (resistant), DM, obesity, hyperlipidemia, CAD, PAD, CKD, abdominal aortic aneurysm

### Risk Factors — FMD

Female, premenopausal (15-50), smoking, HTN

## Clinical Presentation

### When to Suspect RAS (Testing Indications)

**Strong Recommendation (perform diagnostic testing):**
- HTN onset before age 30
- Severe HTN onset after age 55
- Accelerated/resistant/malignant HTN
- New azotemia or worsening renal function after ACEi/ARB
- Unexplained atrophic kidney or > 1.5 cm size discrepancy
- Sudden unexplained pulmonary edema (especially with azotemia)
- HTN with abdominal bruit

**Conditional Recommendation (consider testing):**
- Unexplained renal failure including those starting dialysis/considering transplant
- Multivessel CAD with no other RAS indications at time of angiography
- Unexplained heart failure or refractory angina

**Exam pearl:** ACEi/ARB-induced AKI is a CLASSIC exam clue for bilateral RAS or RAS in solitary kidney. Mechanism: efferent arteriolar dilation removes the compensatory mechanism maintaining GFR in the stenotic kidney. sCr rise >= 20% after ACEi = sensitivity 100%, specificity 70% for severe bilateral RAS.

### Atherosclerotic vs FMD Presentation

| Feature | Atherosclerotic | FMD |
|---|---|---|
| Age/sex | Male, > 65 | Female, 15-50 |
| BP pattern | Resistant HTN, often with renal insufficiency | Moderate-severe HTN, often acute onset |
| Renal function | Often impaired | Usually preserved |
| Imaging | Ostial/proximal narrowing | Distal "string of beads" |
| Other vascular beds | CAD, PVD, cerebrovascular disease | Carotid, vertebral arteries |
| Complications | Progressive CKD, flash pulmonary edema | Aneurysm, dissection -> flank pain, hematuria |

## Flash Pulmonary Edema (Pickering Syndrome)

- Recurrent flash pulmonary edema in bilateral RAS or unilateral RAS + solitary kidney
- Mechanism: bilateral RAS -> cannot perform pressure natriuresis -> volume retention -> sudden pulmonary edema
- Imaging findings suggesting Pickering: renal artery ostial calcifications, kidney diameter <= 7 cm with no measurable resistive indexes, OR kidney > 7 cm with RI < 0.55 or heterogeneity between kidneys
- One of the strongest indications for revascularization

**Exam pearl:** "Recurrent flash pulmonary edema in an elderly patient with atherosclerotic disease" = think bilateral RAS. This is a strong indication for revascularization even in the post-CORAL era.

## Diagnostic Imaging

### Recommended Modalities

| Modality | Sensitivity | Specificity | Advantages | Disadvantages |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Duplex US | 75%-85% | 89%-92% | No contrast, repeatable, assesses hemodynamics | Operator-dependent, limited by body habitus/gas |
| CTA | 94% | 93% | High spatial resolution, less operator-dependent | Iodinated contrast (nephrotoxic), calcification artifact |
| MRA (Gd-enhanced) | 90%-97% | 93%-98% | No ionizing radiation, characterizes renal parenchyma | Gadolinium (NSF risk in GFR < 30, but macrocyclic agents safe), may overestimate stenosis, cannot use with metallic implants |
| Catheter angiography (DSA) | Gold standard | Gold standard | Definitive diagnosis + intervention in same session | Invasive, contrast, vascular access risks |

**Exam pearl:** First-line imaging = Duplex US (ACC/AHA Class I, Level B). CTA/MRA if US inconclusive or anatomy needs clarification. MRA preferred if renal insufficiency present (use noncontrast or macrocyclic gadolinium). DSA only if high suspicion + noninvasive tests inconclusive. Captopril renal scintigraphy is NOT recommended (ACC/AHA Class III).

### Ultrasound Criteria for Significant RAS

- Peak systolic velocity > 180-200 cm/s (sensitivity 85%, specificity 92%)
- Renal:aortic velocity ratio > 3.5
- Renal resistive index (RRI) < 0.6 suggests significant RAS > 70%
- RRI = (peak systolic velocity - end diastolic velocity) / peak systolic velocity
- Normal RRI: 0.6-0.7
- RRI > 0.7 = high; may predict poor outcome from revascularization
- RRI > 0.8 after revascularization = poor prognosis

## Management

### Medical Therapy (All Patients)

- **ACEi/ARB:** First-line for unilateral RAS (Strong Recommendation). Use with VERY close monitoring for bilateral RAS or single functioning kidney (Conditional Recommendation). Contraindicated if bilateral severe RAS with rising Cr.
- **CCB:** Strong Recommendation
- **Diuretics:** Strong Recommendation
- **Beta-blockers:** Strong Recommendation
- **Statins, antiplatelet:** As clinically indicated

### Revascularization — Indications

Consider for RAS >= 70% OR 50%-69% with hemodynamic significance AND any of:

| Clinical scenario | Recommendation level |
|---|---|
| Accelerated decrease in renal function (on ACEi/ARB) + bilateral or solitary kidney RAS | Strong |
| Uncontrolled HTN despite >= 3 drugs including diuretic | Conditional |
| Cardiac destabilization: flash pulmonary edema | Strong |
| Recurrent heart failure on maximal medical therapy | Conditional |
| Uncontrolled unstable angina on maximal therapy | Conditional |

### Key Trials: Revascularization vs Medical Therapy

| Trial | Year | N | Bottom line |
|---|---|---|---|
| **ASTRAL** | 2009 | 806 | Angioplasty + medical vs medical alone: no significant difference in renal function, BP, CV events, mortality at median 34 months. CRITICIZED for including patients with < 50% stenosis and excluding "ideal" revascularization candidates |
| **CORAL** | 2014 | 947 | Stenting + medical vs medical alone for atherosclerotic RAS >= 60%: no significant difference in composite CV/renal endpoints at median 43 months. Medical therapy is SUFFICIENT for most patients |

**Exam pearl:** ASTRAL and CORAL demonstrated that for most atherosclerotic RAS, revascularization does NOT improve outcomes compared to optimal medical therapy. The correct answer for "management of atherosclerotic RAS" is almost always "maximize medical therapy." Revascularization reserved for: flash pulmonary edema (Pickering), refractory heart failure, rapidly declining GFR on ACEi/ARB, truly resistant HTN failing >= 3 drugs.

**Exception:** FMD in young patients — revascularization (balloon angioplasty, usually without stent) has much better outcomes than in atherosclerotic RAS. May cure or significantly improve HTN.

## NOT Recommended Tests (ACC/AHA Class III)

- Selective renal vein renin measurements
- Plasma renin activity
- Captopril renal scintigraphy

## High-Yield Exam Scenarios

1. **65-year-old man with resistant HTN, PAD, Cr 2.0, starts enalapril -> Cr rises to 3.5 in 2 weeks:** Bilateral RAS (or RAS + solitary kidney). ACEi removed efferent arteriolar tone maintaining GFR. Stop ACEi. Obtain renal artery duplex US. Consider revascularization if Cr does not recover.

2. **28-year-old woman with new-onset severe HTN, normal Cr, no other medical history:** FMD. MRA showing "string of beads" in distal renal artery. Consider balloon angioplasty (potential for cure/significant improvement in young FMD patients).

3. **72-year-old with recurrent flash pulmonary edema despite optimal cardiac management:** Pickering syndrome (bilateral RAS). Duplex US -> bilateral RAS >= 70%. Strong indication for revascularization.

4. **Patient with atherosclerotic RAS 75%. Best management?** Maximize medical therapy (ACEi/ARB if tolerated + CCB + diuretic + statin + antiplatelet). CORAL showed no benefit of stenting over medical therapy. Revascularize only if medical therapy fails (flash pulmonary edema, refractory HF, rapidly declining GFR).

5. **Which imaging modality for RAS screening in patient with GFR 25?** MRA with noncontrast technique or macrocyclic gadolinium (macrocyclic agents have virtually eliminated NSF risk). Avoid CTA (iodinated contrast nephrotoxicity). US is acceptable but may be limited.
