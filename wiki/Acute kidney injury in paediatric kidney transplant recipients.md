# AKI in Pediatric Kidney Transplant Recipients

## Exam Logic
*   **The "Pseudo-AKI" Trap:** Be alert for drugs that inhibit tubular secretion of creatinine (e.g., **Trimethoprim** in cotrimoxazole). This causes a rise in serum Cr without a decrease in GFR.
*   **Autoregulation Deficit:** Unlike native kidneys, the allograft lacks **sympathetic innervation** (severed during retrieval). This renders the graft hypersensitive to fluctuations in mean arterial pressure (MAP); small drops in BP lead to immediate drops in GFR.
*   **The TRAS Triad:** Transplant Renal Artery Stenosis (TRAS) presents as **difficult-to-control HTN**, **fluid overload**, and **hypokalemic metabolic alkalosis** (due to secondary hyperaldosteronism).
*   **Timing as a Diagnostic Tool:** 
    *   *Immediate (<1 week):* Think surgical (thrombosis, kinking, urinary leak) or hyperacute rejection.
    *   *Early (1 week – 6 months):* Think infection (UTI), acute rejection, or CNI toxicity.
    *   *Late (>6 months):* Think chronic rejection, recurrent primary disease, or TRAS.
*   **Vascular Red Flags:** Sudden anuria + pain = **Renal Artery Thrombosis**. Reversal of arterial diastolic flow on Doppler = **Renal Vein Thrombosis**.

## Clinical Summary

### 1. Definitions & Biomarkers
*   **AKI Criteria:** $\ge$ 26 $\mu$mol/L rise in Cr within 48h, $\ge$ 50% rise in 7 days, or UO < 0.5 mL/kg/h for > 6h.
*   **Pediatric Nuance:** Cr is a lagging marker and non-linear. Muscle mass variations in children make absolute Cr changes misleading.
*   **Cystatin C:** More sensitive than Cr; however, levels are confounded by **inflammation** and **steroid use** in transplant patients.

### 2. Pre-renal & Vascular Causes
*   **Hemodynamics:** Allografts are highly susceptible to **Acute Tubular Injury (ATI)** during low-flow states because the proximal tubule has high mitochondrial density and high O₂ demand.
*   **TRAS (Transplant Renal Artery Stenosis):** 
    *   Most common at the anastomosis site.
    *   **Gold Standard Dx:** Digital subtraction angiography.
    *   **Tx:** Angioplasty/stenting (risk: dissection/infarction).
*   **Thrombotic Events:**
    *   **Artery Thrombosis:** Rare (1.7%), sudden anuria, requires emergency rescue thrombectomy.
    *   **Vein Thrombosis:** Incidence 2.3%, characterized by **reversed diastolic flow** on Doppler.

### 3. Intrinsic & Other Causes
*   **Common Etiologies:** Infection (50%—specifically UTI), Acute Rejection (28%), and CNI Toxicity (11%).
*   **Post-Biopsy Complications:** Pseudoaneurysms or AV fistulae (rare, 0.1%). Pseudoaneurysms may cause macroscopic hematuria.

## Timing of AKI Etiologies

| Period | Primary Suspects |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Immediate (0–1 week)** | ATI, Hypovolemia, Obstruction, Urinary leak, Renal artery/vein thrombosis, Hyperacute rejection |
| **Early (1 week–6 months)** | Infection, Acute rejection, CNI toxicity, ATI, Obstruction, Recurrent disease, TMA |
| **Late (>6 months)** | Infection, Chronic rejection, Acute rejection, CNI toxicity, Obstruction, Recurrent disease, De novo kidney disease, TMA |

## Key Trials/Conceptual Models
*   **Goldblatt (1934):** Conceptual model demonstrating that renal artery clamping $\rightarrow$ $\uparrow$ Renin $\rightarrow$ $\uparrow$ Aldosterone $\rightarrow$ Volume expansion + HTN (The basis for understanding TRAS).
*   **Alkandari et al.:** Identified prevalence of AKI in pediatric recipients (37%) and primary drivers: Infection (50%) > Rejection (28%) > CNI toxicity (11%).
