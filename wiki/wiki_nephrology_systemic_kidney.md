# Systemic Disease and Kidney — Board Review

## Exam Logic Summary

Tests: (1) HIVAN pathology (collapsing FSGS) and association with low CD4, (2) scleroderma renal crisis management (ACEi even if sCr rising), (3) infection-related GN (low C3, IgA-dominant in adults), (4) thin basement membrane nephropathy vs early Alport, (5) organized deposits on EM (fibrillary, immunotactoid). Common distractor: stopping ACEi in scleroderma renal crisis.

## HIV-Associated Nephropathy (HIVAN)

### Key Facts

- **Collapsing variant of FSGS** — pathognomonic
- Almost exclusively in patients of **African descent** (APOL1 risk alleles G1/G2)
- Associated with **low CD4 count** (< 200) and high viral load
- Can be presenting manifestation of HIV

### Clinical Features

- Heavy proteinuria (often nephrotic range)
- Rapidly progressive renal failure
- **Normal or enlarged kidneys** on US (unlike most CKD which has small kidneys)
- Increased renal echogenicity
- Relatively **bland urine sediment** (no significant hematuria)
- Edema may be minimal despite nephrotic proteinuria (low albumin but ↓ GFR limits edema)

### Pathology

- **LM:** collapsing glomerulopathy (global/segmental collapse of capillary tuft with overlying podocyte hypertrophy/hyperplasia); tubuloreticular inclusions, microcystic tubular dilation
- **IF:** negative or nonspecific trapping (IgM, C3)
- **EM:** extensive podocyte foot process effacement, collapse of GBM, tubuloreticular inclusions in endothelial cells ("interferon footprint")

**Exam pearl:** Collapsing FSGS + enlarged echogenic kidneys + African descent + low CD4 = HIVAN. Regular FSGS does NOT have collapsing morphology or tubuloreticular inclusions.

### Other HIV-Related Kidney Diseases

| Disease | Pathology | Associations |
|---|---|---|
| HIVAN | Collapsing FSGS | Low CD4, African descent, APOL1 |
| HIV immune complex kidney disease (HIVICK) | MPGN or membranous or IgA pattern | Any CD4; any race |
| TMA | Thrombotic microangiopathy | Advanced HIV |
| AIN | Interstitial nephritis | Drug-related (TDF, atazanavir, indinavir) |
| Tenofovir nephrotoxicity | Proximal tubular dysfunction (Fanconi-like) | TDF (not TAF) |
| Indinavir stones | Crystalluria/nephrolithiasis | Indinavir use |

### Management

- **ART (antiretroviral therapy):** cornerstone of HIVAN treatment. Suppressing viral load and restoring CD4 = most effective intervention.
- ACEi/ARB for proteinuria reduction
- Avoid nephrotoxic antiretrovirals in patients with CKD (switch TDF to TAF)
- Steroids: some use for acute HIVAN unresponsive to ART (limited evidence)
- HIVAN can recur in transplant if viral suppression lost

## Scleroderma Renal Crisis (SRC)

### Key Facts

- Occurs in 5-15% of patients with diffuse cutaneous systemic sclerosis (dcSSc)
- **Acute onset** of severe hypertension + rapidly progressive AKI
- Can occur in normotensive patients (10-20% of SRC are "normotensive crisis")
- Most common in first 4 years of disease, especially first 1-2 years

### Risk Factors

- Diffuse cutaneous SSc (not limited/CREST)
- Anti-RNA polymerase III antibodies (strongest predictor)
- Corticosteroid use > 15 mg/day (prednisolone) — major precipitant
- Rapid skin progression
- New anemia, pericardial effusion

**Exam pearl:** Anti-Scl-70 (topoisomerase I) = pulmonary fibrosis risk. Anti-RNA polymerase III = scleroderma renal crisis risk. Anti-centromere = limited SSc/CREST (low SRC risk).

### Pathology

- Intimal proliferation and fibrinoid necrosis of interlobular and arcuate arteries ("onion skinning")
- Thrombotic microangiopathy
- Juxtaglomerular apparatus hyperplasia (renin production → malignant HTN)
- Similar to malignant hypertensive nephrosclerosis

### Clinical Features

- **Acute severe hypertension** (often > 150/90 mmHg or > 30/15 above baseline)
- Rapidly rising sCr
- Microangiopathic hemolytic anemia (schistocytes, ↓ haptoglobin, ↑ LDH)
- Thrombocytopenia
- Proteinuria (mild-moderate)
- Headache, visual changes, seizures, pulmonary edema
- May mimic malignant HTN or TTP

### Management

- **ACEi = first-line treatment** (captopril preferred — short acting, titratable)
  - Start immediately, even if sCr is rising
  - **DO NOT STOP** ACEi even as creatinine increases — this is key management principle
  - Titrate to normalize BP over 72 hours (not too rapidly)
  - ARBs are NOT equivalent (lack evidence in SRC)
- Avoid corticosteroids (precipitate SRC)
- RRT if needed (50% require temporary dialysis)
- **Recovery possible even after dialysis** — continue ACEi on dialysis; 50% of those requiring dialysis may recover kidney function over 12-24 months if ACEi maintained

**Exam pearl:** The cardinal rule of SRC management = START and NEVER STOP ACEi. This is the opposite of most AKI situations where you would hold ACEi. Captopril specifically (not ARBs). Patients who stop ACEi during SRC have much worse outcomes.

## Infection-Related Glomerulonephritis (IRGN)

### Evolution of Terminology

- Previously: "post-infectious GN" or "post-streptococcal GN (PSGN)"
- Now: "infection-related GN" — encompasses GN occurring during active infection (not just post-infection)
- Classic PSGN in children has become less common; infection-related GN in adults (especially elderly, diabetic, immunocompromised) with ongoing staphylococcal infection is increasingly recognized

### Pediatric PSGN (Classic)

- Age 5-12 years
- 2-3 weeks after GAS pharyngitis or 3-6 weeks after GAS skin infection
- Nephritic syndrome: hematuria (smoky/brown urine), edema, hypertension, oliguria
- ↓ C3 (normalizes within 6-8 weeks)
- ↑ ASO titer (pharyngitis) or anti-DNase B (skin infection)
- Biopsy (usually not needed): diffuse proliferative GN, "starry sky" IF (granular C3, IgG along GBM and mesangium), subepithelial "humps" on EM
- Self-limited in children: >95% recover completely

### Adult IRGN (Increasingly Common)

- Older adults (> 60), diabetic, alcoholic, immunocompromised
- Often during **ongoing infection** (not post-infection) — endocarditis, abscess, osteomyelitis, diabetic foot, pneumonia
- Organism: **Staphylococcus aureus** most common (not Strep)
- **IgA-dominant** IF pattern (distinguishes from classic PSGN which is IgG/C3 dominant)
- ↓ C3 (similar to classic PSGN)
- Poorer prognosis than pediatric PSGN: many develop CKD

**Exam pearl:** Elderly diabetic with AKI + hematuria + low C3 + active staph infection → infection-related GN (NOT IgA nephropathy). IF showing IgA-dominant + C3 with subepithelial humps on EM = IgA-dominant IRGN. Treatment = treat the infection. IgA nephropathy has normal C3 and no subepithelial humps.

### Key Differentiating Features

| Feature | PSGN (children) | IRGN (adults) | IgA nephropathy |
|---|---|---|---|
| Timing | 2-6 weeks POST infection | DURING active infection | Recurrent with infections |
| Organism | GAS (Streptococcus) | Staphylococcus aureus | Any (often URI) |
| C3 | ↓ (normalizes 6-8 wk) | ↓ | **Normal** |
| IF | IgG + C3 dominant | **IgA dominant** + C3 | IgA dominant |
| EM | Subepithelial humps | Subepithelial humps | Mesangial deposits |
| Outcome | Self-limited (>95%) | CKD common | Variable, often chronic |

## Glomerular Diseases Due to Nonamyloid Organized Deposits

### Fibrillary Glomerulonephritis

- Fibrillary deposits in glomeruli composed of **DNAJB9** protein
- Fibrils 16-24 nm (larger than amyloid 8-12 nm; smaller than immunotactoid 30-50 nm)
- **Congo red NEGATIVE** (distinguishes from amyloid)
- DNAJB9 staining = diagnostic biomarker (IHC)
- Clinical: nephrotic syndrome (often) + hematuria; HTN; progressive CKD
- Mean age 50 years; associated with hepatitis C, autoimmune diseases, malignancy (minority)
- Treatment: limited options; rituximab, MMF; often progressive to ESKD
- Recurs in transplant (~50%)

### Immunotactoid Glomerulopathy

- Organized microtubular deposits 30-50+ nm (larger than fibrillary)
- **Congo red NEGATIVE**
- Strongly associated with **lymphoproliferative disorders** (CLL, lymphoma) and monoclonal gammopathy
- Always check for underlying hematologic malignancy
- Clinical: nephrotic syndrome + hematuria
- Treatment: treat underlying malignancy; may respond to chemotherapy
- Distinguished from fibrillary by fibril SIZE on EM and association with monoclonal disease

### Comparison Table

| Feature | Amyloid | Fibrillary GN | Immunotactoid |
|---|---|---|---|
| Fibril size | 8-12 nm | 16-24 nm | 30-50+ nm |
| Congo red | **Positive** (apple-green birefringence) | Negative | Negative |
| IHC marker | SAA (AA) or light chain (AL) | **DNAJB9** | Light chain restriction |
| Associations | Myeloma, chronic inflammation | Hep C, autoimmune | **CLL, lymphoma, MGUS** |
| Organization | Random (nonbranching) | Random | **Parallel arrays** |

**Exam pearl:** EM showing organized glomerular deposits → three possibilities: amyloid (8-12 nm, Congo red+), fibrillary (16-24 nm, DNAJB9+), immunotactoid (30-50 nm, look for lymphoproliferative disease). Size and Congo red status distinguish them.

## Thin Basement Membrane Nephropathy (TBMN)

### Key Facts

- GBM thickness < 250 nm (normal 300-400 nm in adults)
- Autosomal dominant inheritance of heterozygous COL4A3 or COL4A4 mutations
- Prevalence: 5-10% of general population; up to 20-25% of patients with isolated hematuria
- Previously called "benign familial hematuria" — but NOT always benign

### Clinical Features

- Persistent microscopic hematuria (often discovered incidentally)
- Minimal or no proteinuria
- Normal renal function (in most cases)
- Normal BP
- Normal complement levels
- Family history of hematuria

### Relationship to Alport Syndrome

| Feature | TBMN | Alport (X-linked) | Alport (AR) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Genetics | Heterozygous COL4A3/A4 | Hemizygous COL4A5 (males) | Homozygous COL4A3/A4 |
| GBM | Uniformly thin | Thickened, basket-weaving, splitting | Thickened, basket-weaving |
| Hematuria | Persistent microscopic | Persistent + episodic gross | Persistent + gross |
| Proteinuria | Minimal/none | Progressive | Progressive |
| Hearing loss | No | Yes (males) | Yes |
| Ocular | No | Anterior lenticonus, dot-and-fleck retinopathy | Same |
| CKD | Usually no (but 10-15% progress) | ESKD by 20-30s (males) | ESKD (both sexes) |

**Exam pearl:** TBMN = heterozygous carrier of Alport mutation (autosomal). Two carrier parents can have a child with autosomal recessive Alport syndrome. TBMN is NOT always benign — 10-15% develop proteinuria and progressive CKD.

### Diagnosis

- Renal biopsy: uniformly thin GBM on EM (< 250 nm); no splitting/basket-weaving
- Genetic testing: heterozygous COL4A3 or COL4A4 mutation
- **Distinguish from IgA nephropathy** (most common cause of microscopic hematuria in adults): IF is negative in TBMN, positive for mesangial IgA in IgAN

### Management

- Most need no treatment (observe)
- Annual monitoring: BP, sCr, UPCR
- ACEi/ARB if proteinuria develops
- Genetic counseling (risk of Alport in offspring if partner also carries mutation)

## High-Yield Exam Scenarios

1. **HIV+ patient, African American, CD4 80, proteinuria 8 g/day, sCr 5, large echogenic kidneys:** HIVAN (collapsing FSGS). Start ART immediately. ACEi for proteinuria. Check APOL1 genotype.

2. **Diffuse SSc patient on prednisone 20 mg/day, sudden BP 210/120, sCr rising from 1.2 to 4.5, schistocytes:** Scleroderma renal crisis. Start captopril immediately. Do NOT stop even as Cr rises. May need temporary dialysis but recovery possible. Stop prednisone.

3. **Elderly diabetic with foot abscess, AKI, hematuria, low C3, biopsy shows IgA-dominant deposits with subepithelial humps:** Infection-related GN (staphylococcal). NOT IgA nephropathy. Treat the infection. C3 normalizes when infection resolves.

4. **Biopsy showing organized deposits, 20 nm fibrils, Congo red negative, DNAJB9 positive:** Fibrillary GN. Screen for hep C, autoimmune disease. Treat with rituximab/MMF. Poor prognosis, recurs in transplant.

5. **Young patient with persistent microscopic hematuria, normal renal function, family history of hematuria, biopsy GBM 200 nm:** TBMN. Likely heterozygous COL4A3/A4. Mostly benign. Annual monitoring. Genetic counseling re: Alport risk in offspring.
