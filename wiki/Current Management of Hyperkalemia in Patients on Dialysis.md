# Hyperkalemia in Dialysis Patients

## 1. Overview & Epidemiology
*   **Definition:** Serum $\text{K}^+$ $> 5.0 \text{ mmol/L}$.
*   **Risk Profile:** Highest in maintenance **Hemodialysis (HD)**; lower in **Peritoneal Dialysis (PD)** due to continuous clearance and higher prevalence of residual renal function (RRF) + diuretic use.
*   **Clinical Impact:** Increased risk of arrhythmias, sudden cardiac arrest, and CV mortality (1.4-fold increase with hyperkalemia).
*   **PD Paradox:** Higher risk of **hypokalemia** (10–36%) compared to HD.

## 2. Dialysis Kinetics & Physiology
### Hemodialysis (HD)
*   **Removal Mechanism:** 85% Diffusion (gradient-driven), 15% Convection.
*   **Kinetics:** 
    *   Rapid drop ($\sim 1 \text{ mmol/L}$) in 1st hour $\rightarrow$ gradual drop ($\sim 1 \text{ mmol/L}$) over next 2 hours $\rightarrow$ equilibrium in final hour.
    *   **Rebound Phenomenon:** Post-HD rise in serum $\text{K}^+$ due to intracellular-to-extracellular shift. 
    *   **Exam Logic:** High serum-dialysate $\text{K}^+$ gradients (e.g., dialysate $\text{K}^+ < 2 \text{ mmol/L}$) increase the magnitude and speed of post-dialysis rebound.
*   **Capacity:** Typical 3x/week HD removes $210\text{--}300 \text{ mmol/week}$.

### Peritoneal Dialysis (PD)
*   **Removal Mechanism:** Slower, continuous clearance.
*   **Kinetics:** 
    *   Intermittent PD: Rapid initial $\text{K}^+$ drop driven by glucose-induced insulin release (intracellular shift).
    *   CAPD: Lower total daily removal ($\sim 33\text{--}35 \text{ mmol/d}$) compared to intake ($\sim 70\text{--}80 \text{ mmol/d}$), relying heavily on RRF.

## 3. Determinants of Hyperkalemia (Exam High-Yield)
| Category | Factors Increasing Serum $\text{K}^+$ |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Dietary** | High-K fruits/veg (bananas, potatoes, avocado), $\text{K}^+$-containing salt substitutes, certain additives (e.g., potassium citrate in some sodas). |
| **Medications** | **RAASi** (ACEi, ARB, MRA), NSAIDs, $\beta$-blockers, Digoxin, PPIs, Heparin. |
| **Metabolic/Other** | Acidosis, Insulin deficiency, Hemolysis, Rhabdomyolysis, Hyperosmolality (mannitol, contrast). |
| **Dialysis** | Short session duration, high dialysate $\text{K}^+$. |

## 4. Management Strategies
### Standard Approaches
1.  **Dialysis Optimization:** 
    *   $\downarrow$ Dialysate $\text{K}^+$ concentration.
    *   $\uparrow$ Dialysis frequency or session duration.
2.  **Dietary Modification:** Restriction of high-K foods (Note: may impact nutritional status).
3.  **Pharmacological:** Avoidance of $\text{K}^+$-increasing drugs.

### Emerging Non-Dialysis Options
*   **$\text{K}^+$ Binders:** **Patiromer** and **Sodium Zirconium Cyclosilicate (SZC)**.
*   **Clinical Utility:** May reduce the need for restrictive diets and lower the risk of life-threatening hyperkalemic events.

## 5. Key Exam Logic (TSN Focus)
*   **HD vs. PD:** If a question asks why PD patients are less prone to hyperkalemia $\rightarrow$ Answer: **Continuous clearance + RRF**.
*   **Rebound:** If a patient has severe post-HD $\text{K}^+$ rebound $\rightarrow$ Consider: **Excessive gradient** (dialysate $\text{K}^+$ too low) or **inadequate session time** to allow intracellular equilibration.
*   **Symptom Trigger:** Be alert for "hidden" $\text{K}^+$ sources (e.g., salt substitutes) in patients with refractory hyperkalemia despite "dieting."
